\chapter{Entities}
\label{chap:Entities}

\begin{quote}
\begin{flushright}
\emph{We are all agreed that your theory is crazy. \\
The question which divides us is whether it is crazy enough.} \\
Niels Bohr
\end{flushright}
\end{quote}
\bigskip

The first step in quantifying our lack of knowledge is to identify precisely the entities under investigation. In the theory of nescience, this starting point is the definition of a set of entities $\mathcal{E}$ that constitute the subject of study. The elements of this set depend on the particular application of the theory. In some contexts the entities may be mathematical objects, in others biological organisms, technological artifacts, physical phenomena, or even human needs. Although the nature of the entities varies across domains, the procedure used to quantify our lack of knowledge about them remains the same.

An \emph{entity} is anything that can become the object of systematic investigation within a given research domain. In practical terms, it is something about which researchers can formulate questions, collect observations, construct models, or propose explanations. Entities are therefore not limited to physical objects. They may include measurable phenomena, theoretical constructs, mathematical structures, computational processes, or provisional hypotheses introduced to organize observations. What qualifies something as an entity is not its metaphysical status, but its role in scientific work: it must be something that researchers attempt to describe, analyze, predict, or explain.

In this chapter we introduce the notion of a \emph{research entity} and clarify the types of entities considered within the theory of nescience. We examine the main categories of entities that appear in scientific practice and discuss how they are organized into collections that define particular research areas. These concepts provide the foundation for the formal treatment of representations and descriptions developed in the following chapters.

\section{Entities}
\label{sec:descriptions_entities}

The theory of nescience studies the limits of knowledge within a given domain of investigation. Any such analysis requires a precise specification of the objects about which knowledge may be obtained. These objects will be called \emph{entities}\index{Entity}.

\begin{definition}
A \emph{research entity}\index{Research entity} is anything about which scientists attempt to construct representations, collect evidence, formulate models, or derive explanatory or predictive statements.
\end{definition}

According to this definition, a research entity may be an object, phenomenon, structure, or concept that can become the subject of systematic investigation within a given research domain.

We assume that there exists a non-empty set of \emph{entities}\index{Entities} that we seek to understand.

\begin{notation}
The set of research entities under consideration is denoted by $\mathcal{E}$.
\end{notation}

The elements of $\mathcal{E}$ are defined relative to the particular domain of knowledge in which the theory of nescience is applied. Depending on the context, this domain may correspond to a broad discipline such as mathematics, physics, engineering, or the social sciences, or it may be restricted to a specific subarea within one of these fields.

From a formal perspective, $\mathcal{E}$ should be regarded as a fixed but generally inaccessible collection: although its elements are determined by the underlying domain of inquiry, there is in general no effective (computable) procedure for accessing, deciding membership in, or enumerating its elements. 

The set $\mathcal{E}$ contains both \emph{knowable} and \emph{unknowable} entities (see Section~\ref{sec:types_of_entities} bellow for further discussion of the types of entities allowed in the theory of nesciece). 

The possibility of using universal sets is excluded; that is, the existence of a set $\xi$ containing all possible objects cannot be assumed. The problem with universal sets is that their existence is incompatible with Cantor's theorem (see Example \ref{cantor_theorem}). Cantor's theorem proves that the power set $\mathcal{P}(\xi)$, consisting of all possible subsets of $\xi$, has more elements than the original set $\xi$. This contradicts the assumption that $\xi$ includes everything. The set $\mathcal{E}$ must be explicitly fixed and domain-scoped; that is, it must denote the set of entities under consideration in a given research area, rather than an ambiguous or open-ended collection.

\begin{example}[Cantor's theorem]\index{Cantor's theorem}
\label{cantor_theorem}
Cantor's theorem proves that for any set $A$, $d(A) < d\left(\mathcal{P}(A)\right)$\footnote{Here $d(\cdot)$ denotes cardinality, see Appendix \ref{chap:Discrete_Mathematics}.}. Consider $f: A \rightarrow \mathcal{P}(A)$, a function that maps each element $x \in A$ to the set $\{x\} \in \mathcal{P}(A)$; evidently, $f$ is injective, implying $d(A) \leq d\left(\mathcal{P}(A)\right)$. To substantiate that the inequality is strict, let's assume there exists a surjective function $g: A \rightarrow \mathcal{P}(A)$ and consider the set $B = \{ x \in A : x \notin g(x) \}$. As $g$ is surjective, there must exist a $y \in A$ such that $g(y) = B$. This, however, raises a contradiction, $y \in B \Leftrightarrow y \notin g(y) = B$. Consequently, the function $g$ cannot exist, therefore $d(A) < d\left(\mathcal{P}(A)\right)$.
\end{example}

Not all conceivable sets are acceptable, as some may give rise to paradoxes. Take, for example, Russell's paradox, which proposes a set $R$ consisting of all sets that are not members of themselves. The paradox arises when we try to discern whether $R$ is a member of itself (see the Example \ref{ex:russell_paradox}). To avoid such problems, the theory of nescience is based on the Zermelo-Fraenkel set of axioms\index{Zermelo-Fraenkel set of axioms}, along with the Axiom of Choice\index{Axiom of Choice} (or ZFC). The Axiom of Separation\index{Axiom of Separation} (if $P$ is a property with parameter $p$, then for any set $x$ and parameter $p$ there exists a set $y=\{u \in x : P(u) \}$ that includes all those sets $u \in x$ that have property $P$) allows the use of this notation only to construct sets that are subsets of already existing sets. A more extensive Axiom of Comprehension\index{Axiom of Comprehension} (if $P$ is a property, then there exists a set $y=\{u : P(u) \}$) would be required to allow sets like the one proposed by Russell's paradox. Russell's paradox arises from the use of an unrestricted comprehension principle. In the axioms of ZFC, and in the theory of nescience, the axiom of comprehension is considered false.

\begin{example}[Russell's paradox]\index{Russell's paradox}
\label{ex:russell_paradox}
Suppose $R$ is the set of all sets not members of themselves, such that $R = \{ x : x \notin x \}$. The contradiction arises when querying if $R$ is a member of itself. If $R$ is not a member of itself, by its own definition, it should be; conversely, if $R$ is declared to be a member of itself, its definition dictates it should not be. Symbolically, this can be written as $R \in R \Leftrightarrow R \notin R$.
\end{example}

Entities can be grouped into research areas. The concept of an area is useful insofar as all the entities included in the area are related to a common subdomain of knowledge or share a common property. The specific criteria used for grouping depend on the practical application of the theory of nescience.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathcal{E}$ be a set of entities. We define a \emph{research area}\index{Research area} $\mathcal{A}$ as a subset of entities, $\mathcal{A} \subset \mathcal{E}$.
\end{definition}

% Knowable entities
\subsection*{Knowable entities}

We distinguish between knowable and unknowable entities. A \emph{knowable entity}\index{Knowable entity} is one for which, at least in principle, a sufficiently accurate scientific representation can be constructed. This does not mean that the entity is fully understood or perfectly described, but that it is accessible to systematic investigation. Scientists can refine its representations by incorporating new empirical data, correcting incorrect 
assumptions, or removing irrelevant information. Over time, this iterative process reduces error in the encoding of the entity and improves the predictive and explanatory power of the associated models.

Knowable entities can be either concrete or abstract. \emph{Concrete entities}\index{Concrete entity} are physical systems or phenomena. They exist in space and time and can interact with instruments or other physical systems. These interactions allow researchers to design experiments, collect data, and test hypotheses about their properties. Even when the entity itself is not directly accessible, its physical effects can be detected through controlled interventions and measurement protocols.

Concrete entities can be divided into observable and non-observable. \emph{Observable entities}\index{Observable entity} can be directly detected, either by the naked eye or with instruments that extend our senses, such as microscopes, telescopes, sensors, or imaging devices. Observability does not require unaided perception, but operational accessibility: the entity must produce stable signals that can be recorded, quantified, and compared across experiments. This operational criterion is what allows observable entities to serve as reliable anchors for empirical validation and model testing.

\begin{example}
A bacterial colony growing on a Petri dish is a knowable, concrete, observable entity. It can be visually inspected, counted, measured, and experimentally manipulated. Researchers can quantify its growth rate, analyze its morphology, test its response to antibiotics, or sequence its genetic material. Independent laboratories can reproduce the same experimental conditions and compare results, allowing alternative descriptions of the colony's behavior to be evaluated and refined. Because it produces stable and measurable effects (such as changes in size, color, density, or metabolic activity) its properties can be encoded in increasingly precise representations.
\end{example}

Some concrete entities are \emph{non-observable}\index{Non-observable entity}, that is, they cannot be directly observed, yet their existence can be inferred from stable, reproducible experimental effects. In such cases, the entity does not appear itself in measurement data; rather, it is postulated as the best explanation for a consistent pattern of observations. Its properties are reconstructed indirectly through mathematical models, statistical analysis, or controlled interventions.

\begin{example}
An electron is a knowable, concrete, non-observable entity. We do not observe electrons directly, but we detect their presence through cloud chamber tracks, spectral lines, scattering experiments, and electrical measurements. Their charge, mass, and spin are not accessed by direct perception, but are inferred from highly reproducible quantitative effects, such as discrete energy levels in atoms, interference patterns, and precisely measured deflections in electromagnetic fields. Competing theoretical descriptions of electrons are evaluated by comparing predicted outcomes with experimental data, often to many decimal places of accuracy. Although the electron itself is never seen as a localized object in the classical sense, the stability and precision of the phenomena associated with it allow researchers to construct progressively refined representations.
\end{example}

\emph{Abstract entities}\index{Abstract entity} do not have a physical presence. They are defined within formal systems or theoretical frameworks. Although they do not interact physically with instruments, they mediate our understanding of concrete systems by offering compact and manipulable representations. Equations, probability distributions, algorithms, state spaces, and optimization functions allow researchers to formalize assumptions, derive consequences, and compute quantitative forecasts that can later be confronted with empirical data. In many cases, progress in a discipline results from introducing a new abstract entity that reorganizes existing observations under a more coherent structure, reduces descriptive complexity, or increases predictive accuracy. 

\begin{example}
The notion of a Turing machine (see Definition \ref{def:Turing-Machine}) is a knowable, abstract entity. It is not a physical object, but it is precisely defined, can be formally analyzed, and is used to prove theorems about computability and complexity. Its components are specified with exact rules, allowing rigorous reasoning about what can and cannot be computed. Although no physical device perfectly realizes the infinite tape assumed in the definition, the abstraction captures essential structural properties shared by all digital computers. Because its behavior is fully determined by formal rules, competing representations can be unambiguously compared, and proofs about decidability, universality, and incompleteness can be carried out with mathematical precision.
\end{example}

% Unknowable entities
\subsection*{Unknowable entities}

An \emph{unknowable entity}\index{Unknowable entity} is an entity for which no reliable and stable representation can be constructed within any scientific framework. In this sense, unknowability is structural rather than temporary: it does not arise from current technological limitations or lack of data, but from the absence of a possible operational connection between the entity and reproducible procedures. An entity is structurally unknowable when no experiment, measurement protocol, or formal derivation could, even in principle, discriminate between alternative representations or progressively reduce error in its encoding. Under this definition, unknowable entities fall outside the domain of scientific research.

Unknowable entities can be classified as real or fictitious. Some entities are \emph{real}\index{Real entity} in the sense that they belong to physical reality, yet remain structurally beyond our capacity to investigate or represent with scientific precision. Even if the entity exists independently of our knowledge, no reproducible procedure could in principle establish reliable constraints on its properties. As a consequence, scientific representations cannot converge, and competing encodings cannot be meaningfully evaluated. These entities may be logically conceivable and even physically instantiated, but they remain outside the domain of entities that can be stabilized through empirical or formal refinement.

\begin{example}
Consider the exact microphysical state of the universe at a precise instant of time, including the exact position and momentum of every particle with infinite precision. If physical reality is continuous (as assumed in standard classical models), then such a state is a real entity: it corresponds to an actual configuration of the world. However, it is structurally unknowable. No finite physical system can measure, store, or encode an infinite amount of information with arbitrary precision. Moreover, no experimental protocol could discriminate between two candidate representations that differ only beyond any physically realizable resolution. Therefore, although the entity may exist, no reliable and stable scientific representation of it can in principle be constructed. 
\end{example}

\emph{Ficticious entities}\index{Ficticious entity} are introduced hypothetically but do not correspond to any reproducible phenomenon. Such entities may arise as speculative explanatory devices, heuristic constructions, or artifacts of incomplete theories. Unlike real but unknowable entities, fictitious entities do not merely escape representation due to structural limits; rather, they fail to anchor representations to observable effects or formal necessities. They are usually abandoned when alternative frameworks explain the same phenomena without invoking them, or when continued experimental effort fails to produce reproducible consequences associated with their existence.

\begin{example}
The luminiferous ether, once postulated as the medium for light propagation, is an example of an unknowable and fictitious entity. It was introduced in the nineteenth century to account for the apparent need of a mechanical medium through which electromagnetic waves could travel, in analogy with sound waves in air. However, despite increasingly precise experimental efforts to detect the Earth's motion relative to this supposed medium, no consistent or reproducible effect was ever observed. Eventually, the development of special relativity rendered the ether unnecessary by providing a coherent theoretical framework that explained electromagnetic phenomena without invoking any background medium.
\end{example}

\section{Relations Between Entities}
\label{sec:relations_between_entities}

Entities rarely occur in isolation. Scientific investigation typically reveals
relationships among them. These relationships encode structure within the
domain of inquiry and often play a central role in the organization of
scientific knowledge. In many fields, understanding the relations between
entities is as important as identifying the entities themselves.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathcal{E}$ be a set of entities. A \emph{relation between entities}\index{Relation between entities}
is any subset
\[
\mathcal{L} \subseteq \mathcal{E} \times \mathcal{E}.
\]
If $(e_1,e_2) \in \mathcal{L}$ we say that $e_1$ is \emph{related} to $e_2$ under $\mathcal{L}$.
\end{definition}

Relations can represent many types of scientific connections. For example, $\mathcal{L}$ may encode causal influence between physical systems, biochemical interactions between molecules, logical implication between mathematical statements, or functional dependence between variables in a model.

Relations between entities may exhibit several structural properties that are
frequently encountered in scientific practice.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathcal{L} \subseteq \mathcal{E} \times \mathcal{E}$ be a relation.
\begin{itemize}
\item $\mathcal{L}$ is \emph{reflexive} if $(e,e) \in \mathcal{L}$ for every $e \in \mathcal{E}$.
\item $\mathcal{L}$ is \emph{symmetric} if $(e_1,e_2) \in \mathcal{L}$ implies $(e_2,e_1) \in \mathcal{L}$.
\item $\mathcal{L}$ is \emph{antisymmetric} if $(e_1,e_2) \in \mathcal{L}$ and $(e_2,e_1) \in \mathcal{L}$ imply $e_1 = e_2$.
\item $\mathcal{L}$ is \emph{transitive} if $(e_1,e_2) \in \mathcal{L}$ and $(e_2,e_3) \in \mathcal{L}$ imply $(e_1,e_3) \in \mathcal{L}$.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

These properties appear naturally in different types of scientific relations.

Relations arising in scientific contexts often exhibit well-known structural properties. For instance, logical implication between mathematical propositions is a transitive relation. If a statement $P$ implies a statement $Q$, and $Q$ implies another statement $R$, then $P$ necessarily implies $R$. Symbolically, if $(P,Q)\in\mathcal{L}$ and $(Q,R)\in\mathcal{L}$, then $(P,R)\in\mathcal{L}$. This transitivity allows chains of reasoning in which
the consequences of known results propagate through the network of mathematical statements.

Similarity relations provide another common example. In many scientific domains, two entities may be considered similar if they share relevant structural or functional properties. Such relations are typically symmetric: if an entity $e_1$ is similar to an entity $e_2$, then $e_2$ is also similar to $e_1$. For example, two chemical compounds may be regarded as similar if their molecular structures share common functional groups, or two biological species may be considered similar if they exhibit comparable phenotypic traits.

Finally, hierarchical containment relations frequently arise in systems that exhibit layered organization. In such cases the relation is often antisymmetric. If an entity $e_1$ is contained within $e_2$, and $e_2$ is contained within $e_1$, then the two entities must in fact coincide. This property appears, for example, in biological taxonomies, where species are contained within genera and genera within families, or in engineering systems, where components belong to subsystems and subsystems belong to larger assemblies. These structural properties help reveal how entities are organized within the domain under investigation.

A particularly important class of relations arises when entities can be grouped into classes that share a common property.

\begin{definition}
A relation $\mathcal{L}$ on $\mathcal{E}$ is an \emph{equivalence relation} if it is reflexive, symmetric, and transitive.
\end{definition}

Equivalence relations partition the set $\mathcal{L}$ into disjoint subsets called \emph{equivalence classes}. Each class contains entities that are indistinguishable with respect to the property encoded by $\mathcal{L}$.

In practice, equivalence relations often arise when different descriptions or models refer to the same underlying entity, or when objects share a common structural property. For example, in chemistry two molecular graphs may be considered equivalent if they correspond to the same compound, while in mathematics two objects may be equivalent up to isomorphism.

Another important class of relations is given by partial orders.

A relation $\mathcal{L}$ on $\mathcal{E}$ is a \emph{partial order} if it is reflexive, antisymmetric, and transitive.

When $\mathcal{L}$ is a partial order, the pair $(\mathcal{E},\mathcal{R})$ is called a \emph{partially ordered set} or \emph{poset}.

Order relations arise naturally in scientific contexts where entities exhibit hierarchical organization. Examples include containment relations between physical subsystems, taxonomic classifications in biology, or logical
implication structures between mathematical statements.

Relations between entities can often be conveniently visualized using graphs.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathcal{L} \subseteq \mathcal{E} \times \mathcal{E}$. The pair
\[
G = (\mathcal{E},\mathcal{L})
\]
can be interpreted as a directed graph whose vertices are the entities in $\mathcal{E}$ and whose edges correspond to pairs $(e_1,e_2) \in \mathcal{L}$.
\end{definition}

This representation allows the use of graph-theoretic tools to analyze the structure of relations between entities. Concepts such as connectivity, cycles, and paths can reveal patterns of interaction or dependency within the domain.

Relations between entities frequently encode important scientific structure. For instance:

\begin{itemize}
\item In physics, relations may represent causal influence or interaction between physical systems.
\item In biology, relations often describe regulatory networks or metabolic pathways.
\item In mathematics, relations capture logical implication, equivalence, or structural similarity.
\item In engineering and computer science, relations frequently represent dependency graphs between components or modules.
\end{itemize}

Understanding these relations often reveals higher-level organization within the domain of entities. In many cases, the network formed by the relation $\mathcal{L}$ provides a map of how knowledge about different entities is
structured within a scientific field.

\section{Research Areas}

Entities can be grouped into research areas. The concept of an area is useful in practice insofar as all the entities included in the area are related to a common subdomain of knowledge or share a common property. The specific criteria used for grouping depend on the practical application of the theory of nescience.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathcal{E}$ be a set of entities. We define a \emph{research area}\index{Research area} $\mathcal{A}$ as a subset of entities, $\mathcal{A} \subset \mathcal{E}$.
\end{definition}

The notion of a research area introduced above can be enriched with additional structure. In scientific practice, areas are not merely arbitrary collections of entities. They typically admit natural operations of combination, restriction, and overlap. For example, two areas may intersect in a common subdomain, several areas may be combined into a broader field, and every specialized subarea of a legitimate research area is itself a legitimate area of inquiry. These simple observations suggest that the family of admissible research areas may be organized in a way that resembles the open sets of a topological space.

This motivates the introduction of a topology on the set of entities $\mathcal{E}$. The purpose of this construction is not geometric in the usual sense, but structural: it provides a compact mathematical language to describe how research areas relate to one another through inclusion, specialization, overlap, and aggregation.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathcal{E}$ be a set of entities. A \emph{research topology}\index{Research topology} on $\mathcal{E}$ is a family $\mathcal{T} \subseteq \mathcal{P}(\mathcal{E})$ such that:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $\varnothing \in \mathcal{T}$ and $\mathcal{E} \in \mathcal{T}$,
    \item if $\mathcal{A}_1, \mathcal{A}_2 \in \mathcal{T}$, then $\mathcal{A}_1 \cap \mathcal{A}_2 \in \mathcal{T}$,
    \item if $\{\mathcal{A}_i\}_{i \in I}$ is any family of elements of $\mathcal{T}$, then $\bigcup_{i \in I} \mathcal{A}_i \in \mathcal{T}$.
\end{enumerate}
The pair $\mathcal{E},\mathcal{T})$ is called a \emph{research space}\index{Research space}. The elements of $\mathcal{T}$ are called \emph{open research areas}\index{Open research area}.
\end{definition}

The axioms have a natural interpretation. The empty set represents the absence of entities under consideration, while $\mathcal{E}$ represents the full domain of inquiry. Closure under finite intersections expresses the fact that the overlap of two valid research areas is itself a valid area. Closure under arbitrary unions captures the possibility of forming broader areas by combining several related subareas. Thus, a research topology formalizes the intuitive idea that scientific domains are closed under specialization and aggregation.

\begin{example}
Let $\mathcal{E}$ be the set of entities studied in the life sciences. A possible research topology may contain areas such as molecular biology, genetics, microbiology, and their intersections. The intersection of genetics and microbiology may correspond to microbial genetics, while the union of several such areas may define a broader field such as experimental biology.
\end{example}

Once a topology is fixed, several standard notions acquire a meaningful scientific interpretation. If $e \in \mathcal{E}$, an open research area $\mathcal{A} \in \mathcal{T}$ with $e \in \mathcal{A}$ can be interpreted as
a \emph{context of investigation} for \(e\): a domain in which the entity is studied together with related entities. The family of all open areas containing $e$ describes the different scientific contexts in which that entity appears. This allows one to formalize the idea that an entity may belong simultaneously
to several overlapping areas of research.

\begin{definition}
Let $(\mathcal{E},\mathcal{T})$ be a research space and let $e \in \mathcal{E}$. A \emph{neighborhood}\index{Neighborhood} of $e$ is any research area $\mathcal{A} \in \mathcal{T}$ such that $e \in \mathcal{A}$.
\end{definition}

Neighborhoods represent local regions of inquiry around an entity. In this sense, the topology induces a notion of scientific locality: entities are close to one another, not by metric distance, but by belonging to common areas of investigation. Two entities that repeatedly occur in the same open areas may be regarded as strongly related from the standpoint of scientific organization.

The topological viewpoint is intentionally abstract. It does not specify how the admissible areas are chosen; this depends on the application domain. In some cases, the topology may arise from an existing disciplinary classification. In others, it may be induced by empirical similarity, conceptual dependence, methodological proximity, or common representation schemes. The value of the framework lies in providing a unified mathematical structure within which research areas can be studied systematically.

In particular, the theory of nescience benefits from this organization because it allows ignorance to be analyzed not only at the level of individual entities, but also at the level of neighborhoods, subareas, and unions of areas. This makes it possible to study how lack of knowledge propagates across related entities, how local gaps combine into broader regions of ignorance, and how the refinement of one area may influence adjacent areas within the same research space.

\section{Research Areas and Relations}
\label{sec:relations_induce_areas}

Relations between entities often reveal structural organization within the domain of inquiry. In many scientific contexts, groups of entities are studied together because they participate in a common network of relations. This observation suggests a natural connection between relations and the formation of research areas.

A simple way to obtain a collection of entities from a relation is to consider the entities directly related to a given one.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathcal{L} \subseteq \mathcal{E} \times \mathcal{E}$ be a relation and let $e\in\mathcal{E}$. The \emph{$\mathcal{L}$-neighborhood}\index{Neighborhood} of $e$ is the set
\[
N_{\mathcal{L}}(e) = \{x\in\mathcal{E} : (e,x)\in\mathcal{L}\}.
\]
\end{definition}

The neighborhood $N_{\mathcal{L}}(e)$ contains all entities that interact directly with $e$ according to the relation $\mathcal{R}$. In practice, such neighborhoods frequently correspond to local domains of investigation. For example, if $\mathcal{L}$ represents biochemical interaction, the neighborhood of a molecule may consist of all molecules that participate in the same reaction network.

In many cases, a research area should contain not only a particular entity but also the entities structurally connected to it.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathcal{A}\subseteq\mathcal{E}$ and let $\mathcal{L}\subseteq\mathcal{E}\times\mathcal{E}$ be a relation.
The set $\mathcal{A}$ is said to be \emph{$\mathcal{L}$-closed}\index{Closed set} if
\[
(e_1,e_2)\in\mathcal{L} \quad\text{and}\quad e_1\in\mathcal{A} \quad \Rightarrow \quad e_2\in\mathcal{A}.
\]
\end{definition}

Intuitively, an $\mathcal{L}$-closed set contains every entity that can be reached from its elements through the relation $\mathcal{R}$. Such sets are natural candidates for research areas because they contain all entities that are structurally linked by the relation under consideration.

Relations can generate areas by expanding a set of entities until it becomes closed under the relation.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathcal{S}\subseteq\mathcal{E}$. The \emph{$\mathcal{L}$-closure} of $\mathcal{S}$ is the smallest $\mathcal{L}$-closed set containing $\mathcal{S}$. It is denoted by
\[
\operatorname{Cl}_{\mathcal{L}}(\mathcal{S}).
\]
\end{definition}

The closure operation enlarges a set of entities by incorporating all those that are structurally related to it. In scientific practice this operation corresponds to expanding a field of study as new dependencies or interactions
are discovered.

When the relation $\mathcal{L}$ is an equivalence relation, it partitions the set of entities into disjoint subsets that can naturally be interpreted as research areas.

\begin{proposition}
Let $\mathcal{L}$ be an equivalence relation on $\mathcal{E}$. Then the equivalence classes induced by $\mathcal{L}$ form a partition of $\mathcal{E}$.
\end{proposition}

Each equivalence class groups together entities that are indistinguishable with respect to the property encoded by $\mathcal{L}$. For example, in mathematics objects may be grouped by isomorphism, while in chemistry molecular structures may be grouped by chemical identity.

Relations may also induce hierarchical organization within the set of entities.

\begin{definition}
A relation $\mathcal{L}$ on $\mathcal{E}$ is a \emph{partial order} if it is reflexive, antisymmetric, and transitive.
\end{definition}

When $\mathcal{L}$ is a partial order, the pair $(\mathcal{E},\mathcal{L})$ forms a partially ordered set. Such relations arise naturally when entities are organized by containment, specialization, or dependency. For example, biological taxonomies, subsystem relations in physics, and abstraction hierarchies in computer science can all be modeled in this way.

In these contexts, research areas often correspond to downward-closed or upward-closed subsets of the poset, representing collections of entities that share a common level of generality.

In the most general case, the relation $\mathcal{L}$ defines a graph
\[
G=(\mathcal{E},\mathcal{R})
\]
whose vertices correspond to entities and whose edges correspond to relations between them.

Graph-theoretic structures often reveal meaningful scientific organization. Clusters or highly connected regions of the graph frequently correspond to coherent research areas. For instance:

\begin{itemize}
\item in biology, interaction networks may reveal functional modules;
\item in technology, dependency graphs identify subsystems;
\item in mathematics, chains of logical implications reveal thematic connections between problems and results.
\end{itemize}

From this perspective, research areas can be interpreted as structurally coherent regions of the relational network formed by entities. Studying the relations between entities therefore provides a systematic way to identify, organize, and expand the domains in which scientific knowledge develops.

\section{Knowledge Structure}
\label{sec:knowledge_structure_relations}

Scientific knowledge does not consist merely of isolated entities.  Entities are typically connected through relations that encode causal interactions, structural dependencies, logical implications, or other forms of scientific linkage. These relations induce a natural structure over the domain of entities and allow us to formalize the notions of  knowledge frontier and unknown unknowns.


\begin{definition}
Let $\mathcal{E}$ be a set of entities. A \emph{knowledge state}\index{Knowledge state} is a subset
\[
\mathcal{K} \subseteq \mathcal{E}
\]
containing the entities that have been identified within a given domain of inquiry.
\end{definition}

The complement
\[
\mathcal{U} = \mathcal{E} \setminus \mathcal{K}
\]
represents the set of entities that remain unknown.

Suppose that entities are connected through a relation
\[
\mathcal{R} \subseteq \mathcal{E} \times \mathcal{E}.
\]

The relation $\mathcal{R}$ may represent any form of scientific connection, such as causal influence, interaction, structural dependence, or logical implication.

Relations induce paths that connect entities.

\begin{definition}
Let $e_1,e_2 \in \mathcal{E}$. We say that $e_2$ is \emph{reachable}\index{Reachable entity} from $e_1$, and write
\[
e_1 \leadsto e_2,
\]
if there exists a finite sequence of entities
\[
e_1 = x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_n = e_2
\]
such that
\[
(x_i,x_{i+1}) \in \mathcal{R}
\quad
\text{for all } i \in \{0,\ldots,n-1\}.
\]
\end{definition}

Reachability captures the idea that one entity may be connected to  another through a chain of scientific relationships.

The structure induced by $\mathcal{R}$ allows us to define the boundary between known and unknown entities.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathcal{K}$ be a knowledge state. The \emph{knowledge frontier}\index{Knowledge frontier} associated with $\mathcal{K}$ is the set
\[
\partial \mathcal{K} =
\{ e \in \mathcal{K} :
\exists u \in \mathcal{E}\setminus\mathcal{K}
\text{ such that }
(e,u)\in\mathcal{R}
\}.
\]
\end{definition}

Entities in the frontier are known entities that are directly connected to unknown ones. They therefore represent points where new discoveries are most likely to emerge.

Not all unknown entities lie near the frontier.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathcal{K}$ be a knowledge state. An entity
\[
u \in \mathcal{E}\setminus\mathcal{K}
\]
is called an \emph{unknown unknown}\index{Unknown unknown} if it is not reachable from any known entity, that is, if
\[
\forall k \in \mathcal{K},\quad
k \not\leadsto u .
\]
\end{definition}

Such entities lie outside the structural region currently accessible through known relations. Their existence cannot be inferred from the current knowledge structure.

The set of entities that can be reached from current knowledge plays an important role.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathcal{K}\subseteq\mathcal{E}$ be a knowledge state. The \emph{reachable region}\index{Reachable region} generated by $\mathcal{K}$ is
\[
\operatorname{Reach}(\mathcal{K})
=
\{e\in\mathcal{E} : \exists k\in\mathcal{K}\text{ such that }k\leadsto e\}.
\]
\end{definition}

The reachable region contains all entities that are structurally accessible from current knowledge by following chains of known relations. By definition, every unknown unknown lies outside this region.

\begin{proposition}
For every knowledge state $\mathcal{K}\subseteq\mathcal{E}$,
\[
\mathcal{K}\subseteq \operatorname{Reach}(\mathcal{K}).
\]
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Let $k\in\mathcal{K}$. Then $k$ is reachable from itself by the trivial path of length zero. Hence $k\in\operatorname{Reach}(\mathcal{K})$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}
An entity $u\in\mathcal{E}\setminus\mathcal{K}$ is an unknown unknown if and only if
\[
u\notin \operatorname{Reach}(\mathcal{K}).
\]
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
By definition, $u$ is an unknown unknown precisely when there does not exist any $k\in\mathcal{K}$ such that $k\leadsto u$. This is equivalent to saying that $u$ does not belong to $\operatorname{Reach}(\mathcal{K})$.
\end{proof}

The previous definitions make it possible to state a simple sufficient condition for the existence of unknown unknowns.

\begin{theorem}
\label{thm:existence_unknown_unknowns}
Let $\mathcal{E}$ be a set of entities, let
\[
\mathcal{R}\subseteq \mathcal{E}\times\mathcal{E}
\]
be a relation, and let $\mathcal{K}\subseteq\mathcal{E}$ be a non-empty knowledge state. If
\[
\operatorname{Reach}(\mathcal{K}) \neq \mathcal{E},
\]
then there exists at least one unknown unknown.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
Assume that
\[
\operatorname{Reach}(\mathcal{K}) \neq \mathcal{E}.
\]
Then there exists an entity
\[
u\in \mathcal{E}\setminus \operatorname{Reach}(\mathcal{K}).
\]
Since $\mathcal{K}\subseteq \operatorname{Reach}(\mathcal{K})$, it follows that $u\notin\mathcal{K}$. Therefore
\[
u\in \mathcal{E}\setminus\mathcal{K}.
\]
Moreover, because $u\notin \operatorname{Reach}(\mathcal{K})$, there is no $k\in\mathcal{K}$ such that $k\leadsto u$. Hence $u$ is an unknown unknown.
\end{proof}

The theorem shows that unknown unknowns arise whenever the current knowledge state fails to reach the whole domain through the available relational structure. In other words, even if some entities are known, the graph induced by the relation may still contain regions that remain completely inaccessible from present knowledge.

The previous theorem can be specialized to a simple graph-theoretic condition.

\begin{corollary}
\label{cor:disconnected_unknown_unknowns}
Let $\mathcal{K}\subseteq\mathcal{E}$ be a non-empty knowledge state. If there exists an entity
\[
u\in \mathcal{E}\setminus\mathcal{K}
\]
lying in a connected component of the graph $(\mathcal{E},\mathcal{R})$ that contains no element of $\mathcal{K}$, then $u$ is an unknown unknown.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
If the connected component of $u$ contains no known entity, then there exists no path from any $k\in\mathcal{K}$ to $u$. Hence $u\notin \operatorname{Reach}(\mathcal{K})$, and by the previous proposition $u$ is an unknown unknown.
\end{proof}

The relation $\mathcal{R}$ induces a graph
\[
G = (\mathcal{E},\mathcal{R})
\]
whose vertices represent entities and whose edges represent relations  between them. In this graph:

\begin{itemize}
\item the known entities form a subgraph induced by $\mathcal{K}$,
\item the frontier corresponds to nodes adjacent to unknown regions,
\item the reachable region contains the entities that can in principle be approached from current knowledge by following relational paths,
\item unknown unknowns correspond to nodes that lie outside this reachable region.
\end{itemize}

This perspective provides a structural explanation of how scientific knowledge expands. Discovery typically proceeds along the frontier, where known entities reveal direct links to previously unknown ones. However, the theorem above shows that some unknown entities may remain entirely detached from the current knowledge state. Such entities cannot be reached by local extensions of existing knowledge alone; they require the introduction of new relations, new methods, or new entry points into the domain.

In this sense, unknown unknowns are not merely entities that are currently unknown. They are entities that remain invisible from the standpoint of the present relational organization of knowledge.
