In Chapter \ref{chap:Representations} we addressed the problem of how to encode a possibly abstract entity as a finite string of symbols that preserves enough information to make the entity available for study. Such a string was called a \emph{representation}. A representation, however, is not yet enough for science. Even when it contains relevant information about the entity, it may still be too large, too irregular, or too unstructured to support explanation, prediction, or understanding. The next task is therefore to construct a description that generates, explains, or approximates that representation. Descriptions make it possible not merely to store or inspect a representation, but to do science with it: they capture regularities, compress what can be made systematic, and provide an effective basis for reasoning and prediction.

In the theory of nescience, descriptions are treated as computable objects: finite strings that encode procedures capable of producing an output representation. This requirement is essential. It excludes purely informal, ambiguous, or self-referential descriptions that may be suggestive from a philosophical point of view but cannot be executed or checked mechanically. By grounding descriptions in computability, the theory places scientific explanation within a framework in which descriptions can be compared, transformed, and analyzed with mathematical precision.

Not every computable string qualifies equally as a scientific description. A \emph{valid description} is one that encodes a computation that produces a representation. Among the valid descriptions of a given representation, some may be shorter than others. This leads to the notion of a \emph{minimal description}, namely, a description of shortest possible length for that representation. Minimal descriptions play a central theoretical role, since they capture the smallest amount of information needed to reconstruct the representation. In practice, however, such optimal descriptions are generally unknown and cannot be identified effectively. For this reason, scientific work proceeds instead with \emph{effective descriptions}: descriptions that are actually available and used in a given scientific context, even though they need not be minimal and may even fail to generate the intended representation exactly.

Descriptions may also be restricted to particular families, reflecting the fact that in real scientific work we rarely search over the entire space of conceivable descriptions. Instead, we usually work within structured classes such as linear models, systems of differential equations, decision trees, or neural networks. In this setting, a description may be understood as consisting of two parts: a structural component, which captures the regularities present in the representation, and an input component, which provides the remaining information needed for reconstruction. When the structural component is restricted to a given family, the resulting descriptions correspond to what scientists ordinarily call \emph{models}. The chapter also studies basic operations on descriptions, such as augmentation and reduction, which formalize the ways in which descriptions may be refined, enriched, or simplified.

Whereas the chapter on representations focused on how entities are encoded, the present chapter focuses on how those encodings are modeled. It is at this point that the theory of nescience moves from the problem of representing entities to the problem of explaining their representations through valid, minimal, and effective descriptions, and through the model classes used in scientific practice.

\section{Valid Descriptions}
\label{sec:descriptions_models}

A description is intended to capture a representation in a form that can support explanation, prediction, and further reasoning. By replacing an unstructured string with a computable account of how that string is produced, descriptions make it possible to study representations as scientific objects rather than merely to record them.

Intuitively, a description consists of two parts: a Turing machine, which encodes the regularities present in the representation, and an input string, which supplies the remaining information needed to produce it. In this way, the Turing machine captures the compressible structure, while the input string accounts for what is left unspecified.

\begin{definition}
\label{def:description_model}
Let $d \in \mathcal{B}^\ast$ be a binary string of the form $d = \langle TM, a \rangle$, where $TM$ is the encoding of a prefix-free Turing machine and $a \in \mathcal{B}^\ast$ is an input string. If $TM(a)\downarrow$, then we say that $d$ is a \emph{valid description}\index{Valid description}.
\end{definition}

From an ontological point of view, descriptions are finite strings that satisfy an additional requirement: they must encode a computation that halts. Thus, descriptions are not arbitrary symbolic objects, but effective procedures for producing strings.

This computational requirement has important advantages. First, it excludes purely informal, ambiguous, or metaphorical descriptions that may suggest an interpretation but do not determine any definite procedure. Second, it makes descriptions mechanically checkable: in principle, one can execute the encoded computation and verify the resulting output. Third, it allows descriptions to be compared in a mathematically precise way, for example by their length, their structure, or the transformations that relate one description to another. In this way, computable descriptions provide a rigorous foundation for scientific modeling, since they turn the problem of explanation into the study of explicit procedures rather than of arbitrary symbolic expressions.

The following definition specifies the set of all valid descriptions, that is, the ser of finite binary strings that encode a computable procedure together with an input on which it halts.

\begin{definition}
\label{def:set_of_descriptions}
We define the \emph{set of descriptions}\index{Set of descriptions}, denoted by $\mathcal{D}$, as
\[
\mathcal{D} = \left\{ d \in \mathcal{B}^\ast : d = \langle TM,a\rangle \ \wedge\ TM(a)\downarrow \right\}.
\]
\end{definition}

Only strings belonging to $\mathcal{D}$ will be considered descriptions in the theory of nescience.

Once the global set of descriptions has been fixed, we can restrict attention to those descriptions that generate a given representation. This allows us to associate descriptions directly with individual representations.

\begin{definition}
\label{def:descriptions_for_representation}
Let $r \in \mathcal{R}$ be a representation. We define the \emph{set of descriptions for $r$}\index{Set of descriptions for a representation}, denoted by $\mathcal{D}_r$, as
\[
\mathcal{D}_r = \left\{ d=\langle TM,a\rangle \in \mathcal{D} : TM(a)=r \right\}.
\]
\end{definition}

The set $\mathcal{D}_r$ is important because it contains all the exact descriptions of the representation $r$. It therefore characterizes the full descriptive variability associated with $r$: different elements of $\mathcal{D}_r$ may encode the same representation by means of different procedures and with different amounts of information. This makes $\mathcal{D}_r$ the natural reference set for studying optimality and excess in description, since any shortest or most economical description of $r$ must belong to $\mathcal{D}_r$.

Since a description produces a unique output whenever the computation halts, the sets of descriptions associated with distinct representations are disjoint.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:disjoint_descriptions_of_representations}
Let $r_1,r_2 \in \mathcal{R}$ with $r_1 \neq r_2$. Then
\[
\mathcal{D}_{r_1}\cap \mathcal{D}_{r_2}=\varnothing.
\]
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Suppose, for contradiction, that there exists a description
\[
d=\langle TM,a\rangle \in \mathcal{D}_{r_1}\cap \mathcal{D}_{r_2}.
\]
By definition of $\mathcal{D}_{r_1}$, we have $TM(a)=r_1$, and by definition of $\mathcal{D}_{r_2}$, we also have $TM(a)=r_2$. Since $TM$ is deterministic, the computation on input $a$ has a unique output. Hence $r_1=r_2$, contradicting the assumption that $r_1\neq r_2$.
\end{proof}

This proposition shows that the sets \(\mathcal{D}_r\) are pairwise disjoint. Therefore, any valid description whose output is a representation in \(\mathcal{R}\) is associated with exactly one representation. This makes it possible to classify descriptions according to their output and to study each representation through its own set of descriptions, independently of the others.

It is important to note, however, that not every description in \(\mathcal{D}\) must necessarily generate a string belonging to \(\mathcal{R}\). A valid description is only required to encode a halting computation, and its output may be an arbitrary binary string. Thus, the sets \(\mathcal{D}_r\) do not partition the whole set \(\mathcal{D}\), but only the subset of descriptions whose outputs are representations in \(\mathcal{R}\).

We can generalize and consider all descriptions that generate some representation of a given entity. In this way, the notion of description is lifted from individual representations to the entities they represent.

\begin{definition}
\label{def:descriptions_for_entity}
Let $e \in \mathcal{E}$ be an entity. We define the \emph{set of valid descriptions for $e$}\index{Set of valid descriptions}, denoted by $\mathcal{D}_e$, as
\[
\mathcal{D}_e = \left\{ d=\langle TM,a\rangle \in \mathcal{D} : \exists r \in \mathcal{R}_e,\; TM(a)=r \right\}.
\]
\end{definition}

The set $\mathcal{D}_e$ collects all valid descriptions that generate some representation of the entity $e$. Unlike the sets $\mathcal{D}_r$, which are tied to a single representation, the set $\mathcal{D}_e$ brings together all the different ways in which the same entity may be described through possibly different representations. In this sense, $\mathcal{D}_e$ characterizes the descriptive variability of the entity itself, rather than of any one particular encoding.

By definition, $\mathcal{D}_e$ is the union of the sets $\mathcal{D}_r$ over all representations $r \in \mathcal{R}_e$. It therefore provides the natural domain for studying descriptions at the level of the entity, especially in situations where the entity admits multiple representations and, correspondingly, multiple families of descriptions.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:descriptions_entity_union}
For every entity $e \in \mathcal{E}$,
\[
\mathcal{D}_e=\bigcup_{r\in \mathcal{R}_e}\mathcal{D}_r.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
We prove both inclusions. First, let $d \in \mathcal{D}_e$. By Definition \ref{def:descriptions_for_entity}, there exists some $r \in \mathcal{R}_e$ such that $TM(a)=r$. By Definition \ref{def:descriptions_for_representation}, this implies that $d \in \mathcal{D}_r$. Hence
\[
d \in \bigcup_{r\in \mathcal{R}_e}\mathcal{D}_r.
\]
Therefore,
\[
\mathcal{D}_e \subseteq \bigcup_{r\in \mathcal{R}_e}\mathcal{D}_r.
\]

Conversely, let
\[
d \in \bigcup_{r\in \mathcal{R}_e}\mathcal{D}_r.
\]
Then there exists some $r \in \mathcal{R}_e$ such that $d \in \mathcal{D}_r$. By Definition \ref{def:descriptions_for_representation}, this means that $TM(a)=r$. Since $r \in \mathcal{R}_e$, Definition \ref{def:descriptions_for_entity} implies that $d \in \mathcal{D}_e$. Thus,
\[
\bigcup_{r\in \mathcal{R}_e}\mathcal{D}_r \subseteq \mathcal{D}_e.
\]

Combining both inclusions gives
\[
\mathcal{D}_e=\bigcup_{r\in \mathcal{R}_e}\mathcal{D}_r.
\]
\end{proof}

The next proposition makes explicit the relation between representation and description at the level of entities. Descriptions are defined only through the representations they generate, so the existence of valid descriptions for an entity is equivalent to the existence of representations of that entity. In this sense, the descriptive layer of the theory does not extend beyond the representational one, but presupposes it.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:descriptions_entity_empty_iff_repr_empty}
For every entity $e \in \mathcal{E}$,
\[
\mathcal{D}_e = \varnothing \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \mathcal{R}_e = \varnothing.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
First, assume that $\mathcal{R}_e=\varnothing$. Then, by Proposition \ref{prop:descriptions_entity_union},
\[
\mathcal{D}_e=\bigcup_{r\in \mathcal{R}_e}\mathcal{D}_r=\bigcup_{r\in \varnothing}\mathcal{D}_r=\varnothing.
\]

Conversely, assume that $\mathcal{D}_e=\varnothing$. Suppose, for contradiction, that $\mathcal{R}_e\neq\varnothing$. Then there exists some $r\in \mathcal{R}_e$. Since $r$ is a finite binary string, there exists a prefix-free Turing machine that ignores its input and prints $r$, then halts. Let $d=\langle TM,a\rangle$ be the encoding of such a machine together with any admissible input $a$. Then $d\in\mathcal{D}$ and $TM(a)=r$, so $d\in\mathcal{D}_r\subseteq\mathcal{D}_e$, contradicting $\mathcal{D}_e=\varnothing$. Therefore, $\mathcal{R}_e=\varnothing$.
\end{proof}

This proposition links the existence of descriptions to the representability of entities. In particular, if an entity is unknowable, in the sense that it admits no representations, then it also admits no valid descriptions. Conversely, whenever an entity is representable, it also becomes describable, since any of its representations may be produced by at least one valid description. Thus, at the level of entities, describability and representability coincide: the boundary between describable and undescribable entities is the same as the boundary between knowable and unknowable entities.

In some cases, a description may also qualify as a representation, namely when it is accepted by the oracle as encoding an entity. In this sense, there may exist descriptions that describe other descriptions. In practice, however, it is not advisable to use descriptions as representations of entities. A useful representation should preserve as much relevant detail about the original entity as possible, whereas a description is designed to provide a concise computational account of a representation. Replacing representations with descriptions would therefore make the task of scientific discovery considerably harder.

The set of descriptions associated with an entity is not arbitrary from a computability-theoretic point of view. Under a mild effectiveness assumption on the set of representations of the entity, the corresponding set of descriptions can also be effectively enumerated.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:De_recursively_enumerable}
Let \(e \in \mathcal{E}\). If the set \(\mathcal{R}_e\) is recursively enumerable, then the set \(\mathcal{D}_e\) is recursively enumerable.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
By Proposition \ref{prop:descriptions_entity_union},
\[
\mathcal{D}_e=\bigcup_{r\in \mathcal{R}_e}\mathcal{D}_r.
\]

Fix \(r\in\mathcal{R}_e\). The set \(\mathcal{D}_r\) is recursively enumerable: one can enumerate all binary strings \(d\), interpret each of them as a candidate pair \(\langle TM,a\rangle\), simulate \(TM(a)\) by dovetailing, and output \(d\) whenever the computation halts with value \(r\).

Now assume that \(\mathcal{R}_e\) is recursively enumerable. Then one can enumerate its elements
\[
r_1,r_2,r_3,\ldots
\]
and, for each \(r_i\), run in parallel the corresponding enumeration of \(\mathcal{D}_{r_i}\). By dovetailing these enumerations, every description belonging to some \(\mathcal{D}_{r_i}\) will eventually be produced. Therefore, one obtains an effective enumeration of
\[
\bigcup_{r\in\mathcal{R}_e}\mathcal{D}_r = \mathcal{D}_e.
\]
Hence \(\mathcal{D}_e\) is recursively enumerable.
\end{proof}

This proposition has an important practical implication. Whenever the set \(\mathcal{R}_e\) can be effectively enumerated, the corresponding descriptions of the entity can also be searched effectively, at least in principle. That is, one may systematically generate candidate descriptions of the entity and test their properties. This does not solve the problem of finding good or minimal descriptions, but it shows that the search space is not completely inaccessible from a computability-theoretic point of view.

A final and particularly important point is that, since each valid description determines exactly one output string, it is natural to define a function that maps descriptions to the representations they produce. Because descriptions encode Turing machines together with their inputs, this mapping can be implemented by a universal Turing machine. Thus, not only are individual descriptions computable objects, but the operation that sends a description to its output is computable as well.

\begin{definition}
\label{def:description_function}
We call a \emph{description function}\index{Description function}, denoted by $\Gamma$, a universal Turing machine
\[
\Gamma : \mathcal{D}\to \mathcal{B}^\ast
\]
that halts on all inputs in $\mathcal{D}$ and maps each description to the representation it generates.
\end{definition}

\noindent

If $d=\langle TM,a\rangle$ is a description of the representation $r$, then by definition
\[
\Gamma(d)=\Gamma(\langle TM,a\rangle)=TM(a)=r.
\]
Hence the description function provides the canonical computable map from valid descriptions to the representations they produce.

\section{Minimal Descriptions}

Inspired by the principle of Occam's razor\index{Occam's razor principle}\footnote{Occam's razor concerns the number of assumptions required by an explanation, not merely the number of symbols used to express it.}, if two explanations are equally successful in reconstructing a representation, we should in general prefer the shorter one. The reason is not only aesthetic economy. Shorter descriptions tend to be easier to understand, since they isolate the essential regularities of the representation without obscuring them under unnecessary detail. They are also easier to manipulate in practice: from a shorter and more structured model, it is usually simpler to derive consequences, formulate predictions, compare alternative explanations, and detect which assumptions are actually doing the explanatory work. By contrast, excessively long descriptions may reproduce the representation correctly while still hiding its structure behind redundancy, ad hoc clauses, or irrelevant complexity.

For this reason, shortness plays a central role in the theory of nescience. A representation may admit many valid descriptions, but not all of them are equally informative from a scientific point of view. Some may reconstruct the representation only at the price of introducing superfluous structure, while others may capture the same content more economically. This motivates the relevance of minimal descriptions. A minimal description of a representation is, by definition, a shortest valid description that reconstructs it. In this sense, minimal descriptions mark the limit of what can be known, or understood, about a representation through description: once a shortest description has been reached, no further compression is possible without losing the ability to reconstruct the representation. Minimal descriptions therefore provide a natural theoretical ideal of scientific understanding, even if in practice they are typically unknown and must be approximated by the effective models actually available.

\begin{definition}
\label{def:descriptions_perfect_model}
Let $\mathcal{D}_r$ be the set of descriptions of a representation $r \in \mathcal{R}$, and let $d \in \mathcal{D}_r$ be a description of $r$. We say that $d$ is a \emph{minimal description}\index{Minimal description} of the representation $r$ if there is no other description $d' \in\mathcal{D}_r$ such that $l(d') < l(d)$.
\end{definition}

Recall that what we know about an entity $e$ depends on the quality of the representation $r$ used. A minimal description is defined only relative to a given representation: it is the shortest valid description that reconstructs $r$. Therefore, even if we find a minimal description of $r$, this does not guarantee perfect knowledge of $e$. If the representation \(r\) is incorrect, incomplete, or otherwise defective, then the best possible description of $r$ will still inherit those representational limitations. Thus, perfect knowledge of an entity requires not only a minimal description, but also an admissible representation.

\begin{notation}
We denote by $d_r^{\star}$ the fact that the description $d$ is a minimal description of the representation $r$.
\end{notation}

In general, the minimal description of a representation need not be unique. A given representation \(r\) may admit several distinct descriptions of the same shortest length, corresponding to different but equally economical ways of reconstructing it. This non-uniqueness is not exceptional: different Turing machines, different encodings of the same computational strategy, or different decompositions between regularities and residual input may all yield descriptions that are optimal in length.

For this reason, it is useful to consider not just one minimal description, but the full collection of all optimal descriptions of \(r\). This set captures the entire class of shortest valid descriptions associated with the representation and provides the natural reference point for later notions such as descriptive deficiency, surplus, and mismodel.

\begin{definition}
\label{def:set_descriptions_perfect_model}
Let $\mathcal{D}_r$ be the set of descriptions of a representation $r \in \mathcal{R}_{\mathcal{E}}$. We define the \emph{set of minimal descriptions}\index{Set of minimal descriptiosn} for $r$, denoted by $\mathcal{D}^\star_r$, as the subset of $\mathcal{D}_r$ consisting of all minimal descriptions of $r$.
\end{definition}

That is, \(\mathcal{D}^\star_r\) contains precisely those descriptions in \(\mathcal{D}_r\) having minimum length.

Unfortunately, the set of minimal descriptions of a representation is generally unknown, and as Proposition \ref{prop:nescience-kolmogorov} shows, there exists no algorithm to compute it. In practice, we must rely on approximations to estimate how far our current best description is from a minimal one, that is, to quantify how much we do not know about a particular representation of an entity (see Chapter \ref{chap:Surfeit}).

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:nescience-kolmogorov}
Let $r \in \mathcal{R}$ be a representation and let $d_r^{\star}$ be a minimal description of $r$. Then we have $l \left( d_r^{\star} \right) = K\left( r \right)$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Apply Definition \ref{def:Kolmogorov-Complexity} and note that the Turing machines $TM$ used in descriptions of the form $\langle TM, a \rangle$ are required to be prefix-free.
\end{proof}

The actual length of a description $l(d)$ for a representation $r$ depends on the specific encoding of Turing machines used. This encoding method is determined by the chosen description function $\Gamma$. Fortunately, if we replace our description function with a different one, the length of perfect descriptions remains essentially unchanged, up to an additive constant that does not depend on the representation itself.

\begin{corollary}
Let $r \in \mathcal{R}$ be a representation, and let $\Gamma$ and $\dot{\Gamma}$ be two different description functions. Let $d_r^{\star}$ be a minimal description of $r$ under $\Gamma$, and $\dot{d}_r^{\star}$ a minimal description under $\dot{\Gamma}$. Then $\mid l \left( d_r^{\star} \right) - l \left( \dot{d}_r^{\star} \right) \mid \leq c$, where $c$ is a constant that does not depend on $r$.
\end{corollary}
\begin{proof}
Apply Proposition \ref{prop:nescience-kolmogorov} and Theorem \ref{def:Invariance-theorem}.
\end{proof}

In practice, within the theory of nescience, we are often not interested in computing the exact numerical value of the nescience associated with an entity for a given description–representation pair. Rather, our goal is to determine a weaker but sufficient notion: the relative ordering of different pairs of descriptions and representations according to their nescience. From this perspective, the specific details of the universal Turing machine used to compute description lengths are not essential\footnote{Do not confuse the internal workings of the universal Turing machine $\Gamma$ that maps descriptions to representations, which are not of interest here, with the internal workings of the universal oracle Turing machine $\delta$ that maps representations to entities, which are of interest, as understanding this mechanism is crucial to understanding how things work.}. Accordingly, for the remainder of this book we assume that the description function $\Gamma$ is fixed to a reference universal Turing machine. Alternatively, the reader may interpret all theorems in this book involving the length of the shortest models as holding up to an additive constant that does not depend on the topics themselves.

A central consequence of Proposition \ref{prop:nescience-kolmogorov} is that minimal descriptions are not only shortest among all valid descriptions of a representation, but also algorithmically irreducible as strings in their own right. This fact is one of the key elements of the theory of nescience, because it connects scientific understanding with incompressibility: once a representation has been described in an optimal way, the resulting description cannot itself be simplified any further, except by a fixed additive constant.

\begin{theorem}
\label{th:perfect_incompressible}
Let \(d_r^{\star}\) be a minimal description of a representation \(r\). Then \(d_r^{\star}\) is incompressible, in the sense that
\[
K\!\left(d_r^{\star}\right) \geq l\!\left(d_r^{\star}\right) - c,
\]
for some constant \(c\) independent of \(r\).
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
By Proposition \ref{prop:nescience-kolmogorov}, the length of a minimal description satisfies
\[
l\!\left(d_r^{\star}\right) = K(r),
\]
up to an additive constant fixed by the choice of universal Turing machine. Suppose that \(d_r^{\star}\) were compressible, that is, that
\[
K\!\left(d_r^{\star}\right) < l\!\left(d_r^{\star}\right) - c
\]
for arbitrarily large \(c\). Then there would exist a program strictly shorter than \(d_r^{\star}\) that outputs \(d_r^{\star}\). By composing this program with a fixed universal procedure that, given \(d_r^{\star}\), executes the description and outputs \(r\), one would obtain a description of \(r\) shorter than \(d_r^{\star}\), contradicting the minimality of \(d_r^{\star}\). Therefore, \(d_r^{\star}\) must be incompressible up to an additive constant.
\end{proof}

The significance of this corollary is substantial. It shows that minimal descriptions represent a limit of scientific understanding: once such a description has been reached, no further shortening is possible without losing the ability to reconstruct the representation. In this sense, a minimal description contains no dispensable structure. It also clarifies why minimal descriptions are theoretically so important in the theory of nescience. They do not merely provide the shortest way of generating a representation; they mark the point at which explanatory compression has been exhausted.

This result also explains the deep connection between perfect knowledge and randomness that underlies the theory. A minimal description is not random in the everyday sense of being meaningless or patternless, but it is random in the precise algorithmic sense of being incompressible. Thus, when scientific understanding reaches its ideal limit, the corresponding description can no longer be shortened: it becomes algorithmically irreducible. This is one of the reasons why the theory of nescience identifies the frontier of perfect understanding with incompressibility.

The converse of Theorem \ref{th:perfect_incompressible} does not generally hold: a description can be random without being the shortest possible one (incompressibility does not imply minimality). That is, we may have a description $d$ of a representation $r$ such that $l(d) = K(d)$, yet $l(d_r^{\star}) < l(d)$.

\begin{example}
\label{ex:description_neural}
Consider a deep neural network\index{Neural network} with an input layer of one thousand nodes, ten hidden layers of fifty thousand nodes each, and an output layer of one thousand nodes. Suppose the network is trained to output a fixed string of one thousand 1's for any given input. The Kolmogorov complexity of a string encoding of the trained neural network is much greater than that of the output string itself, which consists of one thousand identical bits.
\end{example}

The concept of a minimal description can be generalized from individual representations to entire entities. This generalization allows us to study the nature and properties of the entities themselves.

\begin{definition}
\label{def:entities_perfect_model}
Let $\mathcal{D}_e$ be the set of descriptions of an entity $e \in \mathcal{E}$. We define the \emph{set of perfect descriptions} of the entity $e$, denoted by $\mathcal{D}^\star_e$, as the subset of $\mathcal{D}_e$ consisting of perfect descriptions. The elements of $\mathcal{D}^\star_e$ are denoted by $d^\star_e$.
\end{definition}

The following proposition shows that the perfect descriptions of an entity are exactly the perfect descriptions of its representations.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:descriptions_entity_union}
For every entity \(e \in \mathcal{E}\),
\[
\mathcal{D}^\star_e=\bigcup_{r\in \mathcal{R}_e}\mathcal{D}^\star_r.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By definition, a description \(d\) belongs to \(\mathcal{D}_e^\star\) if and only if \(d\) is a perfect description of the entity \(e\). This means that \(d\) is a perfect description of some representation \(r \in \mathcal{R}_e\). Therefore, there exists \(r \in \mathcal{R}_e\) such that
\[
d \in \mathcal{D}_r^\star.
\]
Hence,
\[
\mathcal{D}_e^\star \subseteq \bigcup_{r\in \mathcal{R}_e}\mathcal{D}_r^\star.
\]

Conversely, suppose that
\[
d \in \bigcup_{r\in \mathcal{R}_e}\mathcal{D}_r^\star.
\]
Then there exists some \(r \in \mathcal{R}_e\) such that \(d \in \mathcal{D}_r^\star\). By definition, \(d\) is a perfect description of the representation \(r\). Since \(r\) is a representation of \(e\), it follows that \(d\) is a perfect description of the entity \(e\), and therefore
\[
d \in \mathcal{D}_e^\star.
\]
Thus,
\[
\bigcup_{r\in \mathcal{R}_e}\mathcal{D}_r^\star \subseteq \mathcal{D}_e^\star.
\]

Combining both inclusions, we conclude that
\[
\mathcal{D}^\star_e=\bigcup_{r\in \mathcal{R}_e}\mathcal{D}^\star_r.
\]
\end{proof}

This proposition makes explicit that perfect descriptions of an entity are always mediated by its representations. In particular, if an entity admits several representations, then its set of perfect descriptions is obtained by collecting the perfect descriptions associated with each of them. Thus, as in the case of valid descriptions, the descriptive study of an entity is organized through the representations by which that entity is encoded.

If $d^\star_e \in \mathcal{D}^\star_e$ there must exist a representation $r \in \mathcal{R}^\star_e$ such that $d^\star_e \in \mathcal{D}^\star_r$.

Technically speaking, we could have descriptions that are longer than the representations they describe, that is, descriptions that do not compress the representations. However, they are epistemically uninformative, since they fail to achieve any compression.

\begin{definition}
\label{def:trivial_model}
Let $r \in \mathcal{B}^\ast$ be a representation, and $d \in \mathcal{D}_r$ one of its descriptions. If $l(d) \geq l(r)$, we say that $d$ is a \emph{pleonastic description}\index{Pleonastic description} of the representation $r$.
\end{definition}

\begin{example}
\label{ex:topics_models_graph}
Consider the set of all possible finite graphs\index{Graph}. Since graphs are abstract mathematical objects, we must represent them as strings, for instance, using a binary encoding of their adjacency matrices (see Section \ref{sec:Graphs} for an introduction to graphs). The description $d = \langle TM, r \rangle$, where $r$ is the representation of a graph and $TM$ is a Turing machine that simply halts, belongs to $\mathcal{D}_r$ because $TM(r) = r$. However, this description is of limited interest, as it is likely not the shortest possible description of $r$.
\end{example}

It may happen that there is no shorter possible description of a representation than the representation itself. This occurs when the representation is an incompressible string. As discussed in Section \ref{sec:incompressibility_randomness}, the overwhelming majority of strings are incompressible. Conducting research on incompressible representations is unproductive, as it is not possible to find shorter models for such representations.

An interesting case arises when all the descriptions in $\mathcal{D}_e$ are pleonastic, that is, there exist no models shorter than the representation for any of the possible representations of the entity. This situation would occur if all representations of the entity $e$ are random strings. In such a case, scientific research would be fundamentally limited, as it would be impossible to find a suitable model for $e$. Our ability to understand and make predictions about $e$ would then be constrained by the length of its incompressible representations.

\section{Effective Descriptions}
\label{sec:effective_descriptions}

The set of valid descriptions \(\mathcal{D}\) is not directly usable in scientific practice. Although
every element of \(\mathcal{D}\) is a computable object, the space of all such descriptions is far too
large to be searched or analyzed exhaustively. In practice, scientists work only with a restricted
collection of descriptions that are currently available, communicable, and accepted within a given
scientific context. These are the descriptions that are actually proposed, discussed, refined, and
used in research. We call them \emph{effective descriptions}.

As in the case of effective representations, effective descriptions are context-dependent and need
not be correct. A description may be used in practice as a model of a given representation even if
it fails to generate that representation exactly. Thus, the notion of effective description captures
scientific usage rather than guaranteed adequacy. This distinction will be essential later, when we
study the inaccuracy of descriptions.

\begin{definition}
Let \(r \in \mathcal{R}\) be a representation. We define the set of \emph{effective descriptions}\index{Effective description} of \(r\), denoted by
\[
\hat{\mathcal{D}}_r \subseteq \mathcal{D},
\]
as the set of valid descriptions that are currently taken, within a given scientific context, to
generate, explain, or approximate the representation \(r\).
\end{definition}

The notion of effective description allows us to distinguish between representations that are
currently modeled in practice and those that are not.

\begin{definition}
A representation \(r \in \mathcal{R}\) is said to be \emph{effectively described}\index{Effectively described} if
\[
\hat{\mathcal{D}}_r \neq \varnothing,
\]
and \emph{effectively undescribed}\index{Effectively undescribed} otherwise.
\end{definition}

In the theory of nescience, effective describability and exact describability are different notions.
A representation is effectively described when there exists at least one description currently used
to model it, that is, when \(\hat{\mathcal{D}}_r \neq \varnothing\). By contrast, a representation is
described exactly when there exists at least one valid description that outputs it, that is, when
\(\mathcal{D}_r \neq \varnothing\). Effective descriptions therefore reflect scientific practice, whereas
the set \(\mathcal{D}_r\) reflects exact descriptive adequacy.

A common situation in science is one in which a representation is exactly describable but still
effectively undescribed. In such cases, a valid description of the representation exists in principle,
but no such description has yet been constructed or adopted in current scientific practice. The
opposite situation is also possible: a representation may be effectively described by one or more
descriptions that are taken to model it, even though those descriptions fail to generate the intended
representation exactly.

\begin{definition}
Let \(\mathcal{R}\) be a set of representations. We define the set of \emph{effective descriptions}
for \(\mathcal{R}\) by
\[
\hat{\mathcal{D}}_{\mathcal{R}} = \bigcup_{r \in \mathcal{R}} \hat{\mathcal{D}}_r.
\]
\end{definition}

The set \(\mathcal{D}\) contains all valid descriptions that are possible in principle, independently
of whether they are known or used. By contrast, the set \(\hat{\mathcal{D}}_{\mathcal{R}}\) contains only
those descriptions that have actually been proposed, formulated, or adopted within scientific
practice. Thus, \(\hat{\mathcal{D}}_{\mathcal{R}}\) should be understood as a time-dependent record of
the descriptions that are taken, within a given scientific context, to model representations in
\(\mathcal{R}\), whereas \(\mathcal{D}\) characterizes the larger conceptual space of all valid descriptions.

\begin{notation}
When the ambient set of representations is clear from the context, we denote the set of effective
descriptions simply by \(\hat{\mathcal{D}}\).
\end{notation}

As in the case of effective representations, the sets of effective descriptions associated with
different representations need not be disjoint. In particular, for two distinct representations
\(r_1, r_2 \in \mathcal{R}\), it is possible that
\[
\hat{\mathcal{D}}_{r_1} \cap \hat{\mathcal{D}}_{r_2} \neq \varnothing,
\]
reflecting the fact that the same description may be used, within a given scientific context, to
model or approximate more than one representation.

A further consequence of working with effective descriptions is that some descriptions currently
assigned to a representation may fail to generate that representation. This phenomenon is not
exceptional; it reflects the exploratory character of scientific modeling. We therefore isolate such
descriptions explicitly.

\begin{definition}
Let \(r \in \mathcal{R}\). We define the set of \emph{inaccurate effective descriptions}\index{Inaccurate effective description} of \(r\), denoted by
\[
\hat{\mathcal{D}}^{\neq}_r,
\]
as
\[
\hat{\mathcal{D}}^{\neq}_r
=
\{d \in \hat{\mathcal{D}}_r : \gamma(d) \neq r\}.
\]
\end{definition}

Thus, an inaccurate effective description of \(r\) is a description that is used in practice to
describe \(r\), but whose output differs from \(r\). These descriptions will be the subject of the
chapter on Inaccuracy, where the discrepancy between \(\gamma(d)\) and the intended representation
\(r\) will be measured quantitatively.

Complementarily, we may isolate those effective descriptions that do generate the intended
representation exactly.

\begin{definition}
Let \(r \in \mathcal{R}\). We define the set of \emph{exact effective descriptions} of \(r\), denoted by
\[
\hat{\mathcal{D}}^{=}_r,
\]
as
\[
\hat{\mathcal{D}}^{=}_r
=
\{d \in \hat{\mathcal{D}}_r : \gamma(d) = r\}.
\]
\end{definition}

The two previous notions partition the effective descriptions of a representation into those that
are exact and those that are inaccurate.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:effective_description_partition}
Let \(r \in \mathcal{R}\). Then
\[
\hat{\mathcal{D}}_r
=
\hat{\mathcal{D}}^{=}_r \cup \hat{\mathcal{D}}^{\neq}_r
\]
and
\[
\hat{\mathcal{D}}^{=}_r \cap \hat{\mathcal{D}}^{\neq}_r = \varnothing.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(d \in \hat{\mathcal{D}}_r\). Since \(d \in \mathcal{D}\), the value \(\gamma(d)\) is defined, and
either \(\gamma(d)=r\) or \(\gamma(d)\neq r\). In the first case, \(d \in \hat{\mathcal{D}}^{=}_r\); in the
second, \(d \in \hat{\mathcal{D}}^{\neq}_r\). Therefore
\[
\hat{\mathcal{D}}_r
\subseteq
\hat{\mathcal{D}}^{=}_r \cup \hat{\mathcal{D}}^{\neq}_r.
\]
The reverse inclusion is immediate from the definitions of
\(\hat{\mathcal{D}}^{=}_r\) and \(\hat{\mathcal{D}}^{\neq}_r\), since both are subsets of
\(\hat{\mathcal{D}}_r\). Hence
\[
\hat{\mathcal{D}}_r
=
\hat{\mathcal{D}}^{=}_r \cup \hat{\mathcal{D}}^{\neq}_r.
\]

Moreover, no description can satisfy simultaneously \(\gamma(d)=r\) and \(\gamma(d)\neq r\). Hence
\[
\hat{\mathcal{D}}^{=}_r \cap \hat{\mathcal{D}}^{\neq}_r = \varnothing.
\]
\end{proof}

The previous proposition shows that every effective description is either exact or inaccurate with
respect to the representation to which it is assigned in practice. In this sense, inaccuracy is not an
external pathology, but an internal possibility built into the very notion of effective scientific
description.

The exact effective descriptions of a representation are precisely those effective descriptions that
also belong to its set of exact descriptions.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:exact_effective_descriptions_intersection}
Let \(r \in \mathcal{R}\). Then
\[
\hat{\mathcal{D}}^{=}_r = \hat{\mathcal{D}}_r \cap \mathcal{D}_r.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By definition,
\[
\hat{\mathcal{D}}^{=}_r
=
\{d \in \hat{\mathcal{D}}_r : \gamma(d)=r\}.
\]
On the other hand,
\[
\mathcal{D}_r
=
\{d \in \mathcal{D} : \gamma(d)=r\}.
\]
Therefore, \(d\) belongs to \(\hat{\mathcal{D}}^{=}_r\) if and only if it belongs to
\(\hat{\mathcal{D}}_r\) and also to \(\mathcal{D}_r\). Hence
\[
\hat{\mathcal{D}}^{=}_r = \hat{\mathcal{D}}_r \cap \mathcal{D}_r.
\]
\end{proof}

This identity clarifies the relation between effective descriptions and exact descriptions: the exact
effective descriptions are precisely those descriptions that are both used in practice and correct with
respect to the intended representation.

Effective descriptions are naturally time-dependent. As new scientific models are proposed,
refined, or superseded, the collection of descriptions associated with a representation evolves.

\begin{notation}
We denote by \(\hat{\mathcal{D}}_r^t\) the set of effective descriptions of a representation \(r \in \mathcal{R}\)
available at time \(t\), and by \(\hat{\mathcal{D}}^t\) the set of all effective descriptions available at time \(t\).
\end{notation}

In many contexts, effective descriptions are monotonic over time, in the sense that descriptions
once formulated are retained in the historical record even if they are later rejected or replaced.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:effective_descriptions_monotonic}
Let \(\hat{\mathcal{D}}_r^{t_1}\) and \(\hat{\mathcal{D}}_r^{t_2}\) denote the set of effective descriptions
associated with a representation \(r \in \mathcal{R}\) at times \(t_1\) and \(t_2\), respectively. Then
\[
\hat{\mathcal{D}}_r^{t_1} \subseteq \hat{\mathcal{D}}_r^{t_2}
\qquad \text{for all} \qquad t_1 < t_2.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By assumption, descriptions available at time \(t_1\) are retained at later times, even if they are
superseded or no longer considered adequate, and additional descriptions may be introduced.
Therefore, any effective description available at time \(t_1\) is also available at time \(t_2 > t_1\).
\end{proof}

As a direct consequence, the class of effectively described representations is also monotonic over
time.

\begin{proposition}
Let
\[
\hat{\mathcal{R}}^{\,\mathcal{D},t}
=
\{r \in \mathcal{R} : \hat{\mathcal{D}}_r^t \neq \varnothing\}
\]
denote the set of effectively described representations at time \(t\). Then
\[
\hat{\mathcal{R}}^{\,\mathcal{D},t_1}
\subseteq
\hat{\mathcal{R}}^{\,\mathcal{D},t_2}
\qquad \text{for all} \qquad t_1 < t_2.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If \(r \in \hat{\mathcal{R}}^{\,\mathcal{D},t_1}\), then \(\hat{\mathcal{D}}_r^{t_1} \neq \varnothing\).
By Proposition~\ref{prop:effective_descriptions_monotonic}, we have
\(\hat{\mathcal{D}}_r^{t_2} \neq \varnothing\) for all \(t_2 > t_1\). Hence
\(r \in \hat{\mathcal{R}}^{\,\mathcal{D},t_2}\).
\end{proof}

The set of effective descriptions is thus not fixed by \(\mathcal{D}\) alone, but by the interaction
between representations, available model classes, accepted scientific assumptions, and the historical
development of scientific practice. In this sense, scientific progress may be understood not only as
the expansion of the space of effective descriptions, but also as the gradual replacement of inaccurate
effective descriptions by exact or more adequate ones. The quantitative study of that inadequacy,
however, belongs properly to the chapter on Inaccuracy.

\section{Models}
\label{sec:models}

The descriptions introduced in Section~\ref{sec:descriptions_models} are computable objects capable of reconstructing representations. Each description $d$ has the form $d = \langle TM, a \rangle$, where the Turing machine $TM$ encodes the regularities present in the representation, and the input string \(a\) contains the remaining incompressible part. In this sense, every description can be understood as a decomposition into a structured component, which captures regularity, and a residual component, which accounts for what cannot be further compressed.

However, the space of all possible descriptions is extremely large and lacks internal organization. In scientific practice, researchers do not attempt to explore this space exhaustively. Instead, they restrict their attention to particular families of descriptions characterized by shared structural assumptions. These restricted families correspond to what scientists commonly call \emph{models}.

Intuitively, a model expresses a hypothesis about the regularities underlying a representation. By fixing a particular structure, one limits the class of admissible descriptions to those that conform to that structure. The remaining variability is then captured by the input component of the description, which specifies the particular instance of the model.

\begin{definition}
\label{def:model_class}
Let \(\mathcal{D}\) be the set of valid descriptions. We define a \emph{model class}\index{Model class} as a subset
\[
\mathcal{M} \subseteq \mathcal{D}.
\]
\end{definition}

Each element of a model class is therefore a description that satisfies a specific structural constraint. The defining condition of the subset $\mathcal{M}$ encodes the structural hypothesis underlying the model.

A particularly important case is that of parametric models, where the variability within the model class is controlled by a parameter space.

\begin{definition}
\label{def:parametric_model}
We define \emph{parametric model}\index{Parametric model} as a model class of the form
\[
\mathcal{M} = \{ d_{\theta} : \theta \in \Theta \},
\]
where $\Theta$ is a parameter space and each $d_{\theta} \in \mathcal{D}$ is a description determined by the parameter $\theta$.
\end{definition}

In this setting, the general structure $d = \langle TM, a \rangle$ admits a natural reinterpretation. The Turing machine $TM$ is no longer an arbitrary program, but a member of a restricted family encoding a specific type of model, while the input $a$ represents the parameters of that model. Thus, descriptions in a parametric model class can be written as $d = \langle TM_m, \theta \rangle$, where $TM_m$ encodes the structural form of the model \(m\), and \(\theta\) specifies a particular instance within that structure.

\begin{example}
\label{ex:linear_regression_model}
Consider a representation composed of a set of observed data points $(x_i, y_i)$ in the plane. A common modeling assumption is that the data follow a linear relationship. This leads to the parametric model class of linear functions.

Let $\Theta = \mathbb{R}^2$, and for each parameter vector $\theta = (w,b)$, define the description
\[
d_{\theta} = \langle TM_{\text{lin}}, \theta \rangle,
\]
where $TM_{\text{lin}}$ is a Turing machine that, on input $(w,b)$, outputs the representation of the function
\[
f_{\theta}(x) = w x + b.
\]
The model class is therefore
\[
\mathcal{M} = \{ d_{\theta} : \theta \in \mathbb{R}^2 \}.
\]

In this example, the machine $TM_{\text{lin}}$ encodes the structural assumption that the relationship between $x$ and $y$ is linear, while the input $\theta = (w,b)$ specifies the particular instance of the model that best fits the data. The regularity of the data is captured by the linear form, and the remaining information is contained in the parameter values.
\end{example}

Restricting attention to model classes therefore provides a principled way to organize the space of descriptions. Instead of searching over all possible descriptions, scientific practice proceeds by selecting a class of models, and then adjusting the parameters within that class. The success of a model then depends both on the adequacy of its structural assumptions and on the choice of parameters used to instantiate it.

A model class also induces an \emph{inductive bias}. By restricting the space of admissible descriptions, one restricts the family of regularities that can be captured. This bias is not merely a computational convenience: it determines, in part, what kinds of explanations are even available within the chosen framework.

\begin{definition}
\label{def:expressive_set_model_class}
Let $\mathcal{M} \subseteq \mathcal{D}$ be a model class. We define the \emph{expressive set}\index{Expressive set of a model class} of $\mathcal{M}$ by
\[
\mathcal{R}_{\mathcal{M}} = \{ r \in \mathcal{R} : \exists d \in \mathcal{M},\ \gamma(d)=r \}.
\]
\end{definition}

The set $\mathcal{R}_{\mathcal{M}}$ contains precisely those representations that can be generated by at least one description in the model class $\mathcal{M}$. It therefore characterizes the expressive power of the class.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:model_class_inclusion}
Let $\mathcal{M}_1, \mathcal{M}_2 \subseteq \mathcal{D}$ be model classes such that
\[
\mathcal{M}_1 \subseteq \mathcal{M}_2.
\]
Then
\[
\mathcal{R}_{\mathcal{M}_1} \subseteq \mathcal{R}_{\mathcal{M}_2}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let $r \in \mathcal{R}_{\mathcal{M}_1}$. By Definition~\ref{def:expressive_set_model_class}, there exists $d \in \mathcal{M}_1$ such that $\gamma(d)=r$. Since $\mathcal{M}_1 \subseteq \mathcal{M}_2$, we also have $d \in \mathcal{M}_2$, and therefore $r \in \mathcal{R}_{\mathcal{M}_2}$.
\end{proof}

This proposition shows that enlarging a model class can only increase, or at least preserve, the set of representations that can be described within it. In this sense, more expressive model classes correspond to larger subsets of \(\mathcal{D}\).

The converse side of this observation is that restrictive model classes may fail to capture some representations altogether.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:model_class_not_universal}
In general, there exist representations \(r \in \mathcal{R}\) such that
\[
\mathcal{D}_r \neq \varnothing
\qquad\text{but}\qquad
\mathcal{D}_r \cap \mathcal{M} = \varnothing.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
A model class \(\mathcal{M}\) is, in general, a proper subset of \(\mathcal{D}\). Hence there may exist valid descriptions \(d \in \mathcal{D} \setminus \mathcal{M}\). Let \(r = \gamma(d)\). Then \(d \in \mathcal{D}_r\), so \(\mathcal{D}_r \neq \varnothing\). However, there need not exist any description in \(\mathcal{M}\) that generates \(r\). In that case,
\[
\mathcal{D}_r \cap \mathcal{M} = \varnothing.
\]
\end{proof}

This proposition formalizes an important limitation of model classes: by imposing structure, they simplify the search for descriptions, but they may also exclude some representations that are describable in principle.

In general, descriptions belonging to a model class need not be minimal, since the model class constitutes a restricted subset of the space of all descriptions. A minimal description of a representation may rely on regularities that are not expressible within the chosen model class, and therefore may not belong to it. As a consequence, even if a representation admits a shorter description, that description may lie outside the model class under consideration.

Restricting attention to a model class therefore introduces a trade-off between tractability and optimality. A more restrictive class simplifies the search for descriptions by imposing structure, but may fail to capture relevant regularities or exclude minimal descriptions. A more expressive class increases representational power, but usually enlarges the search space and makes the modeling problem harder.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:model_not_minimal}
Let \(r \in \mathcal{R}\) be a representation, and let
\[
d = \langle TM_m, \theta \rangle \in \mathcal{M}
\]
be a description of \(r\) belonging to a parametric model class. Then there exists a constant \(c\) such that
\[
K(r) \leq \ell(TM_m) + \ell(\theta) + c.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since \(d = \langle TM_m, \theta \rangle\) is a valid description of \(r\), we have
\[
\gamma(d) = TM_m(\theta) = r.
\]
Therefore, there exists a program of length at most \(\ell(TM_m) + \ell(\theta) + c\) that outputs \(r\), where the constant \(c\) accounts for the fixed overhead of encoding the pairing and simulating the universal Turing machine. By definition of Kolmogorov complexity,
\[
K(r) \leq \ell(TM_m) + \ell(\theta) + c.
\]
\end{proof}

This inequality shows that the length of a model-based description provides an upper bound on the Kolmogorov complexity of the representation. However, this bound need not be tight. A minimal description of \(r\) may exploit regularities that are not captured by the chosen model class, and therefore be strictly shorter than any description of the form \(\langle TM_m, \theta \rangle\) with \(TM_m \in \mathcal{M}\).

\begin{corollary}
Descriptions in a model class need not be minimal.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Let \(d = \langle TM_m, \theta \rangle\) be a description of \(r\) in the model class \(\mathcal{M}\). By Proposition~\ref{prop:model_not_minimal},
\[
K(r) \leq \ell(TM_m) + \ell(\theta) + c.
\]
A minimal description of \(r\) has length \(K(r) + O(1)\), which may be strictly smaller than \(\ell(TM_m) + \ell(\theta)\). Hence, \(d\) need not be a minimal description.
\end{proof}

Another important point is that the same representation may admit descriptions in different model classes. This reflects a common situation in scientific practice, where the same phenomenon may be explained using distinct structural hypotheses.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:representation_multiple_model_classes}
In general, a representation \(r \in \mathcal{R}\) may admit descriptions in more than one model class. That is, there may exist model classes \(\mathcal{M}_1, \mathcal{M}_2 \subseteq \mathcal{D}\) such that
\[
\mathcal{D}_r \cap \mathcal{M}_1 \neq \varnothing
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\mathcal{D}_r \cap \mathcal{M}_2 \neq \varnothing.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
A representation may possess several distinct valid descriptions, and these descriptions may satisfy different structural constraints. Hence it is possible for one description of \(r\) to belong to \(\mathcal{M}_1\) and another to belong to \(\mathcal{M}_2\), yielding the stated nonempty intersections.
\end{proof}

This non-uniqueness is one of the reasons why model selection is a central problem in science: different model classes may provide different, and sometimes incompatible, ways of describing the same representation.

Finally, even within a fixed parametric model class, the parameterization itself need not be unique.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:parametric_model_noninjective}
In general, parametric model classes need not be injective with respect to the parameterization. That is, there may exist \(\theta_1 \neq \theta_2\) such that
\[
\gamma(\langle TM_m, \theta_1 \rangle)
=
\gamma(\langle TM_m, \theta_2 \rangle).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The definition of parametric model class does not require the mapping
\[
\theta \mapsto \gamma(\langle TM_m,\theta\rangle)
\]
to be injective. Hence distinct parameter values may generate the same representation.
\end{proof}

This shows that redundancy may arise not only from the model class itself, but also from the chosen parameterization. Distinct parameter values may encode the same effective description of a representation, a fact that will later be relevant when discussing surplus and the simplification of descriptions.

\section{Operations on Descriptions}

Augmentation and reduction are two complementary operations on descriptions. Augmentation models the idea of enriching a description by supplying additional computable information that may be missing from the original one. Reduction models the opposite process: simplifying a description by separating out information that is not needed for the purpose at hand. Together, these operations provide a basic formal vocabulary for describing how scientific models may be refined, expanded, or simplified over time.

These operations are introduced here at a purely structural level. Their purpose is not yet to determine whether a description has improved, or whether a particular transformation brings it closer to an ideal description. Rather, they formalize two elementary ways of modifying descriptions: by adding information through augmentation, and by removing information through reduction. In both cases, the result is required to remain within the domain of valid descriptions, so that the transformed object continues to be interpretable as a computable description.

This distinction is important because the same formal operation may have very different epistemic effects depending on the context. An augmentation may add genuinely useful information, but it may also introduce irrelevant detail. Likewise, a reduction may remove superfluous structure, but it may also discard information that later proves important. For this reason, augmentation and reduction are best understood at this stage as general operations on descriptions. Their role in decreasing deficiency, surplus, or mismodel will be examined in Chapter \ref{chap:Mismodel}, once those defects have been formally defined.


% Subsection: Operation of Augmentation

\subsection*{Operation of Augmentation}

A natural way to improve the quality of a description is by enriching it with an auxiliary description that contributes part of the information missing from the original one. We call this operation \emph{augmentation}. Its purpose is to produce a new valid description that may better account for the representation under study. Unlike the augmentation of representations (see Section \ref{sec:joint_representations}), where additional symbols are inserted directly into a string, the augmentation of descriptions is more appropriately treated as an operation on valid descriptions themselves.

Since not every auxiliary description need be suitable for augmenting every original description, we model augmentation as a partial computable operation on the set of valid descriptions.

\begin{definition}
We define the \emph{augmentation operator}\index{Augmentation operator} as a partial computable function
\[
\mathrm{aug} : \mathcal{D} \times \mathcal{D} \rightharpoonup \mathcal{D}.
\]
For $d, d' \in \mathcal{D}$, whenever $\mathrm{aug}(d, d')$ is defined, the string $\mathrm{aug}(d, d')$ is called an \emph{augmentation}\index{Augmentation of descriptions} of $d$ by $d'$, and $d'$ is called an \emph{augmentation description}\index{Augmentation description}.
\end{definition}

This definition does not prescribe how the information encoded by $d$ and $d'$ must be combined, only that the combination be computable whenever it is defined, and that the result be a valid description. In this way, augmentation is introduced as a structural operation on descriptions, independently of the particular effect it may have on the representation produced. Questions concerning whether augmentation improves a description, reduces its deficiency, or brings it closer to a target representation will be addressed in Chapter \ref{chap:Mismodel}.

Thus, augmentation is a partially defined internal operation on the set of valid descriptions.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:augmentation_closed}
If $d, d' \in \mathcal{D}$ and $\mathrm{aug}(d,d')\downarrow$, then $\mathrm{aug}(d,d') \in \mathcal{D}$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
This follows directly from the definition of augmentation operator.
\end{proof}

Therefore, whenever an augmentation is defined, augmenting a valid description by another valid description yields a new valid description.

Since augmentation is computable on its domain, it can be iterated whenever each successive step is defined.

\begin{proposition}
Let $d, d_1, \ldots, d_n \in \mathcal{D}$, with $n \geq 1$. If all intermediate augmentations are defined, that is, if
\[
\mathrm{aug}(d,d_1)\downarrow,\quad
\mathrm{aug}(\mathrm{aug}(d,d_1),d_2)\downarrow,\quad \ldots,\quad
\mathrm{aug}(\mathrm{aug}(\cdots \mathrm{aug}(d,d_1),\ldots),d_n)\downarrow,
\]
then the iterated augmentation
\[
\mathrm{aug}(\mathrm{aug}(\cdots \mathrm{aug}(d,d_1),d_2)\cdots,d_n)
\]
belongs to \(\mathcal{D}\).
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
By Proposition \ref{prop:augmentation_closed}, if $\mathrm{aug}(d, d_1)\downarrow$, then $\mathrm{aug}(d, d_1) \in \mathcal{D}$. Repeating the same argument at each successive stage yields the result by induction on \(n\).
\end{proof}

This shows that augmentation can be applied repeatedly, thereby allowing the progressive construction of more elaborate descriptions from simpler ones, provided that each intermediate augmentation is defined.

The definition of augmentation only requires that \(\mathrm{aug}\) be a partial computable function from $\mathcal{D}\times\mathcal{D}$ to $\mathcal{D}$. It imposes no condition requiring the output representation of $\mathrm{aug}(d, d')$, when defined, to coincide with that of $d$. Therefore, an augmentation operator may perfectly well be chosen so that, for some $d, d' \in \mathcal{D}$ such that $\mathrm{aug}(d, d') \downarrow$, we have $\gamma(\mathrm{aug}(d,d')) \neq \gamma(d)$.

An augmentation is therefore not merely a passive attachment of additional information to a description; it is a new valid description obtained by computably combining two existing ones whenever that combination is meaningful within the chosen framework. As a consequence, the resulting description may produce the same representation as the original description, but it may also produce a different one.

% Subsection: Operation of Reduction

\subsection*{Operation of Reduction}

A natural way to improve the quality of a description is by removing information that is not needed for the purpose at hand. We call this operation \emph{reduction}. Its purpose is to decompose a valid description into two parts: a retained part, which is kept as the relevant description, and a discarded part, which contains the information irrelevant or redundant. Unlike the reduction of representations, where symbols are deleted directly from a string, the reduction of descriptions is more appropriately treated as an operation on valid descriptions themselves.

Since not every description need admit a meaningful decomposition, we model reduction as a partial computable operation on the set of valid descriptions.

\begin{definition}
We define the \emph{reduction operator}\index{Reduction operator} as a partial computable function
\[
\mathrm{red} : \mathcal{D} \rightharpoonup \mathcal{D}\times\mathcal{D}
\]
such that, whenever $\mathrm{red}(d) = (d_1,d_2)$, the following conditions hold:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\roman*]
    \item $\ell(d_1) \leq \ell(d)$,
    \item $\mathrm{aug}(d_1, d_2) \downarrow \, \land \, \mathrm{aug}(d_1,d_2)=d$.
\end{enumerate}
The description $d_1$ is called a \emph{reduction}\index{Reduction of descriptions} of $d$, and $d_2$ is called the \emph{discarded description}\index{Discarded description}.
\end{definition}

This definition does not prescribe how the decomposition of \(d\) into \(d_1\) and \(d_2\) is obtained, only that it be computable whenever it is defined, that the retained part \(d_1\) be no longer than the original description, and that the original description be recoverable by augmenting \(d_1\) with \(d_2\). In this way, reduction is introduced as a structural operation on descriptions, independently of the particular effect it may have on the representation produced. Questions concerning whether reduction improves a description, decreases its surplus, or preserves the represented string will be addressed in Chapter \ref{chap:Mismodel}.

Reduction is a partially defined decomposition operation on the set of valid descriptions.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:reduction_components_valid}
If $d \in \mathcal{D}$ and $\mathrm{red}(d)\downarrow$, then there exist $d_1, d_2 \in \mathcal{D}$ such that $\mathrm{red}(d)=(d_1,d_2)$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
This follows directly from the definition of reduction operator.
\end{proof}

Hence, whenever a reduction is defined, it decomposes a valid description into two valid descriptions.

The next proposition records the basic size condition built into the notion of reduction.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:reduction_not_longer}
If \(d \in \mathcal{D}\) and \(\mathrm{red}(d)=(d_1,d_2)\), then $\ell(d_1)\leq \ell(d)$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
This is one of the defining conditions of the reduction operator.
\end{proof}

Thus, the retained part of a reduction is never longer than the original description.

A second basic property is that reduction is compatible with augmentation in the sense that the original description can be recovered from the retained part and the discarded part.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:reduction_aug_reconstructs}
If \(d \in \mathcal{D}\) and \(\mathrm{red}(d)=(d_1,d_2)\), then
\[
\mathrm{aug}(d_1, d_2) \downarrow \, \land \, \mathrm{aug}(d_1,d_2)=d.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
This is again one of the defining conditions of the reduction operator.
\end{proof}

This proposition formalizes the intended interpretation of reduction: the discarded part is not arbitrary, but precisely the information that must be restored, via augmentation, in order to recover the original description.

As an immediate consequence, every defined reduction determines a factorization of the original description through the augmentation operator.

\begin{corollary}
If \(d \in \mathcal{D}\) and \(\mathrm{red}(d)=(d_1,d_2)\), then \(d\) belongs to the image of \(\mathrm{aug}\).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
By Proposition \ref{prop:reduction_aug_reconstructs}, $d=\mathrm{aug}(d_1,d_2)$ with \(\mathrm{aug}(d_1,d_2)\downarrow\). Therefore \(d\) lies in the image of \(\mathrm{aug}\).
\end{proof}

This shows that every reducible description can be reconstructed by augmenting its retained part with its discarded part.

Reduction can also be iterated whenever the retained part admits a further reduction.

\begin{proposition}
Let \(d \in \mathcal{D}\). Suppose
\[
\mathrm{red}(d)=(d_1,e_1),
\qquad
\mathrm{red}(d_1)=(d_2,e_2),
\qquad
\ldots,
\qquad
\mathrm{red}(d_{n-1})=(d_n,e_n)
\]
are all defined. Then
\[
\ell(d_n)\leq \ell(d_{n-1})\leq \cdots \leq \ell(d_1)\leq \ell(d).
\]
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
By Proposition \ref{prop:reduction_not_longer},
\[
\ell(d_1)\leq \ell(d).
\]
Applying the same proposition to each subsequent reduction yields
\[
\ell(d_2)\leq \ell(d_1),\quad \ldots,\quad \ell(d_n)\leq \ell(d_{n-1}).
\]
Chaining these inequalities gives the result.
\end{proof}

This shows that repeated reduction yields a non-increasing sequence of retained-description lengths, and therefore behaves monotonically with respect to description size.

In general, it is not the case that, if \(\mathrm{red}(d)=(d_1,d_2)\), then $\gamma(d_1)=\gamma(d)$. The definition of reduction requires only that \(d_1,d_2\in\mathcal{D}\), that \(\ell(d_1)\leq \ell(d)\), and that $\mathrm{aug}(d_1,d_2)=d$ when defined. It imposes no condition requiring \(d_1\) and \(d\) to produce the same output under \(\gamma\). Therefore, a reduction operator may perfectly well be chosen so that, for some \(d\in\mathcal{D}\) with \(\mathrm{red}(d)=(d_1,d_2)\), $\gamma(d_1)\neq \gamma(d)$.

Thus, a reduction is not merely the deletion of inert material from a description. It is a decomposition into a retained part and a discarded part, and the retained part may or may not describe the same representation as the original description.

\section{Descriptions for Joint Representations}
\label{sec:description_joint_represenation}

In Section \ref{sec:joint_representations}, we introduced the concept of a joint representation $ts$, formed by combining two individual representations $t$ and $s$. In this section, we aim to study how the length of the minimal description of a joint representation relates to the lengths of the minimal descriptions of the individual representations.

The length of the minimal description of a joint representation is greater than or equal to the length of the minimal description of either individual representation. In other words, the more information a representation contains, the longer it takes to describe.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:joint_length}
Let $t,s \in \mathcal{R}_\mathcal{E}$ be two representations, and let $d_{t}^{\star}$, $d_{s}^{\star}$, and $d_{ts}^{\star}$ denote the minimal descriptions of the representations $t$, $s$, and the joint representation $ts$, respectively. Then: $l \left( d_{ts}^{\star} \right) \geq l \left( d_{t}^{\star} \right) \quad \text{and} \quad l \left( d_{ts}^{\star} \right) \geq l \left( d_{s}^{\star} \right).$
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
The inequality $l \left( d_{ts}^{\star} \right) \geq l \left( d_{t}^{\star} \right)$ is equivalent to $K(ts) \geq K(t)$. The result then follows from Proposition \ref{prop:excess_kolmogorov}.
\end{proof}

Intuitively, adding more information to a representation is beneficial if the additional information is relevant to describing the entity of interest. However, including irrelevant information leads to unnecessarily long models. Recall that joining representations can serve either to concatenate two partial representations of the same entity or to enrich a representation by adding missing symbols.

If the selected representations partially overlap, we can exploit this redundancy to produce a joint description that is shorter than the mere concatenation of the individual descriptions. In the worst-case scenario, the minimal description of a joint representation would be equal in length to the sum of the minimal descriptions of the individual representations.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:joint_sum}
Let $t, s \in \mathcal{R}_\mathcal{E}$ be two representations, and let $d_{t}^{\star}$, $d_{s}^{\star}$, and $d_{ts}^{\star}$ denote the minimal descriptions of the representations $t$, $s$, and the joint representation $ts$, respectively. Then: $l \left( d_{ts}^{\star} \right) \leq l \left( d_{t}^{\star} \right) + l \left( d_{s}^{\star} \right).$
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
The inequality $l \left( d_{ts}^{\star} \right) \leq l \left( d_{t}^{\star} \right) + l \left( d_{s}^{\star} \right)$ is equivalent to $K(ts) \leq K(t) + K(s)$. The result follows from Proposition \ref{prop:additive_kolmogorov}.
\end{proof}

One interpretation of Proposition \ref{prop:joint_sum} is that including redundant information in the representation of an entity does not hinder our ability to find its shortest possible description. From the perspective of compression, redundancy can be eliminated during the modeling process. Therefore, in practice, we may prefer to work with representations that are longer but make the process of scientific discovery, i.e., finding the best model, easier, even if they contain superfluous information. In contrast, Proposition \ref{prop:joint_length} highlights a different concern: adding irrelevant or non-informative symbols to a representation should be avoided, as they increase the complexity of the description without contributing useful information about the entity.

Finally, the following proposition shows that the order of the representations in the minimal description of a joint representation does not affect its length.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:joint_order}
Let $t, s \in \mathcal{R}_\mathcal{E}$ be two representations, and let $d_{ts}^{\star}$ and $d_{st}^{\star}$ be the minimal descriptions of the joint representations $ts$ and $st$, respectively. Then: $l \left( d_{ts}^{\star} \right) = l \left( d_{st}^{\star} \right).$
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
The equality $l \left( m_{ts}^{\star} \right) = l \left( m_{st}^{\star} \right)$ is equivalent to $K(ts) = K(st)$. The result follows from Proposition \ref{prop:kolmogorov_order}.
\end{proof}

It is important to note, however, that joining representations is not a commutative operation, there is no guarantee that the strings $ts$ and $st$ encode the same entity. Moreover, given only the concatenated string $ts$, it is generally not possible to recover the original representations $t$ and $s$, since they are not self-delimiting.

Propositions \ref{prop:joint_length}, \ref{prop:joint_sum} and \ref{prop:joint_order} can be generalized to any arbitrary, but finite, collection of representations $t_1, t_2, \ldots, t_n$.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:joint_multiple_topics}
Let $t_1, t_2, \ldots, t_n \in \mathcal{R}_\mathcal{E}$ be a finite collection of representations. Then, we have that:

\renewcommand{\theenumi}{\roman{enumi}}
\begin{enumerate}
\item $l(d_{t_1 t_2 \ldots t_n}^\star) \geq l(d_ {t_i}^\star) \; \forall \, 1 \leq i \leq n$,
\item $l(d_{t_1 t_2 \ldots t_n}^\star) \leq l(d_ {t_1}^\star) + l(d_ {t_2}^\star) + \ldots + l(d_ {t_n}^\star)$,
\item $l(d_{t_1 \ldots t_i \ldots t_j \ldots t_n}^\star) = l(d_{t_1 \ldots t_j \ldots t_i \ldots t_n}^\star) + c \; \forall \, 1 \leq i \leq j \leq n$,
\item $l(d_{t_1 \ldots t_{n-1}}^\star) \leq l(d_{t_1 \ldots t_{n-1} t_n}^\star)$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Apply Propositions \ref{prop:joint_length}, \ref{prop:joint_sum} and \ref{prop:joint_order} to individual pairs of representations $i$ and $j$.
\end{proof}

\section{Conditional Descriptions}
\label{sec:conditional_descriptions}

It is often impractical to include all the information required to reconstruct a representation within a single description. Scientific descriptions are rarely constructed in isolation. They normally presuppose a considerable amount of background knowledge: mathematical tools, measurement conventions, auxiliary theories, previously established empirical facts, and accepted methods of inference. For example, a modern formulation of Newtonian mechanics may rely on the language of infinitesimal calculus without explicitly reconstructing calculus as part of the theory itself.

This motivates the notion of a \emph{conditional description}. A conditional description is a description whose interpretation is made relative to some prior description. The prior description is not incorporated as a further part of the model; rather, it is treated as background knowledge that may be freely used when reconstructing the target representation.

In this section we formalize conditioning as a computable operation on descriptions. Unlike augmentation, which combines two descriptions into a larger description, conditioning changes the context in which a description is
evaluated.

\begin{definition}
We define a \emph{conditioning operator}\index{Conditioning operator} as a computable function
\[
\operatorname{cond}:\mathcal D\times \mathcal D\to \mathcal D
\]
such that, for every pair of descriptions
\[
d_1 = \langle TM_1, a_1 \rangle,
\qquad
d_2 = \langle TM_2, a_2 \rangle \in \mathcal D,
\]
we have
\[
\operatorname{cond}(d_1,d_2)=d_3,
\]
where
\[
d_3=\langle TM_3,\langle a_1,d_2\rangle\rangle \in \mathcal D,
\]
and \(TM_3\) is a Turing machine obtained computably from \(TM_1\) such that, on input
\(\langle a_1,d_2\rangle\), it parses \(d_2=\langle TM_2,a_2\rangle\), computes the output
\(TM_2(a_2)\), and uses that output as auxiliary computable information during the simulation of
\(TM_1(a_1)\).
\end{definition}

More precisely, \(TM_3\) is allowed to parse \(d_2=\langle TM_2,a_2\rangle\),
simulate the computation \(TM_2(a_2)\), and use its result as an auxiliary
computable resource during the execution of \(TM_1(a_1)\).

\begin{definition}
We define a \emph{conditioning operator}\index{Conditioning operator} as a computable function
\[
\operatorname{cond}:\mathcal D\times \mathcal D\to \mathcal D
\]
given by
\[
\operatorname{cond}(d_1,d_2)
=
\langle U_{\mathrm{cond}},\langle d_1,d_2\rangle\rangle,
\]
where \(U_{\mathrm{cond}}\) is a fixed universal Turing machine that executes the
computation encoded by \(d_1\) while using the computation encoded by \(d_2\)
as background information.
\end{definition}



\begin{definition}
We define a \emph{conditioning operator}\index{Conditioning operator} as a computable function
\[
\operatorname{cond}:\mathcal D\times \mathcal D\to \mathcal D
\]
such that, for every pair of descriptions \(d_1 = \langle TM_1, a_1 \rangle\) and
\(d_2 = \langle TM_2, a_2 \rangle\ \in \mathcal D\), we have that
\[
\operatorname{cond}(d_1,d_2) = d_3
\]
where \(d_3=\langle TM_3, \langle a_1, TM_2\rangle \rangle \in \mathcal D\).
\end{definition}


The output of a description is the representation reconstructed by that
description. This notation allows us to distinguish clearly between a
description \(d\), which is a computational object, and the representation
\(\operatorname{out}(d)\), which is the string produced by that object.

\begin{definition}[Conditioning operator]
\label{def:conditioning_operator}
A \emph{conditioning operator}\index{Conditioning operator} is a computable
function
\[
\operatorname{cond}:\mathcal D\times \mathcal D\to \mathcal D
\]
such that, for descriptions \(d=\langle TM,a\rangle\) and
\(c\in\mathcal D\), the description
\[
\operatorname{cond}(d,c)
\]
is interpreted as the description obtained by evaluating \(d\) with free access
to the background representation \(\operatorname{out}(c)\).

Equivalently, \(\operatorname{cond}(d,c)\) is a description whose computation
has the same effect, up to a fixed computable translation, as running \(TM\) on
the pair
\[
\langle a,\operatorname{out}(c)\rangle .
\]
\end{definition}

The second argument \(c\) plays a special role. It is not merged with \(d\) as
an additional component of the description. Instead, \(c\) determines the
background context relative to which \(d\) is evaluated. This is the essential
difference between conditioning and augmentation. Augmentation adds content;
conditioning reuses content that is assumed to be already available.

For this reason, when measuring the size of a conditional description, the cost
of the background description is not charged to the conditional description
itself. The relevant cost is the additional information needed once the
background has been fixed.

\begin{definition}[Conditional description of a representation]
\label{def:conditional_description}
Let \(r\in\mathcal B^\ast\) be a representation, and let
\(c\in\mathcal D\) be a background description. We say that
\(d\in\mathcal D\) is a \emph{valid description of \(r\) conditional on \(c\)}
\index{Conditional description} if
\[
\operatorname{out}(\operatorname{cond}(d,c))=r.
\]
The set of all descriptions of \(r\) conditional on \(c\) is denoted by
\[
\mathcal D_{r\mid c}
=
\left\{
d\in\mathcal D :
\operatorname{out}(\operatorname{cond}(d,c))=r
\right\}.
\]
\end{definition}

Thus, \(\mathcal D_{r\mid c}\) contains the descriptions that reconstruct
\(r\) under the assumption that the background description \(c\) is already
available. Notice that the conditioning object is now a description \(c\), not
an arbitrary string \(s\). This better matches the intended interpretation:
background knowledge is itself represented by a description.

\begin{definition}[Minimal conditional description]
\label{def:minimal_conditional_description}
Let \(r\in\mathcal B^\ast\) be a representation, and let
\(c\in\mathcal D\) be a background description. A description
\(d^\star_{r\mid c}\in\mathcal D_{r\mid c}\) is called a
\emph{minimal conditional description}\index{Minimal conditional description}
of \(r\) given \(c\) if
\[
\ell(d^\star_{r\mid c})
=
\min\{\ell(d):d\in\mathcal D_{r\mid c}\}.
\]
\end{definition}

The quantity \(\ell(d^\star_{r\mid c})\) measures the amount of additional
descriptional information required to reconstruct \(r\) once the background
description \(c\) is assumed to be available. It is the analogue, at the level
of descriptions, of conditional Kolmogorov complexity.

\begin{proposition}[Unconditional descriptions are conditional descriptions]
\label{prop:description_implies_conditional}
Let \(r\in\mathcal B^\ast\), and let \(c\in\mathcal D\) be any background
description. If \(d\in\mathcal D_r\), then there exists a description
\(\widetilde d\in\mathcal D_{r\mid c}\) such that
\[
\ell(\widetilde d)\leq \ell(d)+O(1).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since \(d\in\mathcal D_r\), the description \(d=\langle TM,a\rangle\)
satisfies \(TM(a)=r\). Construct a new machine \(TM'\) that, on input
\(\langle a,\operatorname{out}(c)\rangle\), ignores the second component and
simulates \(TM(a)\). The description
\[
\widetilde d=\langle TM',a\rangle
\]
therefore satisfies
\[
\operatorname{out}(\operatorname{cond}(\widetilde d,c))=r.
\]
The only additional cost is the fixed cost of replacing \(TM\) with a machine
that ignores the background input. Hence
\[
\ell(\widetilde d)\leq \ell(d)+O(1).
\]
\end{proof}

The converse does not hold in general. If \(d\in\mathcal D_{r\mid c}\), then
\(d\) reconstructs \(r\) only with access to the background description \(c\).
It need not reconstruct \(r\) without that background. In general,
\[
d\in\mathcal D_{r\mid c}
\quad\not\Rightarrow\quad
d\in\mathcal D_r.
\]

\begin{proposition}[Conditional descriptions are no longer than unconditional descriptions]
\label{prop:description_conditional_inequality}
Let \(r\in\mathcal B^\ast\), and let \(c\in\mathcal D\) be a background
description. Then
\[
\ell(d^\star_{r\mid c})
\leq
\ell(d^\star_r)+O(1).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Apply Proposition~\ref{prop:description_implies_conditional} to a minimal
description \(d^\star_r\in\mathcal D_r\). This yields a conditional description
\(\widetilde d\in\mathcal D_{r\mid c}\) such that
\[
\ell(\widetilde d)\leq \ell(d^\star_r)+O(1).
\]
Since \(d^\star_{r\mid c}\) is minimal in \(\mathcal D_{r\mid c}\), we obtain
\[
\ell(d^\star_{r\mid c})
\leq
\ell(\widetilde d)
\leq
\ell(d^\star_r)+O(1).
\]
\end{proof}

This proposition formalizes the intuitive idea that background knowledge cannot
make the shortest description essentially longer. At worst, the description can
ignore the background information.

\begin{proposition}[Useful conditioning may strictly reduce description length]
\label{prop:conditioning_can_reduce_length}
There exist representations \(r\in\mathcal B^\ast\) and background descriptions
\(c\in\mathcal D\) such that
\[
\ell(d^\star_{r\mid c})
<
\ell(d^\star_r)-O(1).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(s\in\mathcal B^\ast\) be a string with high Kolmogorov complexity, and
let \(c\in\mathcal D\) be a description such that \(\operatorname{out}(c)=s\).
Take \(r=s\). Without access to \(c\), any description of \(r\) must contain
essentially the information needed to reconstruct \(s\). However, conditional
on \(c\), a fixed-size description can output the background string
\(\operatorname{out}(c)\). Thus the conditional description length may be
smaller than the unconditional description length by an unbounded amount.
\end{proof}

This proposition captures the role of background knowledge in scientific
practice. A theory may become much shorter when expressed relative to a
previously established theory, a mathematical framework, or a body of empirical
knowledge.

\begin{definition}[Conditional equivalence of descriptions]
\label{def:conditional_equivalence}
Let \(c\in\mathcal D\) be a background description. Two descriptions
\(d_1,d_2\in\mathcal D\) are said to be \emph{equivalent conditional on \(c\)}
\index{Conditional equivalence} if
\[
\operatorname{out}(\operatorname{cond}(d_1,c))
=
\operatorname{out}(\operatorname{cond}(d_2,c)).
\]
We write
\[
d_1\equiv_c d_2.
\]
If the two descriptions can be transformed into one another by fixed
computable translators independent of the particular descriptions involved, we
write
\[
d_1\equiv_c^{O(1)} d_2.
\]
\end{definition}

The relation \(\equiv_c^{O(1)}\) is the appropriate notion of equality for
conditional descriptions. Two descriptions may not be identical as strings, but
they may have the same expressive content relative to a given background, up to
a constant-size change of coding.

We now relate conditioning to augmentation. Recall that augmentation is a
computable operation
\[
\operatorname{aug}:\mathcal D\times\mathcal D\to\mathcal D
\]
that combines two descriptions into a joint description. Conditioning should
distribute over augmentation when the same background knowledge is available to
each component. This is the formal counterpart of the idea that using a common
background theory after combining two descriptions is equivalent to using that
background theory in each component before combining them.

\begin{proposition}[Distributivity of conditioning over augmentation]
\label{prop:conditioning_distributes_over_augmentation}
Let \(d_1,d_2,c\in\mathcal D\). Suppose that augmentation is a structural
combination operator, in the sense that
\(\operatorname{aug}(d_1,d_2)\) reconstructs the joint output of \(d_1\) and
\(d_2\) up to a fixed computable pairing convention. Then
\[
\operatorname{cond}(\operatorname{aug}(d_1,d_2),c)
\equiv^{O(1)}
\operatorname{aug}(\operatorname{cond}(d_1,c),\operatorname{cond}(d_2,c)).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The left-hand side first forms a joint description from \(d_1\) and \(d_2\),
and then evaluates that joint description relative to the background
description \(c\). Its output is therefore the joint reconstruction obtained
from \(d_1\) and \(d_2\) with access to \(\operatorname{out}(c)\).

The right-hand side first evaluates \(d_1\) relative to \(c\), evaluates
\(d_2\) relative to \(c\), and then structurally combines the two resulting
conditional descriptions. Its output is therefore the same joint reconstruction.

Since both procedures differ only in the order in which a fixed computable
pairing and a fixed computable conditioning convention are applied, there are
constant-size translators between the two descriptions. Hence the two sides are
equivalent up to \(O(1)\).
\end{proof}

This distributive property is one of the main reasons to define conditioning as
an operation on descriptions rather than as a raw pairing of strings. It makes
conditioning behave like a change of context: the same background knowledge may
be applied before or after a structural decomposition of the description.

\begin{example}[Newtonian mechanics conditional on calculus]
Let \(d_1\) be a description of Newton's first law, let \(d_2\) be a description
of Newton's second law, and let \(c\) be a description of infinitesimal
calculus. Then
\[
\operatorname{cond}(\operatorname{aug}(d_1,d_2),c)
\]
represents the joint theory consisting of the first and second laws, expressed
under the assumption that calculus is already available. On the other hand,
\[
\operatorname{aug}(\operatorname{cond}(d_1,c),
\operatorname{cond}(d_2,c))
\]
represents the combination of the first law expressed relative to calculus and
the second law expressed relative to calculus. Proposition
\ref{prop:conditioning_distributes_over_augmentation} states that these two
ways of using the background theory are equivalent up to a constant-size
translation.
\end{example}

The distributive property should not be confused with augmentation by the
background theory itself. In general,
\[
\operatorname{cond}(d,c)
\]
is not the same as
\[
\operatorname{aug}(d,c).
\]
The former assumes \(c\) as background knowledge; the latter incorporates \(c\)
as an additional component of the description.

\begin{proposition}[Conditioning is not augmentation]
\label{prop:conditioning_not_augmentation}
In general,
\[
\operatorname{cond}(d,c)
\not\equiv
\operatorname{aug}(d,c).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The description \(\operatorname{aug}(d,c)\) reconstructs a joint object
containing the outputs of both \(d\) and \(c\). In contrast,
\(\operatorname{cond}(d,c)\) reconstructs the target output of \(d\) using
\(\operatorname{out}(c)\) only as background information. Thus the output of
\(\operatorname{aug}(d,c)\) contains the contribution of \(c\) explicitly,
whereas the output of \(\operatorname{cond}(d,c)\) need not contain it. Hence
the two operations have different semantics and are not equivalent in general.
\end{proof}

This distinction is crucial. Augmentation expands the object being described;
conditioning changes the informational context in which the description is
evaluated.

The relation between conditional descriptions and joint descriptions can now
be stated in a way that mirrors the usual inequalities for Kolmogorov
complexity.

\begin{proposition}[Conditional, unconditional, and joint descriptions]
\label{prop:description_conditional_joint}
Let \(r\in\mathcal B^\ast\), and let \(c\in\mathcal D\) be a background
description. Then
\[
\ell(d^\star_{r\mid c})
\leq
\ell(d^\star_r)+O(1)
\leq
\ell(d^\star_{r,\operatorname{out}(c)})+O(1),
\]
where \(d^\star_{r,\operatorname{out}(c)}\) denotes a minimal joint description
of the pair \(\langle r,\operatorname{out}(c)\rangle\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The first inequality is Proposition
\ref{prop:description_conditional_inequality}. For the second inequality, any
description of the joint representation
\(\langle r,\operatorname{out}(c)\rangle\) can be converted, by a fixed
computable projection, into a description of \(r\). Therefore a minimal
description of \(r\) cannot be longer than a minimal joint description of
\(\langle r,\operatorname{out}(c)\rangle\), except for the constant cost of the
projection.
\end{proof}

This proposition formalizes the basic asymmetry between conditioning and joint
description. Describing \(r\) given \(c\) may be easier than describing \(r\)
alone, while describing \(r\) together with the output of \(c\) is at least as
demanding as describing \(r\) alone, up to a constant.

Conditioning can also be iterated. In scientific practice, a model may rely on
several layers of background knowledge: for example, a physical theory may
presuppose calculus, geometry, measurement conventions, and numerical methods.

\begin{definition}[Multiple conditioning]
\label{def:multiple_conditioning}
Let \(d,c_1,\ldots,c_n\in\mathcal D\). We define the iterated conditional
description of \(d\) given \(c_1,\ldots,c_n\) recursively by
\[
\operatorname{cond}(d;c_1,\ldots,c_n)
=
\operatorname{cond}(
\operatorname{cond}(d;c_1,\ldots,c_{n-1}),c_n),
\]
with
\[
\operatorname{cond}(d;c_1)=\operatorname{cond}(d,c_1).
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Conditional descriptions with multiple backgrounds]
\label{def:conditional_description_multiple_backgrounds}
Let \(r\in\mathcal B^\ast\), and let \(c_1,\ldots,c_n\in\mathcal D\). We define
\[
\mathcal D_{r\mid c_1,\ldots,c_n}
=
\left\{
d\in\mathcal D:
\operatorname{out}(\operatorname{cond}(d;c_1,\ldots,c_n))=r
\right\}.
\]
A description
\[
d^\star_{r\mid c_1,\ldots,c_n}\in
\mathcal D_{r\mid c_1,\ldots,c_n}
\]
is called a \emph{minimal conditional description} of \(r\) given
\(c_1,\ldots,c_n\) if
\[
\ell(d^\star_{r\mid c_1,\ldots,c_n})
=
\min
\{
\ell(d):d\in\mathcal D_{r\mid c_1,\ldots,c_n}
\}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Monotonicity of conditional description length]
\label{prop:conditional_monotonicity}
Let \(r\in\mathcal B^\ast\), and let \(c_1,\ldots,c_n,c_{n+1}\in\mathcal D\).
Then
\[
\ell(d^\star_{r\mid c_1,\ldots,c_n,c_{n+1}})
\leq
\ell(d^\star_{r\mid c_1,\ldots,c_n})+O(1).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
A description conditional on \(c_1,\ldots,c_n,c_{n+1}\) may ignore the
additional background description \(c_{n+1}\). Therefore any description that
reconstructs \(r\) from \(c_1,\ldots,c_n\) can be converted, at constant cost,
into a description that reconstructs \(r\) from
\(c_1,\ldots,c_n,c_{n+1}\). Taking minimal descriptions gives the result.
\end{proof}

Thus, adding background knowledge cannot essentially increase the length of a
minimal conditional description. It may leave the length unchanged if the new
background is irrelevant, or it may reduce the length if the new background
contains information useful for reconstructing the representation.

\begin{definition}[Relevance of a background description]
\label{def:background_relevance}
Let \(r\in\mathcal B^\ast\), and let \(c\in\mathcal D\). We define the
\emph{descriptional relevance}\index{Descriptional relevance} of \(c\) for
\(r\) as
\[
\Delta(r;c)
=
\ell(d^\star_r)-\ell(d^\star_{r\mid c}).
\]
\end{definition}

The quantity \(\Delta(r;c)\) measures how much shorter the best description of
\(r\) becomes when \(c\) is available as background knowledge. If
\(\Delta(r;c)\) is large, then \(c\) contains information that is highly
relevant to the reconstruction of \(r\). If \(\Delta(r;c)=O(1)\), then \(c\)
does not significantly reduce the descriptional burden.

\begin{proposition}[Irrelevant background descriptions]
\label{prop:irrelevant_background}
Let \(r\in\mathcal B^\ast\), and let \(c\in\mathcal D\). If
\[
\ell(d^\star_{r\mid c})
=
\ell(d^\star_r)+O(1),
\]
then \(c\) is descriptionally irrelevant to \(r\) up to a constant-size coding
effect.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By Definition~\ref{def:background_relevance},
\[
\Delta(r;c)
=
\ell(d^\star_r)-\ell(d^\star_{r\mid c}).
\]
If the two lengths differ only by \(O(1)\), then
\[
\Delta(r;c)=O(1).
\]
Thus \(c\) does not provide a non-constant reduction in the shortest
description length of \(r\).
\end{proof}

This proposition gives an operational interpretation of relevance. A background
description is relevant to a target representation precisely when it reduces
the amount of additional information needed to reconstruct that representation.

Finally, we record the corresponding chain rule. It connects conditional
description length with joint description length and explains why conditioning
is the correct formal tool for separating novel information from assumed
background knowledge.

\begin{proposition}[Descriptional chain rule]
\label{prop:descriptional_chain_rule}
Let \(r\in\mathcal B^\ast\), and let \(c\in\mathcal D\). Then, up to logarithmic
or coding-dependent overheads,
\[
\ell(d^\star_{r,\operatorname{out}(c)})
=
\ell(d^\star_{\operatorname{out}(c)})
+
\ell(d^\star_{r\mid c})
+
O(1).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
A joint description of \(\langle \operatorname{out}(c),r\rangle\) may first
describe \(\operatorname{out}(c)\) and then describe \(r\) conditional on that
background. This gives the upper bound. Conversely, from any joint description
of \(\langle \operatorname{out}(c),r\rangle\), one can recover both
\(\operatorname{out}(c)\) and \(r\), and therefore separate the information
needed for the background from the additional information needed for the target
relative to that background, up to the fixed cost of the chosen coding
convention. This yields the stated equivalence.
\end{proof}

The chain rule clarifies the conceptual role of conditional descriptions.
Scientific knowledge grows not only by constructing larger joint descriptions,
but also by changing what can be assumed as background. Once a description has
become part of the accepted background, subsequent descriptions may become
shorter, more modular, and easier to compare. Conditioning therefore provides a
formal mechanism for representing cumulative scientific knowledge: new models
are not evaluated in isolation, but relative to the knowledge already available.

\section{Hypotheses}
\label{sec:hypotheses}

The theory of nescience is built upon two complementary components: representations and descriptions. A representation encodes the entity under study as a finite string of symbols, while a description provides a computable account of that representation. In scientific practice, however, these two components are not considered in isolation. What researchers actually work with is a \emph{pair} consisting of a proposed representation and a proposed description of it. We call such a pair a \emph{hypothesis}.

Intuitively, a hypothesis expresses a tentative state of scientific understanding. Its representational component specifies how the entity is being encoded, while its descriptive component specifies how that encoding is being modeled or explained. Scientific progress can then be understood as the process of replacing one hypothesis by another that yields a lower level of nescience.

\begin{definition}
\label{def:hypothesis}
We define \emph{hypothesis}\index{Hypothesis} as an ordered pair
\[
x = (r,d),
\]
where \(r \in \mathcal{R}\) is a representation and \(d \in \mathcal{D}\) is a description.
\end{definition}

The set of all hypotheses is therefore the Cartesian product of the set of representations and the set of descriptions.

\begin{definition}
\label{def:set_of_hypotheses}
We define the \emph{set of hypotheses}\index{Set of hypotheses}, denoted by \(\mathcal{H}\), as
\[
\mathcal{H} = \mathcal{R} \times \mathcal{D}.
\]
\end{definition}

Not every hypothesis is equally meaningful. In general, a description \(d\) need not describe the representation \(r\) with which it is paired. The ordered pair \((r,d)\) is therefore a very general notion, intended to include both successful and unsuccessful attempts at scientific modeling. This generality is important, since the theory of nescience is precisely concerned with measuring how far a given hypothesis is from an ideal state of understanding.

A particularly important subclass is formed by those hypotheses in which the description generates exactly the representation with which it is paired.

\begin{definition}
\label{def:exact_hypothesis}
A hypothesis \(x=(r,d)\in\mathcal{H}\) is said to be \emph{exact}\index{Exact hypothesis} if
\[
\gamma(d)=r.
\]
\end{definition}

Thus, exact hypotheses are those in which the descriptive component faithfully reconstructs the chosen representation. They correspond to the case in which the inaccuracy of the description with respect to the representation is zero.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:exact_hypothesis_equiv}
A hypothesis \(x=(r,d)\in\mathcal{H}\) is exact if and only if \(d \in \mathcal{D}_r\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By definition,
\[
\mathcal{D}_r = \{ d \in \mathcal{D} : \gamma(d)=r \}.
\]
Therefore, \((r,d)\) is exact if and only if \(\gamma(d)=r\), which is equivalent to \(d \in \mathcal{D}_r\).
\end{proof}

This proposition shows that exact hypotheses are precisely those obtained by pairing a representation with one of its descriptions.

For a fixed representation \(r\), the set of exact hypotheses associated with \(r\) is naturally determined by the set \(\mathcal{D}_r\).

\begin{definition}
\label{def:exact_hypotheses_for_representation}
Let \(r \in \mathcal{R}\). We define the set of \emph{exact hypotheses for \(r\)} by
\[
\mathcal{H}_r
=
\{ (r,d) \in \mathcal{H} : d \in \mathcal{D}_r \}.
\]
\end{definition}

Similarly, one may restrict attention to hypotheses whose representational component belongs to a given entity.

\begin{definition}
\label{def:hypotheses_for_entity}
Let \(e \in \mathcal{E}\). We define the set of \emph{hypotheses for \(e\)} by
\[
\mathcal{H}_e
=
\{ (r,d) \in \mathcal{H} : r \in \mathcal{R}_e \}.
\]
\end{definition}

This set contains all hypotheses whose representational component is associated with the entity \(e\), regardless of whether the descriptive component is exact or only approximate.

Since a hypothesis is an ordered pair, it admits natural projection maps onto its two components.

\begin{definition}
\label{def:hypothesis_projections}
For a hypothesis \(x=(r,d)\in\mathcal{H}\), we define its \emph{representation projection} and \emph{description projection} by
\[
\pi_{\mathcal{R}}(x)=r,
\qquad
\pi_{\mathcal{D}}(x)=d.
\]
\end{definition}

These projections simply recover the two components of the hypothesis.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:hypothesis_determined_by_components}
A hypothesis is uniquely determined by its representation and description projections. That is, if \(x,y\in\mathcal{H}\) satisfy
\[
\pi_{\mathcal{R}}(x)=\pi_{\mathcal{R}}(y)
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\pi_{\mathcal{D}}(x)=\pi_{\mathcal{D}}(y),
\]
then \(x=y\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(x=(r_1,d_1)\) and \(y=(r_2,d_2)\). If
\[
\pi_{\mathcal{R}}(x)=\pi_{\mathcal{R}}(y)
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\pi_{\mathcal{D}}(x)=\pi_{\mathcal{D}}(y),
\]
then \(r_1=r_2\) and \(d_1=d_2\). Hence
\[
x=(r_1,d_1)=(r_2,d_2)=y.
\]
\end{proof}

This elementary fact is useful because many operations on hypotheses act by modifying only one of the two components while leaving the other fixed.

In scientific applications, one often works with restricted families of hypotheses in which the description is chosen from a model class.

\begin{definition}
\label{def:model_hypotheses}
Let \(\mathcal{M} \subseteq \mathcal{D}\) be a model class. We define the set of \emph{hypotheses based on \(\mathcal{M}\)} by
\[
\mathcal{H}_{\mathcal{M}}
=
\mathcal{R} \times \mathcal{M}.
\]
\end{definition}

The set \(\mathcal{H}_{\mathcal{M}}\) contains all hypotheses whose descriptive component belongs to the model class \(\mathcal{M}\). Thus, restricting attention to a model class induces a corresponding restriction on the space of admissible hypotheses.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:model_hypothesis_inclusion}
Let \(\mathcal{M}_1,\mathcal{M}_2 \subseteq \mathcal{D}\) be model classes such that
\[
\mathcal{M}_1 \subseteq \mathcal{M}_2.
\]
Then
\[
\mathcal{H}_{\mathcal{M}_1} \subseteq \mathcal{H}_{\mathcal{M}_2}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If \((r,d) \in \mathcal{H}_{\mathcal{M}_1}\), then \(r \in \mathcal{R}\) and \(d \in \mathcal{M}_1\). Since \(\mathcal{M}_1 \subseteq \mathcal{M}_2\), we also have \(d \in \mathcal{M}_2\), and therefore \((r,d) \in \mathcal{H}_{\mathcal{M}_2}\).
\end{proof}

This proposition shows that enlarging the model class enlarges the corresponding hypothesis space.

In general, different hypotheses may share the same representation but differ in their descriptions, or share the same description but be paired with different representations. These two sources of variability reflect the two main degrees of freedom in scientific modeling: how the entity is represented, and how that representation is described.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:hypotheses_same_representation}
Let \(r \in \mathcal{R}\) and let \(d_1,d_2 \in \mathcal{D}\) with \(d_1 \neq d_2\). Then
\[
(r,d_1) \neq (r,d_2).
\]
Similarly, if \(r_1,r_2 \in \mathcal{R}\) with \(r_1 \neq r_2\) and \(d \in \mathcal{D}\), then
\[
(r_1,d) \neq (r_2,d).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Both statements follow immediately from the definition of ordered pair.
\end{proof}

This simple observation underlines an important point: changing either the representation or the description yields a different hypothesis. Scientific refinement may therefore proceed in two distinct ways: by changing how the entity is encoded, or by changing how that encoding is modeled.

Finally, exact hypotheses form a distinguished subset of the full hypothesis space.

\begin{definition}
\label{def:set_of_exact_hypotheses}
We define the set of \emph{exact hypotheses}\index{Set of exact hypotheses} by
\[
\mathcal{H}^{\top}
=
\{ (r,d) \in \mathcal{H} : \gamma(d)=r \}.
\]
\end{definition}

Thus, \(\mathcal{H}^{\top}\) contains precisely those hypotheses in which the descriptive component reproduces the representational component exactly. In the chapters that follow, the general concept of hypothesis will make it possible to measure the quality of scientific proposals using miscoding, inaccuracy, surplus, and, ultimately, nescience itself.

\section{Descriptions of Research Areas}
\label{sec:description_of_areas}

Research areas are subsets of the set of entities \(\mathcal{E}\). Once individual
entities admit descriptions, it is natural to ask how an entire research area can be
described. Intuitively, a description of an area should collect descriptions of the
entities that compose it in such a way that each component can be identified and,
when needed, recovered effectively. In this way, a research area is not treated as an
undifferentiated aggregate, but as a structured family of entity-level descriptions.

As in the case of representations, two difficulties appear immediately. First, a research
area need not be finite, whereas every description is a finite binary string. Second,
some entities in the area may fail to admit any exact description, or may only be
associated in practice with effective descriptions. For this reason, the description of a
research area must be understood relative to finite subsets of the area and, in practice,
relative to the effective descriptions currently available.

Recall that, for each entity \(e \in \mathcal{E}\), the set \(\mathcal{D}_e\) denotes the set of
valid descriptions associated with \(e\), and \(\hat{\mathcal{D}}_e\) denotes the set of effective
descriptions of \(e\).

\begin{definition}
Let \(\mathcal{A} \subseteq \mathcal{E}\) be a research area. We define the
\emph{describable part}\index{Describable part of a research area} of \(\mathcal{A}\) as
\[
\mathcal{A}^{\mathcal{D}}
=
\{e \in \mathcal{A} : \mathcal{D}_e \neq \varnothing\}.
\]
\end{definition}

Thus, \(\mathcal{A}^{\mathcal{D}}\) consists of those entities in the area for which at least
one valid description exists. If \(\mathcal{A}\) contains entities outside
\(\mathcal{A}^{\mathcal{D}}\), then no exact area description can include them as individually
described components.

To describe several entities together, we must separate their descriptions and attach
identifiers that allow each component to be recognized. Any standard self-delimiting
encoding of finite strings may be used for this purpose.

\begin{definition}
Let \(\mathcal{A} \subseteq \mathcal{E}\) be a research area, let
\[
F=\{e_1,\ldots,e_n\}\subseteq \mathcal{A}
\]
be a finite subset, and let \(d_i \in \mathcal{D}_{e_i}\) for each \(i=1,\ldots,n\). Let
\(\iota_1,\ldots,\iota_n \in \mathcal{B}^\ast\) be pairwise distinct identifier strings. We define a
\emph{description of the finite subarea \(F\)}\index{Description of a finite subarea} as any
finite binary string of the form
\[
\Delta_F
=
\langle (\iota_1,d_1),\ldots,(\iota_n,d_n)\rangle,
\]
where \(\langle \cdot,\cdot\rangle\) and its finite iteration denote a fixed self-delimiting
computable encoding of pairs and finite sequences of binary strings.
\end{definition}

The identifiers \(\iota_i\) need not carry scientific meaning by themselves. Their role is
only to make it possible to separate the different components of the area description
and determine which description is associated with which entity in the represented
finite family.

\begin{definition}
Let \(\mathcal{A}\subseteq \mathcal{E}\) be a research area. We say that a binary string
\(\Delta\in\mathcal{B}^\ast\) is an \emph{extended description of the area}
\index{Extended description of a research area} if there exists a finite subset
\[
F=\{e_1,\ldots,e_n\}\subseteq \mathcal{A}
\]
and strings \(d_i\in \mathcal{D}_{e_i}\) such that
\[
\Delta = \langle (\iota_1,d_1),\ldots,(\iota_n,d_n)\rangle
\]
for some pairwise distinct identifiers \(\iota_1,\ldots,\iota_n\).
\end{definition}

This definition deliberately allows area descriptions to be partial. The string
\(\Delta\) need not describe all entities in \(\mathcal{A}\), but only a finite subset of them.
This is unavoidable whenever \(\mathcal{A}\) is infinite, and it remains useful even when
\(\mathcal{A}\) is finite but some of its entities are not describable.

\begin{definition}
Let \(\mathcal{A}\subseteq \mathcal{E}\) be a research area. We say that \(\Delta\) is a
\emph{complete area description}\index{Complete area description} of \(\mathcal{A}\) if
\(\Delta\) is an area description associated with the finite set
\[
F=\mathcal{A}^{\mathcal{D}}.
\]
In particular, such a complete description can exist only if \(\mathcal{A}^{\mathcal{D}}\) is finite.
\end{definition}

Thus, if \(\mathcal{A}\) is finite and all its entities admit at least one description, then
\(\mathcal{A}\) admits a complete area description. If \(\mathcal{A}\) contains entities with no
description, then at best one can describe its describable part
\(\mathcal{A}^{\mathcal{D}}\).

The next proposition formalizes the main purpose of tagging individual descriptions:
the description of an area preserves the identity of its components.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:recover_components_area_description}
Let
\[
\Delta_F=\langle (\iota_1,d_1),\ldots,(\iota_n,d_n)\rangle
\]
be a description of a finite subarea \(F=\{e_1,\ldots,e_n\}\subseteq \mathcal{A}\). Then
each pair \((\iota_i,d_i)\) can be recovered effectively from \(\Delta_F\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By construction, \(\Delta_F\) is encoded using a fixed self-delimiting computable coding
of finite sequences of pairs. Therefore, there exists a computable decoding procedure
that parses \(\Delta_F\) and returns each pair \((\iota_i,d_i)\). Hence every component
description is effectively recoverable from the area description.
\end{proof}

The previous proposition shows that a description of a research area does not erase the
individual descriptions of its members, but rather organizes them within a single
computable object.

Area descriptions are generally not unique.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:area_descriptions_not_unique}
Descriptions of research areas are not unique.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Non-uniqueness may arise in at least two ways. First, an entity
\(e\in\mathcal{E}\) may admit several distinct descriptions \(d,d' \in \mathcal{D}_e\).
Second, even for a fixed family of entity-level descriptions, one may choose different
identifiers or a different order of concatenation. Therefore, the same research area may
admit multiple distinct area descriptions.
\end{proof}

This non-uniqueness is not a defect. It reflects the fact that a research area may be
organized in different descriptive styles, just as an individual entity may admit several
valid descriptions.

Finite area descriptions are also stable under finite aggregation.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:union_finite_area_descriptions}
Let \(F_1,F_2\subseteq \mathcal{A}\) be finite subareas with \(F_1\cap F_2=\varnothing\).
If \(\Delta_{F_1}\) and \(\Delta_{F_2}\) are descriptions of \(F_1\) and \(F_2\), respectively,
then there exists a description of \(F_1\cup F_2\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Write
\[
\Delta_{F_1}
=
\langle (\iota_1,d_1),\ldots,(\iota_m,d_m)\rangle
\]
and
\[
\Delta_{F_2}
=
\langle (\kappa_1,d'_1),\ldots,(\kappa_\ell,d'_\ell)\rangle.
\]
Choosing the identifiers so that all
\(\iota_1,\ldots,\iota_m,\kappa_1,\ldots,\kappa_\ell\) are pairwise distinct, the string
\[
\langle
(\iota_1,d_1),\ldots,(\iota_m,d_m),
(\kappa_1,d'_1),\ldots,(\kappa_\ell,d'_\ell)
\rangle
\]
is a description of the finite subarea \(F_1\cup F_2\).
\end{proof}

This result shows that finite area descriptions are closed under finite aggregation. In
this sense, describing an area amounts to collecting identifiable entity-level
descriptions into a single structured string.

It is often useful to isolate the family of all area descriptions associated with a given
research area.

\begin{definition}
Let \(\mathcal{A}\subseteq \mathcal{E}\) be a research area. We define the set of
\emph{descriptions of the area \(\mathcal{A}\)}\index{Set of descriptions of a research area}
by
\[
\mathcal{D}_{\mathcal{A}}
=
\left\{
\Delta \in \mathcal{B}^\ast :
\Delta \text{ is an extended description of } \mathcal{A}
\right\}.
\]
\end{definition}

By definition, \(\mathcal{D}_{\mathcal{A}}\) contains all finite strings that describe finite
subsets of \(\mathcal{A}\) by means of identifiable valid descriptions of their entities.

The description of an area can also be understood as the union, in a structural sense,
of the descriptions of its finite subareas.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:area_description_from_finite_subareas}
Let \(\mathcal{A}\subseteq\mathcal{E}\) be a research area. Then every element of
\(\mathcal{D}_{\mathcal{A}}\) is a description of some finite subarea \(F\subseteq \mathcal{A}\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
This follows immediately from the definition of extended description of a research
area, which requires the existence of a finite subset
\(F=\{e_1,\ldots,e_n\}\subseteq \mathcal{A}\) and corresponding descriptions
\(d_i\in\mathcal{D}_{e_i}\) such that
\[
\Delta=\langle (\iota_1,d_1),\ldots,(\iota_n,d_n)\rangle.
\]
Therefore every \(\Delta\in\mathcal{D}_{\mathcal{A}}\) is associated with some finite
subarea \(F\subseteq \mathcal{A}\).
\end{proof}

In practice, however, science does not work with the full sets \(\mathcal{D}_e\) of valid
descriptions of entities, but with the effective descriptions currently available.

\begin{definition}
Let \(\mathcal{A}\subseteq \mathcal{E}\) be a research area and let \(t\) denote a time index.
We define the \emph{effectively describable part of the area at time \(t\)}
\index{Effectively describable part of a research area} by
\[
\hat{\mathcal{A}}^{\,t}_{\mathcal{D}}
=
\{e\in \mathcal{A} : \hat{\mathcal{D}}_e^{\,t}\neq \varnothing\}.
\]
\end{definition}

The set \(\hat{\mathcal{A}}^{\,t}_{\mathcal{D}}\) contains those entities of the area for which
at least one effective description is available at time \(t\).

\begin{definition}
Let \(\mathcal{A}\subseteq \mathcal{E}\) be a research area and let
\[
F=\{e_1,\ldots,e_n\}\subseteq \hat{\mathcal{A}}^{\,t}_{\mathcal{D}}
\]
be a finite subset of effectively describable entities at time \(t\). For each
\(i=1,\ldots,n\), let \(\hat{d}_i\in \hat{\mathcal{D}}_{e_i}^{\,t}\), and let
\(\iota_1,\ldots,\iota_n\in\mathcal{B}^\ast\) be pairwise distinct identifiers. We define an
\emph{effective description of the finite known subarea \(F\) at time \(t\)}
\index{Effective description of a finite subarea} as any binary string of the form
\[
\hat{\Delta}^{\,t}_F
=
\langle (\iota_1,\hat{d}_1),\ldots,(\iota_n,\hat{d}_n)\rangle.
\]
\end{definition}

Effective area descriptions are the practical counterparts of exact area descriptions.
They need not be correct or minimal, because their components are only effective
descriptions of the individual entities. Nevertheless, they encode the current
descriptive state of the part of the area that is effectively described.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:effective_describable_area_monotone}
Let \(\mathcal{A}\subseteq \mathcal{E}\) be a fixed research area. If \(t_1<t_2\), then
\[
\hat{\mathcal{A}}^{\,t_1}_{\mathcal{D}}
\subseteq
\hat{\mathcal{A}}^{\,t_2}_{\mathcal{D}}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If \(e\in \hat{\mathcal{A}}^{\,t_1}_{\mathcal{D}}\), then \(e\in\mathcal{A}\) and
\(\hat{\mathcal{D}}_e^{\,t_1}\neq\varnothing\). Since effective descriptions are monotonic over
time, we have \(\hat{\mathcal{D}}_e^{\,t_2}\neq\varnothing\) for all \(t_2>t_1\). Therefore
\(e\in \hat{\mathcal{A}}^{\,t_2}_{\mathcal{D}}\).
\end{proof}

As a consequence, effective descriptions of areas may be progressively extended over
time by incorporating descriptions of entities that were previously not effectively
described.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:effective_area_description_extension}
Let \(\mathcal{A}\subseteq\mathcal{E}\) be a fixed research area, and let
\(\hat{\Delta}_{F_1}^{\,t_1}\) be an effective description of a finite subarea
\(F_1\subseteq \hat{\mathcal{A}}^{\,t_1}_{\mathcal{D}}\). If \(t_1<t_2\), then there exists a
finite set \(F_2\) such that
\[
F_1 \subseteq F_2 \subseteq \hat{\mathcal{A}}^{\,t_2}_{\mathcal{D}}
\]
and an effective description \(\hat{\Delta}_{F_2}^{\,t_2}\) extending
\(\hat{\Delta}_{F_1}^{\,t_1}\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By Proposition \ref{prop:effective_describable_area_monotone},
\[
\hat{\mathcal{A}}^{\,t_1}_{\mathcal{D}}
\subseteq
\hat{\mathcal{A}}^{\,t_2}_{\mathcal{D}}.
\]
Hence every entity in \(F_1\) remains effectively describable at time \(t_2\). Let
\(F_2\) be any finite subset of \(\hat{\mathcal{A}}^{\,t_2}_{\mathcal{D}}\) containing \(F_1\).
Retaining the previous effective descriptions for the entities in \(F_1\), and choosing
effective descriptions at time \(t_2\) for the entities in \(F_2\setminus F_1\), we obtain
an effective description \(\hat{\Delta}_{F_2}^{\,t_2}\) of \(F_2\) that extends
\(\hat{\Delta}_{F_1}^{\,t_1}\).
\end{proof}

This monotonicity property reflects a natural feature of scientific practice: as more
entities in an area become describable, the descriptive coverage of the area expands.

Finally, it is important to note that the description of a research area is not, in general,
a minimal description of a single entity. It is a structured object that organizes
descriptions of several entities simultaneously. For this reason, area descriptions are
best understood as a bookkeeping and modeling device that allows the theory of
nescience to be lifted from individual entities to families of related entities.

In later chapters, this lifting will make it possible to define area-level notions of
mismodel, inaccuracy, surfeit, and nescience. At that point, the description of a
research area will no longer be merely a collection of component descriptions, but the
basis for measuring how well an entire domain of inquiry is currently understood.
