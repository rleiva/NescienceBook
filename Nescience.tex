\begin{quote}
\begin{flushright}
\emph{There are known knowns. These are things we know that we know.
There are known unknowns. That is to say, there are things that we know we don't know.
But there are also unknown unknowns. There are things we don't know we don't know.} \\
Donald Rumsfeld
\end{flushright}
\end{quote}
\bigskip

Following Chapters \ref{chap:Entities}, \ref{chap:representations}, and \ref{chap:Descriptions}, which introduced the basic objects of the theory of nescience, and Chapters \ref{chap:Miscoding} and \ref{chap:Mismodel}, which developed the two fundamental sources of epistemic error, we are now in a position to introduce the central concept of this book: \emph{nescience}. Nescience measures the extent to which our research hypotheses fail to provide an adequate understanding of the entities under investigation.

Unlike Shannon entropy or Kolmogorov complexity, which quantify properties of strings or sources of information, nescience is a metric framework for quantifying ignorance. More precisely, it characterizes ignorance through two complementary components. The first is \emph{miscoding}, which measures the representational error of a hypothesis, that is, how far a candidate representation is from an adequate encoding of the entity under study. The second is \emph{mismodel}, which measures the descriptive error of a hypothesis, that is, how far a candidate description is from an adequate model of the chosen representation. Together, these two quantities capture the two main ways in which scientific understanding may fail: by representing the world poorly, or by describing those representations poorly.

These two components are generally interdependent and often conflicting. Improving the representation of an entity may require abandoning a previously successful description, thereby increasing mismodel. Conversely, although improving the description of a fixed representation does not alter its miscoding, it may reveal limitations of the current hypothesis and motivate the search for a different representation-description pair. For this reason, the theory of nescience treats science as a \emph{bi-objective optimization problem}: the aim is not to minimize representational error or descriptive error in isolation, but to reduce both simultaneously. This perspective leads naturally to the notions of Pareto optimality, current best hypothesis, and perfect knowledge developed in this chapter.

A major consequence of this framework is that it provides a systematic way to classify the domain of inquiry according to the current state of our hypotheses. Some entities belong to the region of the \emph{known knowns}, for which at least one known hypothesis already achieves negligible nescience. Others belong to the \emph{known unknowns}, for which hypotheses exist but none yet reach that standard. Finally, there remain the \emph{unknown unknowns}: entities for which no hypothesis has yet been formulated. One of the most important ambitions of the theory of nescience is to supply a formal basis for understanding this last region and, ultimately, for developing methods capable of bringing parts of it into scientific view.

The theory also suggests a more subtle and perhaps counterintuitive conclusion. Scientific progress is not guaranteed merely by the accumulation of further hypotheses. In some situations, inquiry may reach a stage at which no reduction in one component of nescience is possible without worsening the other. In such cases, research encounters a frontier beyond which improvement requires a change of representation, a change of descriptive framework, or a revision of the preference criterion used to rank hypotheses. Nescience therefore does not merely quantify ignorance; it also reveals the structural limits within which scientific progress must operate.

\section{Nescience}

Intuitively, our understanding of an entity depends on two complementary aspects of scientific work: the construction of a representation that encodes the entity, and the construction of a description that models that representation. According to the theory of nescience, there are two sources of ignorance, captured by the notions of miscoding and mismodel. Miscoding measures how far a candidate representation is from being algorithmically equivalent to an admissible representation of the entity, whereas mismodel measures how far a candidate description is from being algorithmically equivalent to a minimal description of the given representation.

From this perspective, scientific inquiry consists in jointly reducing representational and descriptive error. Although miscoding depends only on $r$, while mismodel depends on $d$ relative to $r$, the goal is to optimize both components simultaneously. Science is therefore naturally formulated as a bi-objective optimization problem.

\begin{tBox}
\textbf{The Science Problem}
\index{Science problem}
\begin{align*}
 & \text{minimize} \quad \{ \mu(r), \nu(d, r)\} \\
 & \text{subject to} \quad (r,d) \in \mathcal{B}^\ast \times \mathcal{D}
\end{align*}
\end{tBox}

The feasible region is the Cartesian product $\mathcal{B}^\ast \times \mathcal{D}$, where $\mathcal{B}^\ast$ denotes the set of finite binary strings and $\mathcal{D}$ denotes the set of descriptions. The decision vectors are hypotheses, that is, pairs $(r,d)$ consisting of a representation and a description. The objective functions to be minimized are miscoding and mismodel. The objective space is therefore a subset $\mathbf{Z} \subseteq \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}^2$. Our primary focus lies in the decision space $\mathcal{B}^\ast \times \mathcal{D}$, the space of representations and descriptions, rather than in the objective space $\mathbf{Z} \subseteq \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}^2$ of metric values.

In our formulation of science, we deliberately exclude the set $\mathcal{E}$ of entities from the optimization problem. Requiring direct access to an entity $e \in \mathcal{E}$ would render the problem ill-posed in most research contexts, since entities are not directly available to us, but only through their representations. Accordingly, science operates on strings of symbols.

From a practical perspective, scientific activity consists in discovering representations and descriptions that admit meaningful interpretations and can be used to solve concrete problems, such as explanation, optimization, or prediction. From a more theoretical perspective, the aim of science may be viewed as the construction of representation-description pairs that are consistent with the behavior of an unknown abstract oracle, which mediates the relationship between strings and entities.

% Subsection: Pareto Optimality

\subsection*{Pareto Optimality}

If the current representation and description are not perfect, the objective of scientific inquiry is to identify alternative hypotheses that reduce our ignorance. This means finding a new representation or a new description that decreases at least one of the two components of nescience, miscoding or mismodel, without increasing the other.

\begin{definition}
We say that a hypothesis $(r,d) \in \mathcal{B}^\ast \times \mathcal{D}$ \emph{dominates}\index{Dominate} another hypothesis $(r',d') \in \mathcal{B}^\ast \times D$ if
\[
\mu(r) \leq \mu(r'), \qquad \nu_r(d) \leq \nu_{r'}(d'),
\]
and at least one of these two inequalities is strict.
\end{definition}

This notion of dominance formalizes the idea of unambiguous scientific improvement. A dominating hypothesis is never worse in either component of nescience and is strictly better in at least one. Consequently, dominated hypotheses can be regarded as suboptimal and, in principle, replaceable by better alternatives.

The definition also highlights the two fundamental directions in which scientific progress may occur. On the one hand, we may improve the representation of the entity, thereby reducing miscoding, while retaining a description of comparable quality. On the other hand, we may refine the description of a given representation, thereby reducing mismodel, without degrading the representational fidelity.

In practice, there is rarely a single solution that simultaneously minimizes both miscoding and mismodel. Instead, one typically obtains a collection of mutually incomparable hypotheses that define the frontier of optimal trade-offs.

\begin{definition}
We say that a hypothesis $(r,d) \in \mathcal{B}^\ast \times \mathcal{D}$ is \emph{Pareto optimal}\index{Pareto optimal} if there does not exist another hypothesis $(r',d') \in \mathcal{B}^\ast \times \mathcal{D}$ such that $(r',d')$ dominates $(r,d)$. The set of Pareto optimal solutions, denoted by $\mathbf{P}_{\mathcal{B}^\ast \times \mathcal{D}}$, is called the \emph{Pareto frontier}\index{Pareto frontier}.
\end{definition}

In the context of scientific research, the Pareto frontier represents the set of best-achievable balances between representational and descriptive quality. Along this frontier, one cannot reduce miscoding without worsening mismodel, nor reduce mismodel without worsening miscoding. It therefore provides the natural set of candidate hypotheses from which a decision maker may later select a single preferred solution.

In some applications it may still be convenient to adopt a hypothesis that is not Pareto optimal, for example when one wishes to privilege representational fidelity over descriptive economy, or conversely. Such choices belong to the stage of decision making and scalarization, not to the structural definition of nescience itself.

% Subsection: Range of Solutions

\subsection*{Range of Solutions}

The ideal objective vector\index{Ideal vector} is the point in objective space at which all objective
functions attain their minimum possible value. For the science problem, this ideal vector is the
origin.

\begin{proposition}
The ideal objective vector for the science problem is the origin $(0,0)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By definition, both miscoding and mismodel are non-negative:
\[
\mu(r) \geq 0, \qquad \nu_r(d) \geq 0.
\]
Hence no objective vector can lie below $(0,0)$.

Moreover, the value $0$ is attainable for both objectives in the limiting ideal case.
If $r^\star$ is a minimal representation and $d^\star$ is a minimal description of $r^\star$, then
\[
\mu(r^\star)=0, \qquad \nu_{r^\star}(d^\star)=0.
\]
Therefore the ideal objective vector is $(0,0)$.
\end{proof}

A hypothesis $(r,d) \in \mathcal{B}^\ast \times D$ is said to be \emph{ideal} if it exhibits zero
miscoding and zero mismodel. Intuitively, this means that the representation is already
algorithmically equivalent to a minimal representation of the target entity, and that the
description is already algorithmically equivalent to a minimal description of that representation.
Ideal hypotheses embody the notion of perfect knowledge within the present formulation of the
theory.

Unfortunately, in most practical applications the ideal vector is not attainable. The obstruction
may lie in the representation, in the description, or in both. Some entities resist faithful
representation with finite strings; others admit only highly imperfect or highly non-minimal
descriptions within the limits of current scientific practice.

\begin{example}
A natural example is weather prediction. The entity under study is the state of the atmosphere
over a geographical region. Any finite representation of that entity is necessarily imperfect,
because measurements are noisy, incomplete, and spatially limited. Hence miscoding remains
strictly positive. In addition, even highly sophisticated numerical models fail to produce
descriptions that are both fully adequate and minimal, because the underlying dynamics are
chaotic and the models necessarily rely on approximations and auxiliary structure. Thus
mismodel also remains strictly positive. In such a case, the ideal vector $(0,0)$ is not reachable.
\end{example}

Unlike the previous three-objective formulation, the present approach does not single out a fixed
nadir vector in the absolute objective space. Since miscoding and mismodel are measured in bits,
their values are non-negative but not naturally bounded above by a universal constant.
When a bounded objective region is required for practical purposes, one may instead work with
the corresponding relative versions of miscoding and mismodel introduced in Chapters
\ref{chap:miscoding} and \ref{chap:mismodel}.

\subsection*{Trade-offs}

Because the science problem is now bi-objective, the global structure of trade-offs becomes
simpler. Every transition between two hypotheses can be analyzed in terms of how mismodel
changes relative to miscoding.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathbf{x}_1=(r_1,d_1)$ and $\mathbf{x}_2=(r_2,d_2)$ be two hypotheses such that
$\mu(r_2)\neq \mu(r_1)$. We define the \emph{nescience trade-off ratio}\index{Trade-off ratio}
between $\mathbf{x}_1$ and $\mathbf{x}_2$ as
\[
\Delta_{\nu\mu}(\mathbf{x}_1,\mathbf{x}_2)
=
\frac{\nu_{r_2}(d_2)-\nu_{r_1}(d_1)}
{\mu(r_2)-\mu(r_1)}.
\]
\end{definition}

This ratio quantifies the rate at which descriptive error changes relative to representational
error when moving from one hypothesis to another.
A positive value of $\Delta_{\nu\mu}$ indicates that miscoding and mismodel move in the same
direction, either improving together or deteriorating together. A negative value indicates a genuine
trade-off: one objective improves only at the expense of worsening the other.

In the present two-objective formulation, this single ratio replaces the more complicated chain
of trade-off quantities required in the previous three-objective setting. It provides a compact way
to study how candidate hypotheses move through the objective space and how close they come to
the Pareto frontier.

The structural notion of nescience developed in this section is therefore not a single scalar
quantity but a bi-objective characterization of ignorance through the pair
\[
\bigl(\mu(r),\nu_r(d)\bigr).
\]
A single numerical score may still be introduced later by means of a decision maker or a
scalarization criterion, but such a score should be understood as a practical selection device built
on top of the more fundamental multiobjective formulation given here.

% Subsection: Local Pareto Optimality

\subsection*{Local Pareto Optimality}

The notion of Pareto optimality introduced above is global, in the sense that it compares a
hypothesis with all other hypotheses in the search space. In practice, however, scientific inquiry
often proceeds incrementally, by modifying a current hypothesis through limited changes in its
representation, its description, or both. This motivates a localized version of Pareto optimality.

To formalize this idea, we must first specify what counts as a \emph{small} modification of a
hypothesis. In the present framework, we measure the size of a modification in algorithmic terms:
a nearby hypothesis is one that can be transformed into the current one, and conversely, by means
of short programs.

\begin{definition}
Let $(r,d) \in \mathcal{B}^\ast \times \mathcal{D}$ and let $\varepsilon > 0$.
We define the \emph{algorithmic neighborhood}\index{Algorithmic neighborhood} of $(r,d)$ as
\[
\mathcal{N}_\varepsilon(r,d)
=
\left\{
(r',d') \in \mathcal{B}^\ast \times \mathcal{D}
\;\middle|\;
K(r' \mid r) + K(r \mid r') \leq \varepsilon,
\;\;
K(d' \mid d) + K(d \mid d') \leq \varepsilon
\right\}.
\]
\end{definition}

This definition captures the idea that two hypotheses are close when both their representations and
their descriptions are mutually transformable by short programs. In practical terms, the
neighborhood $\mathcal{N}_\varepsilon(r,d)$ includes hypotheses obtained from $(r,d)$ through
small modifications such as slight reformulations of the representation, the addition or removal of a
small number of attributes, minor adjustments of parameters, or limited refinements of the
description. By contrast, hypotheses that require a substantial redesign of the representation, a
radically different descriptive framework, or a major conceptual reorganization lie outside the
neighborhood, since the algorithmic information needed to pass from one hypothesis to the other is
too large.

\begin{definition}
Let $(r,d) \in \mathcal{B}^\ast \times \mathcal{D}$ and let $\varepsilon > 0$.
We say that $(r,d)$ is \emph{locally Pareto optimal}\index{Local Pareto optimal} with respect to
$\mathcal{N}_\varepsilon(r,d)$ if there does not exist $(r',d') \in \mathcal{N}_\varepsilon(r,d)$
such that $(r',d')$ dominates $(r,d)$.
\end{definition}

A locally Pareto optimal hypothesis is therefore one that cannot be improved through small
algorithmic modifications. In this sense, local Pareto optimality expresses a form of
\emph{algorithmic stability}: within the region of hypotheses accessible from the current one by
bounded transformations, no strictly better alternative exists.

It is important to stress that a local Pareto optimum does not, in general, correspond either to a
different representation style of the same entity or to a different entity altogether. The reason is that
the notion of neighborhood is purely algorithmic. A dominating hypothesis may lie outside
$\mathcal{N}_\varepsilon(r,d)$ simply because it requires a transformation of high algorithmic
complexity, even if it concerns the same entity and uses the same representation style. Conversely,
two hypotheses concerning different entities might accidentally lie close to each other in algorithmic
terms. Thus local Pareto optimality should be interpreted as a property of the geometry of the
hypothesis space under bounded transformations, not as a direct classification of entities or
representation styles.

The next proposition makes explicit the computational meaning of local Pareto optimality.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:local_pareto_algorithmic_interpretation}
Let $(r,d) \in \mathcal{B}^\ast \times \mathcal{D}$ and let $\varepsilon > 0$. The hypothesis
$(r,d)$ is locally Pareto optimal with respect to $\mathcal{N}_\varepsilon(r,d)$ if and only if there
does not exist any hypothesis $(r',d')$ such that
\[
K(r' \mid r) + K(r \mid r') \leq \varepsilon,
\qquad
K(d' \mid d) + K(d \mid d') \leq \varepsilon,
\]
and
\[
\mu(r') \leq \mu(r), \qquad \nu_{r'}(d') \leq \nu_r(d),
\]
with at least one of these two inequalities being strict.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
This follows immediately by combining the definition of algorithmic neighborhood with the
definition of local Pareto optimality. Indeed, $(r,d)$ is locally Pareto optimal if and only if there
is no dominating hypothesis inside $\mathcal{N}_\varepsilon(r,d)$, and belonging to
$\mathcal{N}_\varepsilon(r,d)$ is equivalent to satisfying the two algorithmic bounds stated above.
\end{proof}

Proposition \ref{prop:local_pareto_algorithmic_interpretation} shows that local Pareto optimality
is a statement about the impossibility of reducing nescience through small algorithmic
transformations of the current hypothesis. In other words, if a locally Pareto optimal hypothesis is
to be improved, the improvement must come from a transformation whose algorithmic complexity
exceeds the radius $\varepsilon$ of the neighborhood.

\begin{example}
Consider a machine learning task in which a current hypothesis consists of a representation $r$
built from a fixed set of customer attributes and a description $d$ given by a logistic regression
model. Small modifications of this hypothesis include adding or removing a small number of
features, slightly changing the preprocessing pipeline, or adjusting the form of the model while
retaining the same overall descriptive framework. If no such small modification decreases either
miscoding or mismodel without worsening the other, then the hypothesis is locally Pareto optimal.
Nevertheless, a substantially different hypothesis, for example one based on a new representation
built from call transcripts and a transformer-based description, may still dominate it. Such a
hypothesis would lie outside the algorithmic neighborhood because the transformation required to
reach it is too large.
\end{example}

Global and local Pareto optimality are related in the expected way: global optimality is stronger
than local optimality.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:global_implies_local_pareto_eps}
Let $(r,d) \in \mathcal{B}^\ast \times \mathcal{D}$ and let $\varepsilon > 0$. If $(r,d)$ is Pareto
optimal in $\mathcal{B}^\ast \times \mathcal{D}$, then $(r,d)$ is locally Pareto optimal with respect
to $\mathcal{N}_\varepsilon(r,d)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Assume that $(r,d)$ is Pareto optimal in $\mathcal{B}^\ast \times \mathcal{D}$. Then there does not
exist any hypothesis $(\tilde r,\tilde d) \in \mathcal{B}^\ast \times \mathcal{D}$ that dominates
$(r,d)$. Since
\[
\mathcal{N}_\varepsilon(r,d) \subseteq \mathcal{B}^\ast \times \mathcal{D},
\]
there cannot exist any hypothesis inside $\mathcal{N}_\varepsilon(r,d)$ that dominates $(r,d)$.
Hence $(r,d)$ is locally Pareto optimal with respect to $\mathcal{N}_\varepsilon(r,d)$.
\end{proof}

The converse fails in general.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:local_not_global_pareto_eps}
Local Pareto optimality with respect to $\mathcal{N}_\varepsilon(r,d)$ does not, in general, imply
global Pareto optimality in $\mathcal{B}^\ast \times \mathcal{D}$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Consider a hypothesis $(r,d)$ and suppose there exists another hypothesis $(r^\dagger,d^\dagger)$
such that $(r^\dagger,d^\dagger)$ dominates $(r,d)$, but
\[
K(r^\dagger \mid r) + K(r \mid r^\dagger) > \varepsilon
\qquad\text{or}\qquad
K(d^\dagger \mid d) + K(d \mid d^\dagger) > \varepsilon.
\]
Then $(r^\dagger,d^\dagger) \notin \mathcal{N}_\varepsilon(r,d)$.
If, in addition, no hypothesis inside $\mathcal{N}_\varepsilon(r,d)$ dominates $(r,d)$, then
$(r,d)$ is locally Pareto optimal, yet not globally Pareto optimal, since it is dominated by
$(r^\dagger,d^\dagger)$ in the full search space.
\end{proof}

This proposition captures an important feature of scientific inquiry: a hypothesis may resist all
sufficiently small improvements and yet fail to be globally optimal. In such cases, progress
requires a transformation of higher algorithmic complexity, such as the introduction of new
variables, a substantial change of representation, or the adoption of a fundamentally different
descriptive framework.

The next proposition records a simple monotonicity property with respect to the neighborhood
radius.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:local_pareto_radius_monotonicity}
Let $0 < \varepsilon_1 \leq \varepsilon_2$. If a hypothesis $(r,d)$ is locally Pareto optimal with
respect to $\mathcal{N}_{\varepsilon_2}(r,d)$, then it is also locally Pareto optimal with respect to
$\mathcal{N}_{\varepsilon_1}(r,d)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since $\varepsilon_1 \leq \varepsilon_2$, we have
\[
\mathcal{N}_{\varepsilon_1}(r,d) \subseteq \mathcal{N}_{\varepsilon_2}(r,d).
\]
If no hypothesis in the larger neighborhood $\mathcal{N}_{\varepsilon_2}(r,d)$ dominates $(r,d)$,
then a fortiori no hypothesis in the smaller neighborhood
$\mathcal{N}_{\varepsilon_1}(r,d)$ dominates $(r,d)$. Therefore $(r,d)$ is locally Pareto optimal
with respect to $\mathcal{N}_{\varepsilon_1}(r,d)$.
\end{proof}

Proposition \ref{prop:local_pareto_radius_monotonicity} shows that local Pareto optimality
becomes more demanding as the neighborhood radius increases. A hypothesis that is locally Pareto
optimal for a large value of $\varepsilon$ is stable against a broader class of modifications than one
that is locally Pareto optimal only for a small value of $\varepsilon$.

Finally, observe that the notion of local Pareto optimality is primarily useful for understanding the
dynamics of scientific search, rather than for defining the ultimate standard of optimality.
Global Pareto optimality identifies the truly nondominated hypotheses in the full search space,
whereas local Pareto optimality captures the possibility that inquiry may become trapped in regions
of the hypothesis space where no small improvement is available. This makes the concept valuable
for analyzing scientific stagnation, incremental progress, and the need for discontinuous conceptual
shifts.





\section{Entity-Constrained Nescience}
\label{sec:entity_constrained_nescience}

The formulation given in the previous section is intentionally maximally general: it treats science as
a search over the full space $\mathcal{B}^\ast \times \mathcal{D}$ of representations and descriptions.
That level of generality is useful when studying the global geometry of the hypothesis space, in
particular when introducing local Pareto optimality and the possibility of algorithmic traps.
In many applications, however, the object of inquiry is not an arbitrary hypothesis, but a fixed
entity $e \in \mathcal{E}$ whose representations are already known, or at least approximately
delimited. In such cases, it is natural to restrict the feasible region to the hypotheses that concern
that entity.

This leads to the following entity-constrained version of the science problem.

\begin{tBox}
\textbf{The Entity-Constrained Science Problem}
\index{Entity-constrained science problem}
\begin{align*}
 & \text{minimize} \quad \{ \mu(r), \nu_r(d)\} \\
 & \text{subject to} \quad (r,d) \in \mathcal{R}_e \times \mathcal{D}
\end{align*}
\end{tBox}

The objective functions are unchanged: miscoding still measures representational error, and
mismodel still measures descriptive error. What changes is only the feasible region. Instead of
searching over all finite binary strings, we now restrict attention to representations of a fixed entity
$e$. This restriction has an important conceptual effect: all hypotheses in the feasible region are
about the same entity, so Pareto comparisons become semantically homogeneous. In particular,
entity-constrained nescience avoids the difficulty, already noted above, that a globally dominating
hypothesis may concern a different entity and therefore be irrelevant from the standpoint of the
investigation at hand.

\subsection*{Entity-Constrained Pareto Optimality}

The dominance relation is the same as before, but it is now interpreted relative to the restricted
feasible region.

\begin{definition}
Let $e \in \mathcal{E}$. We say that a hypothesis $(r,d) \in \mathcal{R}_e \times \mathcal{D}$
\emph{dominates for the entity $e$}\index{Dominate!for the entity $e$} another hypothesis
$(r',d') \in \mathcal{R}_e \times \mathcal{D}$ if
\[
\mu(r) \leq \mu(r'), \qquad \nu_r(d) \leq \nu_{r'}(d'),
\]
and at least one of these two inequalities is strict.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
Let $e \in \mathcal{E}$. We say that a hypothesis $(r,d) \in \mathcal{R}_e \times \mathcal{D}$ is
\emph{Pareto optimal for the entity $e$}\index{Pareto optimal!for the entity $e$} if there does not
exist another hypothesis $(r',d') \in \mathcal{R}_e \times \mathcal{D}$ such that $(r',d')$
dominates $(r,d)$. The corresponding set of Pareto optimal hypotheses is denoted by
\[
\mathbf{P}_e := \mathbf{P}_{\mathcal{R}_e \times \mathcal{D}},
\]
and is called the \emph{entity-constrained Pareto frontier}\index{Entity-constrained Pareto frontier}.
\end{definition}

The frontier $\mathbf{P}_e$ represents the set of best-achievable trade-offs between miscoding and
mismodel among hypotheses concerning the same entity. This is often the most relevant notion in
scientific practice, since researchers usually compare competing hypotheses about one target system,
one phenomenon, or one topic at a time.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:global_pareto_implies_entity_pareto}
Let $e \in \mathcal{E}$ and let $(r,d) \in \mathcal{R}_e \times \mathcal{D}$. If $(r,d)$ is Pareto
optimal in the global sense, that is, in $\mathcal{B}^\ast \times \mathcal{D}$, then $(r,d)$ is Pareto
optimal for the entity $e$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If $(r,d)$ is Pareto optimal in $\mathcal{B}^\ast \times \mathcal{D}$, then no hypothesis in the full
search space dominates it. Since
\[
\mathcal{R}_e \times \mathcal{D} \subseteq \mathcal{B}^\ast \times \mathcal{D},
\]
it follows a fortiori that no hypothesis in the smaller set $\mathcal{R}_e \times \mathcal{D}$
dominates $(r,d)$. Hence $(r,d)$ is Pareto optimal for the entity $e$.
\end{proof}

The converse fails in general.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:entity_pareto_not_global_pareto}
Pareto optimality for a fixed entity does not, in general, imply global Pareto optimality.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let $(r,d) \in \mathcal{R}_e \times \mathcal{D}$ be Pareto optimal for the entity $e$. It may happen
that there exists another hypothesis $(\tilde r,\tilde d) \in \mathcal{B}^\ast \times \mathcal{D}$ such
that $(\tilde r,\tilde d)$ dominates $(r,d)$, but $\tilde r \notin \mathcal{R}_e$. In that case,
$(\tilde r,\tilde d)$ is irrelevant for the entity-constrained problem but relevant for the global one.
Therefore $(r,d)$ is Pareto optimal in $\mathcal{R}_e \times \mathcal{D}$, yet not Pareto optimal in
$\mathcal{B}^\ast \times \mathcal{D}$.
\end{proof}

Proposition \ref{prop:entity_pareto_not_global_pareto} makes clear why the entity-constrained
formulation is often preferable in applications. A hypothesis can be scientifically optimal relative to
a given entity even if, from the global point of view, it is dominated by hypotheses concerning other
entities.

The first and most important reduction arises when the entity under investigation is fixed.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:entity_restriction_search}
Let $e \in \mathcal{E}$ be an entity. If the objective of inquiry is to improve a hypothesis about
$e$, then it is sufficient to restrict the search space from $\mathcal{B}^\ast \times \mathcal{D}$ to
\[
\mathcal{R}_e \times \mathcal{D}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By definition, $\mathcal{R}_e$ is the set of representations of the entity $e$. Any hypothesis
$(r,d)$ such that $r \notin \mathcal{R}_e$ does not concern $e$ and is therefore irrelevant to the
problem of improving our knowledge of $e$. Hence, when the target entity is fixed, all relevant
hypotheses lie in $\mathcal{R}_e \times \mathcal{D}$.
\end{proof}

Proposition \ref{prop:entity_restriction_search} is conceptually important because it transforms a
global search problem into a topic-specific one. In practice, this restriction is often the natural
starting point of scientific inquiry.


\subsection*{Range of Solutions}

The restriction of the feasible region does not alter the ideal objective vector.

\begin{proposition}
The ideal objective vector for the entity-constrained science problem is also the origin $(0,0)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The proof is the same as in the global case. Both miscoding and mismodel are non-negative, so no
objective vector can lie below $(0,0)$. Moreover, if $r^\star \in \mathcal{R}_e$ is a minimal
representation of $e$ and $d^\star$ is a minimal description of $r^\star$, then
\[
\mu(r^\star)=0, \qquad \nu_{r^\star}(d^\star)=0.
\]
Hence the ideal objective vector is $(0,0)$.
\end{proof}

Thus, ideal and negligible perfect knowledge admit an entity-constrained reading without any
change in their formal content. What changes is only the scope of comparison: instead of asking
whether a hypothesis is globally nondominated among all possible hypotheses, we ask whether it is
nondominated among hypotheses concerning the entity $e$.

\subsection*{Trade-offs}

The trade-off ratio introduced in the previous section also specializes immediately to the
entity-constrained setting. If
\[
\mathbf{x}_1=(r_1,d_1), \qquad \mathbf{x}_2=(r_2,d_2)
\]
are two hypotheses in $\mathcal{R}_e \times \mathcal{D}$ with $\mu(r_2)\neq \mu(r_1)$, then
\[
\Delta_{\nu\mu}(\mathbf{x}_1,\mathbf{x}_2)
=
\frac{\nu_{r_2}(d_2)-\nu_{r_1}(d_1)}
{\mu(r_2)-\mu(r_1)}
\]
measures the rate at which mismodel changes relative to miscoding while remaining within the
space of hypotheses for the entity $e$. In this setting, the ratio has a more direct interpretation
than in the global problem, since both hypotheses concern the same target and the trade-off is
therefore internal to a single scientific topic.

\subsection*{Relation with Local Pareto Optimality}

The entity-constrained problem and the locally constrained problem play different roles.
Entity-constrained nescience restricts the search space semantically, by fixing the entity under
investigation. Local Pareto optimality, by contrast, restricts the search space geometrically, by
limiting attention to hypotheses that are algorithmically close to the current one. The two notions
therefore address different questions:
\begin{itemize}
\item entity-constrained Pareto optimality asks which hypotheses are best among those concerning
a fixed entity;
\item local Pareto optimality asks whether a hypothesis can be improved by means of small
algorithmic modifications.
\end{itemize}

In practical applications, the entity-constrained formulation is often the more natural one, since it
matches the ordinary scientific situation in which a researcher studies one entity at a time. By
contrast, the generic formulation over $\mathcal{B}^\ast \times \mathcal{D}$, together with local
Pareto optimality, is better suited to the analysis of search dynamics, stagnation, and the need for
discontinuous conceptual shifts.

\subsection*{Methodological Significance}

The main advantage of entity-constrained nescience is that it isolates the genuinely relevant
hypotheses for a given investigation. Once the target entity is fixed, the resulting Pareto frontier
$\mathbf{P}_e$ provides a clearer standard of comparison than the global frontier, since it excludes
domination by hypotheses that concern other entities. For this reason, entity-constrained nescience
should be regarded as the natural formulation for topic-specific scientific analysis, whereas the
global version remains the more general formulation for the foundations of the theory.

\section{Minimizing Nescience}
\label{sec:minimizing_nescience}

{\color{red} 
A \emph{scientific method}\index{Scientific method} (see Appendix \ref{chap:Philosophy}) refers to any algorithm or computable procedure capable of
solving, or closely approximating a solution to, the above minimization problem.
This includes a broad class of techniques aimed at systematically reducing miscoding and
mismodel. In doing so, scientific methods improve both the quality of our representations and
the quality of the descriptions built upon them.
}

From a mathematical perspective, any hypothesis in the Pareto frontier is a valid solution to the
Science Problem. In this formal sense, the problem is solved once the full set of Pareto optimal
hypotheses has been identified. In scientific practice, however, this is often not sufficient. Researchers
usually seek a single hypothesis that is most appropriate for the specific goals, constraints, and
standards of the investigation.

In multi-objective optimization, a \emph{decision maker} (see Section
\ref{sec:multiobjective_optimization}) is an entity, either a human agent, a set of explicit criteria,
or an algorithm, that selects one hypothesis from the Pareto frontier. The role of the decision maker
is to introduce a preference relation on the set of feasible hypotheses, thereby transforming the
multi-objective problem into a single-objective selection problem.

Within the present formulation of the theory of nescience, the decision maker evaluates hypotheses
through their miscoding and mismodel. This reflects the fact that ignorance now has two primary
components: representational error and descriptive error.

\begin{definition}
Let $(r,d) \in \mathcal{B}^\ast \times D$ be a hypothesis. A \emph{decision maker}\index{Science decision maker}
for the science problem is a function
\[
V : \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}^2 \to \mathbb{R}
\]
that assigns to each pair $\bigl(\mu(r),\nu_r(d)\bigr)$ the real value
\[
V\bigl(\mu(r),\nu_r(d)\bigr).
\]
\end{definition}

The function $V$ is often called a \emph{value function}\index{Value function} or
\emph{utility function}\index{Utility function}. It encodes the preferences of the scientist or the
research community. Depending on the application, $V$ may treat miscoding and mismodel
symmetrically, may privilege one of them, or may penalize imbalances between them.

This formalism enables the selection of a single hypothesis from the Pareto frontier. It also makes
explicit that the final choice of a hypothesis is not determined by the structural theory alone, but
also by the practical aims of the investigation.

A basic requirement for a reasonable decision maker is monotonicity: if one hypothesis is no worse
in either objective and strictly better in at least one, then it should receive a strictly better score.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:strictly_increasing_dm_pareto}
Let $V : \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$ be strictly increasing in each coordinate.
If $(r^\star,d^\star)$ is a global minimizer of
\[
V\bigl(\mu(r),\nu_r(d)\bigr)
\]
over the feasible region $\mathcal{B}^\ast \times D$, then $(r^\star,d^\star)$ is Pareto optimal.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Assume, for contradiction, that $(r^\star,d^\star)$ is not Pareto optimal. Then there exists
$(r',d') \in \mathcal{B}^\ast \times D$ such that
\[
\mu(r') \leq \mu(r^\star), \qquad \nu_{r'}(d') \leq \nu_{r^\star}(d^\star),
\]
with at least one strict inequality. Since $V$ is strictly increasing in each coordinate, it follows that
\[
V\bigl(\mu(r'),\nu_{r'}(d')\bigr) < V\bigl(\mu(r^\star),\nu_{r^\star}(d^\star)\bigr),
\]
contradicting the optimality of $(r^\star,d^\star)$. Therefore $(r^\star,d^\star)$ must be Pareto
optimal.
\end{proof}

This proposition is useful because it unifies many practical optimization methods under a single
principle: any scalarization that is strictly increasing in both miscoding and mismodel can only
select Pareto optimal hypotheses.

\subsection{Global Criterion}

The global criterion method (see Section \ref{sub:multiobjective_global_criterion}) selects a
hypothesis by minimizing the distance from its objective vector to the ideal vector. In the present
case, the ideal vector is the origin $(0,0)$.

A natural version of the global criterion is obtained by using the Euclidean distance:
\begin{align*}
& \text{minimize} \quad \sqrt{\mu(r)^2 + \nu_r(d)^2} \\
& \text{subject to} \quad (r,d) \in \mathcal{B}^\ast \times D
\end{align*}

This scalarization is geometrically intuitive: it chooses the hypothesis whose pair
$\bigl(\mu(r),\nu_r(d)\bigr)$ is closest to perfect knowledge. It treats both objectives symmetrically
and penalizes hypotheses that are far from the ideal point in either coordinate.

More generally, one may use an $L_p$ distance to the ideal vector:
\begin{align*}
& \text{minimize} \quad \left( \mu(r)^p + \nu_r(d)^p \right)^{1/p} \\
& \text{subject to} \quad (r,d) \in \mathcal{B}^\ast \times D,
\qquad 1 \leq p < \infty
\end{align*}
or, in the limit $p \to \infty$,
\begin{align*}
& \text{minimize} \quad \max\{\mu(r),\nu_r(d)\} \\
& \text{subject to} \quad (r,d) \in \mathcal{B}^\ast \times D.
\end{align*}

The case $p=\infty$ is particularly interesting in the theory of nescience, because it directly
penalizes imbalance and selects hypotheses that control the worst of the two errors.

\begin{example}
Consider the following three Pareto optimal objective vectors:
\[
A=(1,7), \qquad B=(7,1), \qquad C=(4,4).
\]
A Euclidean global criterion selects $C$, since
\[
\sqrt{1^2+7^2}=\sqrt{50}, \qquad \sqrt{7^2+1^2}=\sqrt{50}, \qquad \sqrt{4^2+4^2}=\sqrt{32}.
\]
This illustrates the main feature of the global criterion: it favors hypotheses that achieve a balanced
reduction of both miscoding and mismodel, even when the more extreme points are also Pareto
optimal.
\end{example}

The main drawback of the global criterion is that it does not recover all Pareto optimal hypotheses.
In particular, it tends to favor centrally located trade-offs and may exclude hypotheses that are
highly desirable because they are exceptionally good in one dimension.

In the previous three-objective formulation it was possible to state that normalization was unnecessary
because the objectives were assumed to lie in $[0,1]$. In the present framework that statement is no
longer appropriate in general. Both miscoding and mismodel are absolute quantities measured in
bits, and they need not share the same numerical scale for a given class of problems. Consequently,
when a global criterion is used in practice, it is often advisable either to normalize the objectives
explicitly or to replace them by their relative versions:
\[
\mu^{\mathrm{rel}}(r), \qquad \nu_r^{\mathrm{rel}}(d).
\]

\subsection{Weighted Sum Method}

The weighting method is the most classical way to incorporate preferences into a bi-objective
problem. The idea is to assign a nonnegative weight to each objective and minimize the resulting
weighted sum.

\begin{align*}
& \text{minimize} \quad w_\mu\,\mu(r) + w_\nu\,\nu_r(d) \\
& \text{subject to} \quad (r,d) \in \mathcal{B}^\ast \times D
\end{align*}
where
\[
w_\mu \geq 0, \qquad w_\nu \geq 0, \qquad w_\mu + w_\nu = 1.
\]

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:weighted_sum_pareto}
If $w_\mu > 0$ and $w_\nu > 0$, then every global minimizer of the weighted-sum problem is
Pareto optimal.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The scalarization
\[
V(x,y)=w_\mu x+w_\nu y
\]
is strictly increasing in each coordinate whenever $w_\mu>0$ and $w_\nu>0$. The conclusion
therefore follows from Proposition \ref{prop:strictly_increasing_dm_pareto}.
\end{proof}

The weighted sum is simple, interpretable, and often convenient computationally. It is especially
appropriate when the decision maker has a clear view of the acceptable trade-off between
representational and descriptive error.

However, the weights should not be interpreted as absolute measures of importance. A more precise
interpretation is that they quantify the rate at which the decision maker is willing to trade one
objective against the other. A larger value of $w_\mu$ indicates a lower tolerance for miscoding;
a larger value of $w_\nu$ indicates a lower tolerance for mismodel.

\begin{example}
Suppose a scientist is comparing candidate hypotheses for a newly studied biological mechanism.
If the main goal is to obtain a highly faithful representation of the underlying phenomenon before
refining the explanatory model, a choice such as
\[
w_\mu=0.8, \qquad w_\nu=0.2
\]
expresses a willingness to tolerate a larger mismodel provided the miscoding is significantly reduced.
Conversely, if the available representation is already trusted and the main concern is descriptive
quality, the scientist may prefer
\[
w_\mu=0.2, \qquad w_\nu=0.8.
\]
\end{example}

The main theoretical limitation of the weighted-sum method is that, in nonconvex problems, it
does not recover all Pareto optimal hypotheses. Since the science problem is discrete and typically
nonconvex, this limitation is especially relevant here.

\subsection{$\varepsilon$-Constraint Method}

A particularly useful alternative in practice is the \emph{$\varepsilon$-constraint method}\index{$\varepsilon$-constraint method}.
Instead of combining the two objectives into a single weighted sum, one objective is minimized while
the other is converted into an explicit constraint.

For example, one may minimize mismodel under a prescribed representational tolerance:
\begin{align*}
& \text{minimize} \quad \nu_r(d) \\
& \text{subject to} \quad (r,d) \in \mathcal{B}^\ast \times D, \\
& \hspace{2.25cm} \mu(r) \leq \varepsilon_\mu.
\end{align*}

Dually, one may minimize miscoding under a descriptive tolerance:
\begin{align*}
& \text{minimize} \quad \mu(r) \\
& \text{subject to} \quad (r,d) \in \mathcal{B}^\ast \times D, \\
& \hspace{2.25cm} \nu_r(d) \leq \varepsilon_\nu.
\end{align*}

This method is particularly natural in scientific applications. Very often one of the two dimensions
is not simply a preference but a hard admissibility requirement. For example, one may demand that
the representation be sufficiently reliable before accepting any description built from it, or one may
impose a maximal acceptable mismodel and then search for the least miscoded representation under
that requirement.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:epsilon_constraint_pareto}
Assume that the feasible set
\[
\{(r,d)\in \mathcal{B}^\ast \times D : \mu(r)\leq \varepsilon_\mu\}
\]
is nonempty. Then every global minimizer of
\[
\nu_r(d)
\]
over that set is weakly Pareto optimal. If, in addition, the minimizer is unique in the objective
space, then it is Pareto optimal.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If a feasible hypothesis $(r^\star,d^\star)$ were strictly improved in both coordinates by another
feasible hypothesis $(r',d')$, then in particular
\[
\nu_{r'}(d') < \nu_{r^\star}(d^\star),
\]
contradicting the optimality of $(r^\star,d^\star)$ for the constrained problem. Hence
$(r^\star,d^\star)$ is weakly Pareto optimal.

If the objective vector is unique and there existed a feasible hypothesis that dominated
$(r^\star,d^\star)$, then it would have to improve one coordinate without worsening the other.
Because the constraint already controls $\mu$, such a feasible dominating point would necessarily
have
\[
\nu_{r'}(d') \leq \nu_{r^\star}(d^\star),
\]
with strict improvement in at least one coordinate, contradicting uniqueness in the objective space.
Therefore the solution is Pareto optimal.
\end{proof}

The main practical advantage of the $\varepsilon$-constraint method is that it expresses scientific
standards in a direct and transparent way. Rather than asking how much one objective should be
weighted, it asks how much of that objective is acceptable.

\subsection{Weighted Tchebycheff Method}

Another method of particular interest is the \emph{weighted Tchebycheff method}\index{Weighted Tchebycheff method}.
Given positive weights $w_\mu,w_\nu$ and the ideal vector $(0,0)$, it solves
\begin{align*}
& \text{minimize} \quad \max\{w_\mu\,\mu(r),\, w_\nu\,\nu_r(d)\} \\
& \text{subject to} \quad (r,d) \in \mathcal{B}^\ast \times D.
\end{align*}

This method differs from the weighted sum in an important way: it focuses on the worst weighted
deviation from the ideal point. As a consequence, it strongly discourages highly unbalanced
solutions.

The weighted Tchebycheff method is especially appropriate when one wishes to avoid hypotheses
that are excellent in one dimension but very poor in the other. It is also useful because, unlike the
weighted sum, it is often able to recover Pareto optimal solutions that lie on nonconvex parts of the
frontier.

In practice, one may also use the augmented Tchebycheff scalarization
\[
\max\{w_\mu\,\mu(r),\, w_\nu\,\nu_r(d)\}
+
\rho\bigl(w_\mu\,\mu(r)+w_\nu\,\nu_r(d)\bigr),
\qquad \rho>0,
\]
which breaks ties between points having the same worst weighted deviation and prevents certain
pathological weakly Pareto optimal selections.

\subsection{Lexicographic Method}

A limiting case of preference-based selection is the \emph{lexicographic method}\index{Lexicographic method}.
Here the decision maker declares one objective to have absolute priority over the other.

For example, if miscoding is given priority, the selection proceeds as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item minimize $\mu(r)$ over all feasible hypotheses;
\item among the hypotheses attaining the minimum miscoding, minimize $\nu_r(d)$.
\end{enumerate}

The reverse order is also possible.

The lexicographic method is relevant when the scientific context imposes a strict hierarchy between
representation and description. For example, in some experimental settings one may insist that no
description is scientifically acceptable unless the representation first satisfies the strongest available
reliability criterion.

However, this method is usually too rigid to serve as a default decision rule for the theory of
nescience. It can force highly unbalanced choices and may ignore substantial improvements in the
secondary objective. It is therefore best understood as a special-purpose selection rule rather than
as a general prescription.

\subsection{Other Useful Properties}

Because miscoding and mismodel are commensurable, both are measured in bits, additive
scalarizations such as weighted sums have a natural interpretation in the theory of nescience.
Nevertheless, commensurability does not eliminate the practical issue of scale. If one objective
systematically takes much larger values than the other on a given class of problems, then naive
scalarizations may be dominated by the larger-scale objective. For this reason, the use of relative
versions or explicit normalization remains important in applications.

A second useful observation is that the choice of optimization method reflects a substantive
epistemic attitude:
\begin{itemize}
\item the global criterion favors balanced proximity to perfect knowledge;
\item the weighted sum expresses smooth trade-offs;
\item the $\varepsilon$-constraint method expresses hard scientific standards;
\item the weighted Tchebycheff method protects against severe imbalance;
\item the lexicographic method enforces absolute priority.
\end{itemize}

Thus, minimizing nescience is not merely a technical exercise. The selection of a decision maker is
itself part of the scientific methodology, because it expresses what counts as an acceptable balance
between representational fidelity and descriptive adequacy.

For this reason, no single decision maker should be regarded as universally optimal. Different
research contexts may legitimately require different selection principles. The structural theory of
nescience identifies the Pareto frontier; the final choice of a single hypothesis must be made by a
decision rule adapted to the goals of the inquiry.

\section{Perfect Knowledge}
\label{sec:perfect_knowledge}

According to the theory of nescience, the fundamental aim of scientific research is to reduce our
ignorance concerning the entities under investigation. In the present formulation, this ignorance is
captured by two quantities: the miscoding of the representation and the mismodel of the
description. Miscoding measures the representational error of the hypothesis, whereas mismodel
measures its descriptive error. Perfect knowledge is therefore naturally understood as the limit in
which both sources of ignorance vanish, or cannot be further improved without sacrifice.

This viewpoint leads to two complementary notions of perfect knowledge. The first is an ideal,
theoretical notion, in which both miscoding and mismodel are exactly zero. The second is a
practical notion, based on Pareto optimality, in which no further decrease in one of the two
components is possible without increasing the other. The former captures the endpoint of inquiry in
an absolute sense; the latter captures the best attainable state within a given symbolic and
methodological framework.

\subsection*{Ideal Perfect Knowledge}

We begin with the ideal notion.

\begin{definition}
Let $(r,d) \in \mathcal{B}^\ast \times D$ be a hypothesis. We say that $(r,d)$ attains
\emph{ideal perfect knowledge}\index{Ideal perfect knowledge} if
\[
\mu(r)=0
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\nu_r(d)=0.
\]
\end{definition}

Thus, ideal perfect knowledge is attained when the representation exhibits zero miscoding and the
description exhibits zero mismodel. Intuitively, this means that the representation is already
optimal with respect to the target entity, and that the description is already optimal with respect to
that representation.

It is important to emphasize that this definition is internal to the formal framework. It does not
presuppose direct access to the underlying entity. The correspondence between representations and
entities remains mediated by the reconstruction oracle, and therefore ideal perfect knowledge should
be understood as perfect knowledge of a hypothesis within a representational framework, not as
direct metaphysical access to the entity independently of that framework.

Because Kolmogorov-based quantities are defined only up to additive constants, the exact equalities
appearing in the previous definition should be regarded as an ideal limiting case. For many
theoretical purposes, a more robust notion is obtained by allowing constant-size discrepancies.

\begin{definition}
Let $(r,d) \in \mathcal{B}^\ast \times D$ be a hypothesis. We say that $(r,d)$ attains
\emph{negligible perfect knowledge}\index{Negligible perfect knowledge} if
\[
\mu(r)=O(1)
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\nu_r(d)=O(1).
\]
\end{definition}

Negligible perfect knowledge expresses that both the representational and the descriptive defects
are bounded by a constant amount of algorithmic information. This is often the more stable notion
from the standpoint of algorithmic information theory.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:minimal_rep_minimal_desc_negligible_pk}
Let $e \in E$ be an entity, let $r \in R_e^\star$ be a minimal representation of $e$, and let
$d \in D_r^\star$ be a minimal description of $r$. Then the hypothesis $(r,d)$ attains negligible
perfect knowledge.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since $r \in R_e^\star$, the representation $r$ is minimal for $e$. Hence its representational defect
is negligible, that is,
\[
\mu(r)=O(1).
\]
Likewise, since $d \in D_r^\star$, the description $d$ is minimal for the target representation $r$.
Therefore its descriptive defect is also negligible:
\[
\nu_r(d)=O(1).
\]
Consequently,
\[
\mu(r)=O(1)
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\nu_r(d)=O(1),
\]
so $(r,d)$ attains negligible perfect knowledge.
\end{proof}

This proposition identifies the structural content of perfect knowledge in the present theory:
minimal representations together with minimal descriptions yield vanishing nescience up to the
usual constant-size ambiguity.

\subsection*{Pareto Perfect Knowledge}

In practice, ideal perfect knowledge is often unattainable. Even when a hypothesis cannot be made
perfect in the strict sense, it may still be impossible to improve it in one component of nescience
without worsening the other. This motivates the practical notion of Pareto perfect knowledge.

\begin{definition}
Let $(r,d) \in \mathcal{B}^\ast \times D$ be a hypothesis. We say that $(r,d)$ attains
\emph{Pareto perfect knowledge}\index{Pareto perfect knowledge} if $(r,d)$ is Pareto optimal with
respect to the two objectives
\[
\mu(r)
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\nu_r(d).
\]
\end{definition}

Pareto perfect knowledge does not mean that ignorance has been reduced to zero. It means only
that no further improvement in miscoding can be achieved without worsening mismodel, and no
further improvement in mismodel can be achieved without worsening miscoding. In this sense,
Pareto perfect knowledge marks the boundary of what can be improved within the current symbolic
framework.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:ideal_implies_pareto_perfect}
Every hypothesis that attains ideal perfect knowledge also attains Pareto perfect knowledge.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let $(r,d)$ be a hypothesis such that
\[
\mu(r)=0
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\nu_r(d)=0.
\]
Since both miscoding and mismodel are nonnegative, there is no other hypothesis
$(r',d') \in \mathcal{B}^\ast \times D$ such that
\[
\mu(r') \leq \mu(r), \qquad \nu_{r'}(d') \leq \nu_r(d),
\]
with at least one strict inequality. Hence no hypothesis dominates $(r,d)$, and therefore
$(r,d)$ is Pareto optimal.
\end{proof}

The converse does not hold in general.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:pareto_not_ideal_perfect}
Pareto perfect knowledge does not, in general, imply ideal perfect knowledge.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Consider a hypothesis $(r,d)$ lying on the Pareto frontier such that
\[
\mu(r)>0
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\nu_r(d)>0.
\]
Such a situation occurs whenever any attempt to reduce miscoding necessarily increases mismodel,
or any attempt to reduce mismodel necessarily increases miscoding. Then $(r,d)$ is Pareto optimal,
hence it attains Pareto perfect knowledge, but it does not attain ideal perfect knowledge because
neither objective is zero.
\end{proof}

This distinction is important. Ideal perfect knowledge corresponds to complete success in both
dimensions of nescience, whereas Pareto perfect knowledge corresponds only to the best attainable
trade-off. The latter is often the practically relevant notion.

\subsection*{Perfect Knowledge as a Stopping Criterion}

The concept of Pareto perfect knowledge provides a natural stopping criterion for inquiry. When a
hypothesis becomes Pareto optimal, further research can no longer improve one of the two sources
of ignorance without worsening the other. At that point, continued inquiry is justified only if one is
willing to revise the decision rule, alter the representation style, enlarge the class of admissible
descriptions, or accept a different trade-off structure.

\begin{example}
In weather prediction, the target entity is the evolving state of the atmosphere over a geographical
region. Any finite representation of this entity is necessarily imperfect due to measurement noise,
limited spatial resolution, and incomplete coverage. Thus miscoding remains strictly positive.
Moreover, even the best available numerical descriptions rely on approximations, discretizations,
and auxiliary assumptions, so mismodel also remains strictly positive. In such a case, ideal perfect
knowledge is unattainable. Nevertheless, one may still reach Pareto perfect knowledge when no
further reduction of miscoding is possible without worsening mismodel, and vice versa.
\end{example}

\subsection*{A Remark on Randomness}

In the previous formulation of nescience, the connection between perfect knowledge and
randomness followed from the requirement that surfeit be zero. In the present bi-objective
formulation, that conclusion is no longer immediate from perfect knowledge alone. What perfect
knowledge now requires is zero mismodel, that is, optimality of the description with respect to the
target representation, not necessarily incompressibility of the description as a finite string.

Accordingly, perfect knowledge should now be interpreted primarily as \emph{algorithmic
optimality relative to a representational framework}, rather than as randomness in the strict sense.
If one additionally imposes a zero-surplus condition on the description, then the earlier link with
incompressibility may be recovered. But this stronger conclusion no longer follows from the
definition of nescience itself.

\subsection*{Summary}

The present formulation therefore distinguishes three increasingly practical levels:
\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{ideal perfect knowledge}, when $\mu(r)=0$ and $\nu_r(d)=0$;
\item \emph{negligible perfect knowledge}, when $\mu(r)=O(1)$ and $\nu_r(d)=O(1)$;
\item \emph{Pareto perfect knowledge}, when $(r,d)$ lies on the Pareto frontier.
\end{itemize}

The first is a limiting ideal, the second is the robust algorithmic version of that ideal, and the third
is the practical frontier of scientific improvement. Together, these notions clarify both the ultimate
goal of inquiry and the point at which further progress ceases to be possible without sacrifice.

\section{Current Best Hypothesis}

Scientific discovery is inherently iterative. As we investigate an entity, we accumulate a collection
of candidate hypotheses, each consisting of a representation and a description. These hypotheses
typically differ in both their representational quality and their descriptive quality. Some hypotheses
encode the entity more adequately, while others provide better descriptions of the available
representations. It is therefore both natural and practically useful to identify, among the hypotheses
known at a given time, one that is preferred according to the decision rule adopted in the theory of
nescience.

We call this distinguished hypothesis the \emph{current best hypothesis}\index{Current best hypothesis}.
Intuitively, it is the best approximation to perfect knowledge that is currently available. It provides a
provisional benchmark against which scientific progress can be measured, and it may change over
time as new hypotheses are discovered or as the decision maker is modified.

To define this notion formally, we assume that the scientist has fixed a decision maker
\[
V : \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}^2 \to \mathbb{R},
\]
as introduced in Section \ref{sec:minimizing_nescience}, and that lower values of
\[
V\bigl(\mu(r),\nu_r(d)\bigr)
\]
are preferred.

\begin{definition}
Let $e \in E$ be an entity, and let $\hat{H}_e \subseteq \mathcal{B}^\ast \times D$ denote the
collection of known hypotheses about $e$. A hypothesis
\[
(r^\star,d^\star) \in \hat{H}_e
\]
is called a \emph{current best hypothesis}\index{Current best hypothesis} for $e$ if
\[
V\bigl(\mu(r^\star),\nu_{r^\star}(d^\star)\bigr)
=
\min_{(r,d)\in \hat{H}_e}
V\bigl(\mu(r),\nu_r(d)\bigr).
\]
\end{definition}

Thus, the current best hypothesis is not defined by minimizing miscoding or mismodel separately,
but by minimizing the value assigned by the chosen decision maker. This is important because, in
general, the two components of nescience are conflicting. A hypothesis with lower miscoding may
have higher mismodel, and conversely. The current best hypothesis is therefore the best known
trade-off according to the adopted scientific preference criterion.

When the set of known hypotheses is finite, the existence of a current best hypothesis is immediate.

\begin{proposition}
Let $e \in E$. If $\hat{H}_e$ is non-empty and finite, then there exists at least one current best
hypothesis for $e$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since $\hat{H}_e$ is finite and non-empty, the finite set
\[
\left\{
V\bigl(\mu(r),\nu_r(d)\bigr) : (r,d)\in \hat{H}_e
\right\}
\subseteq \mathbb{R}
\]
has a minimum. Any hypothesis in $\hat{H}_e$ attaining this minimum is a current best
hypothesis.
\end{proof}

The current best hypothesis need not be unique. Different hypotheses may achieve the same value
of the decision maker, even though they rely on different representations or different descriptions.

\begin{proposition}
The current best hypothesis for an entity need not be unique.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let $(r_1,d_1),(r_2,d_2)\in \hat{H}_e$ be two distinct hypotheses such that
\[
V\bigl(\mu(r_1),\nu_{r_1}(d_1)\bigr)
=
V\bigl(\mu(r_2),\nu_{r_2}(d_2)\bigr)
=
\min_{(r,d)\in \hat{H}_e} V\bigl(\mu(r),\nu_r(d)\bigr).
\]
Then both hypotheses satisfy the definition of current best hypothesis, and therefore the current
best hypothesis is not unique.
\end{proof}

If the decision maker is strictly increasing in both coordinates, then every current best hypothesis
must be nondominated among the known hypotheses.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:current_best_nondominated}
Let $V : \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$ be strictly increasing in each coordinate. If
$(r^\star,d^\star)\in \hat{H}_e$ is a current best hypothesis for $e$, then $(r^\star,d^\star)$ is
Pareto optimal within the restricted set $\hat{H}_e$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Assume, for contradiction, that $(r^\star,d^\star)$ is not Pareto optimal in $\hat{H}_e$. Then
there exists $(r',d')\in \hat{H}_e$ such that
\[
\mu(r') \leq \mu(r^\star), \qquad \nu_{r'}(d') \leq \nu_{r^\star}(d^\star),
\]
with at least one strict inequality. Since $V$ is strictly increasing in each coordinate, it follows that
\[
V\bigl(\mu(r'),\nu_{r'}(d')\bigr)
<
V\bigl(\mu(r^\star),\nu_{r^\star}(d^\star)\bigr),
\]
contradicting the assumption that $(r^\star,d^\star)$ minimizes $V$ on $\hat{H}_e$. Therefore
$(r^\star,d^\star)$ must be Pareto optimal within $\hat{H}_e$.
\end{proof}

This proposition shows that, under a reasonable decision maker, the current best hypothesis must
lie on the frontier of the known hypotheses. It need not lie on the true Pareto frontier of the full
science problem, since unknown hypotheses may still exist, but it must at least be undominated
within the set of hypotheses currently available to the investigator.

\begin{example}
Suppose we are studying planetary motion and consider three known hypotheses:
\begin{enumerate}
\item a geocentric representation with a description based on epicycles;
\item a heliocentric representation with a description based on elliptical orbits;
\item a relativistic representation with a description based on spacetime curvature.
\end{enumerate}
If the adopted decision maker assigns the smallest value to the third hypothesis, then the latter is
the current best hypothesis. This does not mean that it is the final or perfect hypothesis in an
absolute sense, only that among the hypotheses currently known, it achieves the best trade-off
between miscoding and mismodel according to the adopted criterion.
\end{example}

The concept of current best hypothesis is inherently time-dependent. As new hypotheses are
proposed, old ones refined, or the decision maker revised, the current best hypothesis may change.
Scientific progress can therefore be viewed, in part, as a sequence of revisions of the current best
hypothesis.

\begin{definition}
Let $t$ denote time, and let $\hat{H}_e^{\,t}$ be the set of hypotheses about $e$ known at time $t$.
A hypothesis $(r_t^\star,d_t^\star)\in \hat{H}_e^{\,t}$ is called the
\emph{current best hypothesis at time $t$}\index{Current best hypothesis!at time $t$} if
\[
V\bigl(\mu(r_t^\star),\nu_{r_t^\star}(d_t^\star)\bigr)
=
\min_{(r,d)\in \hat{H}_e^{\,t}}
V\bigl(\mu(r),\nu_r(d)\bigr).
\]
\end{definition}

This temporal viewpoint is useful because it makes it possible to speak of improvement in a precise
way: progress occurs when the value of the current best hypothesis decreases over time, or when
its objective vector moves closer to the ideal vector.

In many scientific contexts, however, the representation is fixed in advance. One may be given an
experimental dataset, an observational record, or an agreed symbolic encoding, and the task is then
to determine the best available description of that fixed representation. This motivates a restricted
notion analogous to the current best hypothesis.

\begin{definition}
Let $r \in R$ be a representation, and let $\hat{D}_r \subseteq D$ denote the collection of known
descriptions of $r$. A description $d^\star \in \hat{D}_r$ is called a
\emph{current best description}\index{Current best description} for $r$ if
\[
V_r\bigl(\nu_r(d^\star)\bigr)
=
\min_{d\in \hat{D}_r} V_r\bigl(\nu_r(d)\bigr),
\]
where $V_r : \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0} \to \mathbb{R}$ is any strictly increasing scalar preference
function on the mismodel of descriptions of $r$.
\end{definition}

Since the representation is fixed, miscoding is constant across all descriptions in $\hat{D}_r$.
Therefore, selecting the current best description amounts to minimizing mismodel, or any strictly
increasing transformation of it.

\begin{proposition}
Let $r \in R$ and let $\hat{D}_r$ be non-empty. A description $d^\star \in \hat{D}_r$ is a current
best description for $r$ if and only if
\[
\nu_r(d^\star)=\min_{d\in \hat{D}_r}\nu_r(d).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since $V_r$ is strictly increasing, for any $d_1,d_2\in \hat{D}_r$ we have
\[
\nu_r(d_1)<\nu_r(d_2)
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
V_r\bigl(\nu_r(d_1)\bigr)<V_r\bigl(\nu_r(d_2)\bigr).
\]
Therefore the minimizers of $V_r(\nu_r(d))$ coincide exactly with the minimizers of $\nu_r(d)$.
\end{proof}

Although the notion of current best description is often useful in practice, the theory of nescience
discourages fixing the representation prematurely. Doing so introduces an important epistemological
risk: one may become trapped in a local minimum of the nescience landscape. The best description
of a poor representation may still be substantially worse than the best hypothesis obtainable from a
different representation paired with a more suitable description.

\begin{example}
Consider a machine learning task to predict customer churn in a telecommunications company.
A standard approach may fix the representation as the full set of available customer attributes,
including service usage metrics, demographic information, and complaint history, and then search
for the model that minimizes mismodel on that fixed representation. Suppose that after extensive
training and hyperparameter tuning, the best available description still performs poorly. This may
indicate that the representation itself is suboptimal. A better hypothesis may arise from a different
representation, for example one based on a carefully selected subset of variables, augmented with
external information such as regional economic indicators or customer sentiment extracted from
support calls. In such a case, the current best description for the original representation is not the
current best hypothesis overall.
\end{example}

This example illustrates a central methodological lesson of the theory: scientific inquiry should, as
far as possible, explore representations and descriptions jointly. Restricting attention to descriptions
of a fixed representation may be computationally convenient, but it can prevent the discovery of
hypotheses with substantially lower nescience.

For this reason, the current best hypothesis should be regarded as the fundamental notion, whereas
the current best description is only a derived, restricted concept appropriate for settings in which
the representation is externally imposed or temporarily held fixed.

\section{Reducing Nescience}
\label{sec:reducing_nescience}

The pair $(\mu(r),\nu_r(d))$ provides the canonical structural representation of nescience. It captures, in a minimal way, the two fundamental sources of epistemic error: representational defect and descriptive defect. This bi-objective formulation is sufficient to define the science problem, characterize optimality via Pareto dominance, and analyze the global structure of trade-offs.

For the practical task of reducing nescience, however, this representation is too coarse. The quantities $\mu(r)$ and $\nu_r(d)$ are aggregated measures, defined as maxima of more primitive defects, and therefore do not indicate how a hypothesis should be modified to achieve improvement. For instance, a reduction in $\mu(r)$ may arise either from removing irrelevant information (surplus) or from incorporating missing relevant structure (deficiency), but its value alone does not distinguish between these cases. The same ambiguity arises for $\nu_r(d)$, where improvement may require either simplification or enrichment of the description.

To obtain operational guidance, it is advantageous to work at a finer level of resolution by decomposing miscoding and mismodel into their primitive components: deficiency $d_R(r)$ and surplus $s_R(r)$ of the representation, and deficiency $d_D(d\mid r)$ and surplus $s_D(d\mid r)$ of the description. This yields a four-dimensional optimization problem in which each coordinate corresponds to a specific and actionable defect. Each component naturally suggests a corrective operation: augmentation, reduction, enrichment, and simplification.

The full search space $\mathcal{B}^\ast \times \mathcal{D}$ is too large to be explored directly, and most of its elements are scientifically irrelevant. It is therefore essential to restrict the search without excluding meaningful hypotheses. As shown in Proposition~\ref{prop:entity_restriction_search}, when focusing on a fixed entity $e$, the search can be restricted to $\mathcal{R}_e \times \mathcal{D}$ without loss of optimal solutions.

Accordingly, we consider the following four-dimensional, entity-constrained science problem:
\begin{align*}
 & \text{minimize} \quad \{ d_R(r), s_R(r), d_D(d\mid r), s_D(d\mid r) \} \\
 & \text{subject to} \quad (r,d) \in \mathcal{R}_e \times \mathcal{D},
\end{align*}
where minimization is understood in the Pareto sense on $\mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}^4$.

It is convenient, from a notational and analytical point of view, to introduce the following concept of primitive nescience vector.

\begin{definition}
Let $(r,d) \in \mathcal{B}^\ast \times \mathcal{D}$. We define the
\emph{primitive nescience vector}\index{Primitive nescience vector} of $(r,d)$ by
\[
\mathbf{n}(r,d) = \bigl( d_R(r), s_R(r), d_D(d\mid r), s_D(d\mid r) \bigr) \in \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}^4.
\]
\end{definition}

As next proposition shows, the proposed refinement complements rather than replaces the bi-objective formulation. The pair $\bigl(\mu(r),\nu_r(d)\bigr)$ remains the canonical representation of nescience, while the vector $\mathbf{n}(r,d)$ provides a finer diagnostic account of the sources of imperfection in the hypothesis.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:4d_implies_2d_improvement}
Let $(r,d)$ and $(r',d')$ be two hypotheses. If
\[
d_R(r') \le d_R(r), \quad s_R(r') \le s_R(r), \quad d_D(d'\mid r') \le d_D(d\mid r), \quad s_D(d'\mid r') \le s_D(d\mid r),
\]
and at least one of these inequalities is strict, then
\[
\mu(r') \le \mu(r), \qquad \nu_{r'}(d') \le \nu_r(d),
\]
and at least one of these two inequalities is strict.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since the maximum function is monotone in each argument,
\[
\mu(r')=\max\{d_R(r'),s_R(r')\}\le \max\{d_R(r),s_R(r)\}=\mu(r),
\]
and similarly
\[
\nu_{r'}(d')=\max\{d_D(d'\mid r'),s_D(d'\mid r')\} \le \max\{d_D(d\mid r),s_D(d\mid r)\}=\nu_r(d).
\]
If all four primitive inequalities are non-strict and at least one is strict, then at least one of the two maxima must decrease or, at worst, both maxima remain unchanged because the strict decrease occurs in a non-active component. In either case, the pair $\bigl(\mu(r'),\nu_{r'}(d')\bigr)$ does not worsen, and whenever one strict decrease occurs in a component attaining one of the maxima, the corresponding aggregate objective strictly decreases. In particular, if the four-vector improves componentwise and at least one active component decreases, then the pair improves in the Pareto sense.
\end{proof}

Proposition \ref{prop:4d_implies_2d_improvement} shows that a componentwise improvement of the primitive defects induces a valid reduction of nescience. This justifies using the four-dimensional vector as a practical guide for modifying hypotheses.

To compare a current hypothesis $\mathbf{x}=(r,d)$ with a candidate hypothesis $\mathbf{y}=(r',d')$, it is useful to record how the four primitive defects change. Rather than evaluating hypotheses solely in terms of their absolute nescience, this approach focuses on the direction and magnitude of the transition from $\mathbf{x}$ to $\mathbf{y}$.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathbf{x}=(r,d)$ and $\mathbf{y}=(r',d')$ be two hypotheses. We define the \emph{change vector}\index{Change vector} from $\mathbf{x}$ to $\mathbf{y}$ as
\[
\Delta(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) = \mathbf{n}(\mathbf{y})-\mathbf{n}(\mathbf{x}) = \bigl( \Delta d_R, \Delta s_R, \Delta d_D, \Delta s_D \bigr),
\]
where
\begin{align*}
\Delta d_R &= d_R(r')-d_R(r), \\
\Delta s_R &= s_R(r')-s_R(r), \\
\Delta d_D &= d_D(d'\mid r')-d_D(d\mid r), \\
\Delta s_D &= s_D(d'\mid r')-s_D(d\mid r).
\end{align*}
\end{definition}

Negative entries of the change vector indicate improvement, positive entries deterioration, and zero entries invariance. By tracking how each component evolves, we obtain a more informative description of the effect of a candidate modification, making explicit which aspects of the hypothesis improve, which deteriorate, and which remain unchanged.

Since the four defects may have different magnitudes, a normalized version is useful when comparing candidate hypotheses.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathbf{x}=(r,d)$ and $\mathbf{y}=(r',d')$ be two hypotheses. We define the \emph{normalized variation vector}\index{Normalized variation vector} from $\mathbf{x}$ to $\mathbf{y}$ as
\[
\widehat{\Delta}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) = \bigl( \widehat{\Delta}_{d_R}, \widehat{\Delta}_{s_R}, \widehat{\Delta}_{d_D}, \widehat{\Delta}_{s_D} \bigr),
\]
where, for each primitive defect $q \in \{d_R,s_R,d_D,s_D\}$,
\[
\widehat{\Delta}_q(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})
=
\begin{cases}
\dfrac{q(\mathbf{y})-q(\mathbf{x})}{q(\mathbf{x})}, & \text{if } q(\mathbf{x})>0,\\[1.2ex]
0, & \text{if } q(\mathbf{x})=0=q(\mathbf{y}),\\[0.8ex]
+\infty, & \text{if } q(\mathbf{x})=0<q(\mathbf{y}).
\end{cases}
\]
\end{definition}

Thus, a value of $-0.2$ means that the corresponding defect has decreased by $20\%$, whereas a value of $0.1$ means that it has increased by $10\%$. The normalization allows candidates to be compared on a relative scale, rather than only through absolute changes.

Since miscoding and mismodel are defined as maxima of primitive defects, their values are determined by the largest components in each group. It is therefore natural to identify the defects that actively determine the current level of nescience.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathbf{x}=(r,d)$ be a hypothesis. The \emph{active defect set}\index{Active defect set} of $\mathbf{x}$ consists of those primitive defects that attain the maxima defining miscoding and mismodel, namely
\[
A(\mathbf{x}) = \arg\max\{d_R(r),\, s_R(r)\} \;\cup\; \arg\max\{d_D(d\mid r),\, s_D(d\mid r)\}.
\]
\end{definition}

The active defect set identifies those primitive quantities that currently determine miscoding and mismodel. From a practical standpoint, these are the most urgent defects to attack, since reducing a non-active defect may leave the aggregate nescience unchanged.

\begin{proposition}
Let $\mathbf{x}=(r,d)$ be a hypothesis with primitive nescience vector
\[
\mathbf{n}(\mathbf{x}) = \bigl(d_R(r),\, s_R(r),\, d_D(d\mid r),\, s_D(d\mid r)\bigr).
\]
Then the active defect set $A(\mathbf{x})$ consists exactly of those primitive defects that determine the values of miscoding and mismodel, that is,
\[
\mu(r) = \max\{d_R(r), s_R(r)\}
\quad \text{and} \quad
\nu_r(d) = \max\{d_D(d\mid r), s_D(d\mid r)\}
\]
are attained precisely by the elements of $A(\mathbf{x})$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By definition,
\[
A(\mathbf{x})
=
\arg\max\{d_R(r),\, s_R(r)\}
\;\cup\;
\arg\max\{d_D(d\mid r),\, s_D(d\mid r)\}.
\]
Hence, the elements of $\arg\max\{d_R(r), s_R(r)\}$ are exactly those primitive defects whose value equals $\mu(r)$, and the elements of $\arg\max\{d_D(d\mid r), s_D(d\mid r)\}$ are exactly those whose value equals $\nu_r(d)$.

Therefore, every element of $A(\mathbf{x})$ attains either $\mu(r)$ or $\nu_r(d)$, and conversely, every primitive defect that attains one of these maxima belongs to $A(\mathbf{x})$. This proves that $A(\mathbf{x})$ coincides with the set of primitive defects that determine miscoding and mismodel.
\end{proof}

The notion of active defect set is particularly relevant because it identifies the specific components that determine the current value of nescience. Since miscoding and mismodel are defined as maxima, any improvement that does not reduce at least one active defect cannot decrease the overall level of ignorance. In this sense, the active defect set provides a principled criterion for prioritizing transformations of a hypothesis: it indicates where effort is most likely to have a direct impact. In much of current scientific practice, however, improvements are often pursued without an explicit identification of these controlling components. Efforts may focus, for instance, on refining models when the dominant limitation lies in the representation, or on collecting additional data when the main issue is the presence of irrelevant or redundant attributes. While such approaches can still yield progress, they may do so inefficiently, as they do not directly target the defects that govern the current level of nescience. Making the active defect set explicit helps to align methodological choices with the structure of the problem, thereby promoting more effective and systematic progress.

\subsection*{Corrective Operations}

The four primitive defects naturally suggest four corresponding modes of intervention on a hypothesis. At a high level, these operations provide a structured way to modify either the representation or the description in order to reduce nescience. Augmentation targets missing relevant information in the representation, for instance by incorporating additional data or variables. Reduction removes irrelevant or redundant components of the representation, such as unnecessary features or noisy attributes. Enrichment improves the description by adding missing explanatory structure, while simplification removes superfluous elements from the description, leading to more concise and efficient models. Each operation generates a set of candidate hypotheses that explore a particular direction of improvement.

These operations should be understood as idealized directions rather than rigid transformations. In practice, modifying a hypothesis typically affects several defects at once: reducing one component may slightly increase another. Nevertheless, it is useful to conceptually isolate the intended effect of each operation. This allows us to reason about their impact in a clean and systematic way. In particular, when a given defect is active, that is, when it determines the current value of miscoding or mismodel, applying the corresponding operation is the most direct way to reduce the associated aggregate error.

This observation highlights an important principle for guiding scientific practice. The primitive defects are not merely descriptive quantities; they have an operational interpretation. They indicate not only what is wrong with a hypothesis, but also how it should be modified. When an operation successfully targets an active defect, it leads to a strict improvement in the corresponding aggregate measure of nescience. This provides a clear connection between diagnosis and action, and forms the basis for systematic procedures aimed at reducing ignorance.

The four corrective operations generate candidate hypotheses. To decide which one should be accepted, we first identify those candidates that actually improve the primitive nescience vector.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathbf{x}=(r,d)$ be a hypothesis and let $\mathbf{y}=(r',d')$ be a candidate hypothesis. We say that $\mathbf{y}$ is a \emph{four-dimensional descent candidate}\index{Four-dimensional descent candidate} for $\mathbf{x}$ if
\[
\widehat{\Delta}_{d_R}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) \le 0,\qquad
\widehat{\Delta}_{s_R}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) \le 0,\qquad
\widehat{\Delta}_{d_D}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) \le 0,\qquad
\widehat{\Delta}_{s_D}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) \le 0,
\]
and at least one of these four inequalities is strict.
\end{definition}

Thus, a four-dimensional descent candidate does not worsen any primitive defect and improves at least one. By Proposition \ref{prop:4d_implies_2d_improvement}, every such candidate induces a valid reduction of nescience.

This notion coincides with Pareto dominance in the four-dimensional space of primitive defects, expressed in terms of the normalized change vector. The formulation in terms of changes is nevertheless useful, as it emphasizes the directional character of improvement and provides a natural basis for iterative descent procedures.

To select one descent candidate among several, we use the active defects to define a score.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathbf{x}$ be a hypothesis and let $\mathbf{y}$ be a four-dimensional descent candidate for $\mathbf{x}$. We define the \emph{active normalized descent score}\index{Active normalized descent score} of $\mathbf{y}$ relative to $\mathbf{x}$ is
\[
\Sigma(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) = -\sum_{i\in A(\mathbf{x})}\widehat{\Delta}_i(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}).
\]
\end{definition}

Since the active components are those currently responsible for miscoding and mismodel, the score $\Sigma$ measures the total normalized reduction achieved on the bottlenecks of the current hypothesis. Larger values of $\Sigma$ correspond to stronger relative improvements on the defects
that matter most at that stage.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:score_nonnegative}
If $\mathbf{y}$ is a four-dimensional descent candidate for $\mathbf{x}$, then
\[
\Sigma(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) \ge 0.
\]
Moreover, if at least one active defect strictly decreases, then
\[
\Sigma(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) > 0.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For every four-dimensional descent candidate, all components of
$\widehat{\Delta}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})$ are non-positive. Therefore, for each
$i\in A(\mathbf{x})$,
\[
-\widehat{\Delta}_i(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) \ge 0.
\]
Summing over the active set gives
\[
\Sigma(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}) \ge 0.
\]
If at least one active component is strictly negative, then at least one term in the sum is strictly
positive, hence $\Sigma(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})>0$.
\end{proof}

We now combine the previous ingredients into a formal descent scheme.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathbf{x}=(r,d)$ be a hypothesis. We define the \emph{operation-generated candidate set}
\index{Operation-generated candidate set} of $\mathbf{x}$ by
\[
\mathcal{C}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathcal{A}(\mathbf{x}) \cup \mathcal{R}(\mathbf{x}) \cup \mathcal{E}(\mathbf{x}) \cup \mathcal{S}(\mathbf{x}).
\]
The corresponding \emph{descent set}\index{Descent set} is
\[
\mathcal{C}^\downarrow(\mathbf{x}) = \left\{ \mathbf{y}\in \mathcal{C}(\mathbf{x}) : \mathbf{y}\text{ is a four-dimensional descent candidate for }\mathbf{x} \right\}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{tBox}
\textbf{Defect-Guided Descent Algorithm}
\index{Defect-guided descent algorithm}
Given an initial hypothesis $\mathbf{x}_0=(r_0,d_0)$, define recursively:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Compute the primitive nescience vector $\mathbf{n}(\mathbf{x}_t)$ and the active defect set
$A(\mathbf{x}_t)$.
\item Generate the candidate set $\mathcal{C}(\mathbf{x}_t)$ by applying augmentation, reduction,
enrichment, and simplification to $\mathbf{x}_t$.
\item Form the descent set $\mathcal{C}^\downarrow(\mathbf{x}_t)$.
\item If $\mathcal{C}^\downarrow(\mathbf{x}_t)=\varnothing$, stop.
\item Otherwise, choose
\[
\mathbf{x}_{t+1}\in \arg\max_{\mathbf{y}\in \mathcal{C}^\downarrow(\mathbf{x}_t)}
\Sigma(\mathbf{x}_t,\mathbf{y}).
\]
\end{enumerate}
\end{tBox}

The algorithm is \emph{defect-guided} because the score $\Sigma$ depends on the active defects of
the current hypothesis. It is therefore not a blind search through the space of hypotheses, but an
iterative attempt to reduce the currently dominant sources of nescience.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:defect_guided_monotone}
Let $(\mathbf{x}_t)_{t\ge 0}$ be a sequence produced by the defect-guided descent algorithm.
Then, for every $t$ such that $\mathbf{x}_{t+1}$ is defined,
\[
d_R(\mathbf{x}_{t+1}) \le d_R(\mathbf{x}_t), \qquad
s_R(\mathbf{x}_{t+1}) \le s_R(\mathbf{x}_t), \qquad
d_D(\mathbf{x}_{t+1}) \le d_D(\mathbf{x}_t), \qquad
s_D(\mathbf{x}_{t+1}) \le s_D(\mathbf{x}_t),
\]
with at least one strict inequality. Consequently,
\[
\mu(\mathbf{x}_{t+1}) \le \mu(\mathbf{x}_t), \qquad
\nu(\mathbf{x}_{t+1}) \le \nu(\mathbf{x}_t),
\]
with at least one strict inequality on the active aggregates whenever an active defect decreases.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By construction,
\[
\mathbf{x}_{t+1}\in \mathcal{C}^\downarrow(\mathbf{x}_t).
\]
Hence $\mathbf{x}_{t+1}$ is a four-dimensional descent candidate for $\mathbf{x}_t$, which means
precisely that all four primitive defects do not increase and that at least one of them strictly
decreases. The conclusion for $\mu$ and $\nu$ follows from
Proposition \ref{prop:4d_implies_2d_improvement}.
\end{proof}

The algorithm therefore produces a monotone sequence of improvements in the primitive defects,
and, as a consequence, a monotone descent of nescience in the aggregate sense whenever the
improved components are active.

\begin{definition}
A hypothesis $\mathbf{x}$ is said to be \emph{operation-stationary}\index{Operation-stationary
hypothesis} if
\[
\mathcal{C}^\downarrow(\mathbf{x})=\varnothing.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:halt_iff_stationary}
The defect-guided descent algorithm halts at a hypothesis $\mathbf{x}_t$ if and only if
$\mathbf{x}_t$ is operation-stationary.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The algorithm halts exactly when, at Step 4, the descent set
$\mathcal{C}^\downarrow(\mathbf{x}_t)$ is empty. This is precisely the definition of
operation-stationarity.
\end{proof}

Operation-stationarity does not imply global Pareto optimality, and in general it does not even
imply local Pareto optimality with respect to the algorithmic neighborhoods introduced earlier.
What it means is only that, among the candidates generated by the four corrective operations, none
is a four-dimensional descent candidate. In practice, this situation indicates either that the current
hypothesis is already very good, or that further progress requires a broader repertoire of
transformations than those currently available.

Pruning by active defects exploits the fact that miscoding and mismodel are aggregated through maxima:
\[
\mu(r)=\max\{d_R(r),s_R(r)\},
\qquad
\nu_r(d)=\max\{d_D(d\mid r),s_D(d\mid r)\}.
\]
Only the active components determine the current values of these two aggregate objectives.

\begin{definition}
Let $(r,d)$ be a hypothesis. We define the \emph{active representational defect set} by
\[
A_R(r)=\{q \in \{d_R(r),s_R(r)\} : q=\mu(r)\},
\]
and the \emph{active descriptive defect set} by
\[
A_D(r,d)=\{q \in \{d_D(d\mid r),s_D(d\mid r)\} : q=\nu_r(d)\}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:inactive_defect_no_change}
Let $(r,d)$ and $(r',d')$ be two hypotheses. Assume that every active representational defect of
$(r,d)$ remains unchanged in $(r',d')$, and likewise every active descriptive defect remains
unchanged. Then
\[
\mu(r')=\mu(r), \qquad \nu_{r'}(d')=\nu_r(d),
\]
provided that no inactive defect increases above the current active value.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If the active representational defect(s) remain unchanged and no inactive representational defect
exceeds the current value of $\mu(r)$, then the maximum of
$\{d_R(r'),s_R(r')\}$ remains equal to $\mu(r)$. Hence $\mu(r')=\mu(r)$. The same argument
applies to the descriptive side, giving $\nu_{r'}(d')=\nu_r(d)$.
\end{proof}

Proposition \ref{prop:inactive_defect_no_change} yields a useful pruning principle: candidates that
affect only inactive defects, while leaving all active defects unchanged, need not be explored if the
goal is to reduce current nescience.

A very common reduction is to impose explicit complexity or length bounds.

\begin{definition}
Let $L_R,L_D \in \mathbb{N}$. We define the \emph{bounded search region}\index{Bounded search
region}
\[
\mathcal{H}_{L_R,L_D}
=
\{(r,d)\in\mathcal{B}^\ast\times\mathcal{D} : \ell(r)\le L_R,\ \ell(d)\le L_D\}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:bounded_region_finite}
If the set of descriptions of length at most $L_D$ is finite, then $\mathcal{H}_{L_R,L_D}$ is finite.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
There are only finitely many binary strings of length at most $L_R$. By assumption, there are only
finitely many descriptions of length at most $L_D$. Hence their Cartesian product is finite.
\end{proof}

Bounded search regions are useful because they transform the search problem into a finite one.
Their main drawback is that they may exclude highly informative hypotheses of greater complexity.
For this reason, they are best viewed as practical truncations rather than as part of the foundational
definition of nescience.

\subsection*{The Parametric Case}

The previous framework becomes more structured when descriptions are restricted to a parametric
family of models.

\begin{definition}
Let $\Theta$ be a parameter set and let
\[
\mathcal{D}_\Theta = \{ d_\theta : \theta \in \Theta \} \subseteq \mathcal{D}
\]
be a parametric family of descriptions. For a fixed entity $e \in \mathcal{E}$, the corresponding
\emph{parametric feasible region}\index{Parametric feasible region} is
\[
\mathcal{H}_{e,\Theta}
=
\mathcal{R}_e \times \mathcal{D}_\Theta.
\]
\end{definition}

For a fixed representation $r \in \mathcal{R}_e$, the primitive nescience vector becomes
\[
\mathbf{n}(r,\theta)
=
\bigl(
d_R(r),\,
s_R(r),\,
d_D(d_\theta\mid r),\,
s_D(d_\theta\mid r)
\bigr).
\]
Thus, the representation-side quantities depend only on $r$, whereas the description-side quantities
depend on the parameter $\theta$.

\begin{definition}
Let $r\in \mathcal{R}_e$ be fixed. A \emph{parametric enrichment step}\index{Parametric
enrichment step} is a transition
\[
\theta \mapsto \theta'
\]
such that
\[
d_D(d_{\theta'}\mid r) < d_D(d_\theta\mid r).
\]
A \emph{parametric simplification step}\index{Parametric simplification step} is a transition
\[
\theta \mapsto \theta'
\]
such that
\[
s_D(d_{\theta'}\mid r) < s_D(d_\theta\mid r).
\]
\end{definition}

In a parametric family, enrichment and simplification become operations on the parameter space.
This makes the descriptional part of the search substantially more structured. It also leads naturally
to an alternating strategy: for a given representation, improve the parameters of the model family;
then, if needed, modify the representation itself through augmentation or reduction.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:parametric_fixed_r}
Let $r \in \mathcal{R}_e$ be fixed and let $\theta,\theta'\in\Theta$.
If
\[
d_D(d_{\theta'}\mid r) \le d_D(d_\theta\mid r),
\qquad
s_D(d_{\theta'}\mid r) \le s_D(d_\theta\mid r),
\]
with at least one strict inequality, then
\[
\nu_r(d_{\theta'}) \le \nu_r(d_\theta),
\]
with strict inequality whenever the strict decrease occurs in an active description-side defect.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since $r$ is fixed, the representation-side quantities are unchanged. On the description side,
\[
\nu_r(d_{\theta'})=\max\{d_D(d_{\theta'}\mid r),s_D(d_{\theta'}\mid r)\}
\le
\max\{d_D(d_\theta\mid r),s_D(d_\theta\mid r)\}=\nu_r(d_\theta),
\]
by monotonicity of the maximum function. If the strict decrease occurs in a component attaining the
maximum, then the inequality is strict.
\end{proof}

Proposition \ref{prop:parametric_fixed_r} shows that, once the representation is fixed, the
descriptional part of nescience reduction can be treated as a structured multiobjective problem over
the parameter space. The general defect-guided descent algorithm therefore specializes naturally to
the parametric setting:
\begin{itemize}
\item augmentation and reduction modify the representation $r$;
\item enrichment and simplification act on the parameter $\theta$.
\end{itemize}

This yields a practically important architecture for future implementations. The abstract theory of
nescience remains defined over representations and descriptions, but when descriptions belong to a
parametric family, the descriptional part of the search acquires a concrete internal structure. As a
result, the generic corrective operations of the theory become directly interpretable as model-space
moves, parameter-space moves, or alternating combinations of both.

\section{Unknown Unknowns}

In Chapter \ref{chap:Introduction}, we introduced the idea of the \emph{unknown unknown} region:
the region of inquiry containing entities for which we not only lack satisfactory explanations, but
for which no scientific hypothesis has yet been formulated. Within the theory of nescience, one of
the central objectives is to develop procedures capable of identifying and exploring entities hidden
in this region (see Chapter \ref{chap:Interesting-Research-Questions}).

To formalize this idea, we classify entities according to the current state of our hypotheses about
them. In the present bi-objective formulation of nescience, what matters is no longer the triplet
formed by miscoding, inaccuracy, and surfeit, but the pair formed by \emph{miscoding} and
\emph{mismodel}. Accordingly, the relevant distinction is whether an entity is represented in our
current stock of hypotheses, and, if so, whether some known hypothesis already brings both
components of nescience down to a negligible level.

\subsection*{Known and Unknown Entities}

We begin by separating the entities about which at least one hypothesis is currently known from
those about which no hypothesis is known.

\begin{definition}
Let $e \in E$ be an entity, and let $\hat{H}_e$ denote the collection of known hypotheses about
$e$. We say that $e$ is a \emph{known entity}\index{Known entity} if
\[
\hat{H}_e \neq \varnothing.
\]
We denote by $E_K$ the set of all known entities:
\[
E_K = \{ e \in E \mid \hat{H}_e \neq \varnothing \}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
Let $e \in E$ be an entity, and let $\hat{H}_e$ denote the collection of known hypotheses about
$e$. We say that $e$ is an \emph{unknown unknown}\index{Unknown unknown} if
\[
\hat{H}_e = \varnothing.
\]
We denote by $E_{UU}$ the set of all unknown unknowns:
\[
E_{UU} = \{ e \in E \mid \hat{H}_e = \varnothing \}.
\]
\end{definition}

Thus, an unknown unknown is simply an entity for which no representation-description pair is
currently known. Such an entity may be knowable or unknowable in principle; the present
classification does not distinguish between these possibilities, since that distinction is not
operationally available at the level of current scientific practice.

The sets $E_K$ and $E_{UU}$ form a partition of the entity space.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:known_unknown_partition}
We have
\[
E = E_K \cup E_{UU}
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
E_K \cap E_{UU} = \varnothing.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let $e \in E$ be arbitrary. Either $\hat{H}_e = \varnothing$ or $\hat{H}_e \neq \varnothing$. In the
first case, $e \in E_{UU}$ by definition. In the second case, $e \in E_K$ by definition. Therefore
every entity belongs to $E_K \cup E_{UU}$.

Moreover, no entity can satisfy simultaneously $\hat{H}_e = \varnothing$ and
$\hat{H}_e \neq \varnothing$. Hence $E_K \cap E_{UU} = \varnothing$.
\end{proof}

\subsection*{Known Knowns and Known Unknowns}

Among the entities that are already known in the weak sense that at least one hypothesis has been
formulated, it is useful to distinguish those for which some known hypothesis already attains
negligible perfect knowledge from those for which all currently known hypotheses still exhibit a
non-negligible degree of nescience.

\begin{definition}
Let $e \in E$ be an entity. We say that $e$ is a \emph{known known}\index{Known known} if
there exists a known hypothesis $(r,d) \in \hat{H}_e$ such that
\[
\mu(r)=O(1)
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\nu_r(d)=O(1).
\]
We denote by $E_{KK}$ the set of all known knowns:
\[
E_{KK}
=
\left\{
e \in E \,\middle|\,
\exists (r,d)\in \hat{H}_e
\text{ such that }
\mu(r)=O(1)
\text{ and }
\nu_r(d)=O(1)
\right\}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}
Let $e \in E$ be an entity. We say that $e$ is a \emph{known unknown}\index{Known unknown} if
\[
\hat{H}_e \neq \varnothing
\]
but no known hypothesis about $e$ attains negligible perfect knowledge. Equivalently,
\[
e \in E_{KU}
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\hat{H}_e \neq \varnothing
\text{ and }
\forall (r,d)\in \hat{H}_e,\;
\mu(r)\neq O(1)
\text{ or }
\nu_r(d)\neq O(1).
\]
We denote by $E_{KU}$ the set of all known unknowns.
\end{definition}

This classification is intentionally epistemic rather than metaphysical. An entity is called a known
known not because we have access to its essence independently of representation, but because
within our currently available hypotheses we already possess at least one whose miscoding and
mismodel are negligible. Conversely, a known unknown is an entity that has entered scientific
awareness, yet for which every currently available hypothesis remains imperfect in a non-negligible
way.

The sets of known knowns, known unknowns, and unknown unknowns form a trichotomy.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:kk_ku_uu_partition}
We have
\[
E = E_{KK} \cup E_{KU} \cup E_{UU},
\]
and these three sets are pairwise disjoint:
\[
E_{KK}\cap E_{KU}=E_{KK}\cap E_{UU}=E_{KU}\cap E_{UU}=\varnothing.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let $e \in E$ be arbitrary.

If $\hat{H}_e=\varnothing$, then $e \in E_{UU}$ by definition.

If $\hat{H}_e\neq\varnothing$, then either there exists a hypothesis $(r,d)\in\hat{H}_e$ such that
\[
\mu(r)=O(1)
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\nu_r(d)=O(1),
\]
in which case $e \in E_{KK}$, or no such hypothesis exists, in which case $e \in E_{KU}$.

Therefore every entity belongs to at least one of the three sets.

To prove pairwise disjointness, first observe that $E_{UU}$ is disjoint from both $E_{KK}$ and
$E_{KU}$, since membership in $E_{UU}$ requires $\hat{H}_e=\varnothing$, whereas membership
in either $E_{KK}$ or $E_{KU}$ requires $\hat{H}_e\neq\varnothing$.

Now suppose, for contradiction, that $e \in E_{KK}\cap E_{KU}$. Then there exists a hypothesis
$(r,d)\in\hat{H}_e$ with
\[
\mu(r)=O(1)
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\nu_r(d)=O(1),
\]
by membership in $E_{KK}$, while by membership in $E_{KU}$ no such hypothesis may exist.
This is a contradiction. Hence $E_{KK}\cap E_{KU}=\varnothing$.
\end{proof}

The previous proposition gives a clean epistemic decomposition of the entity space. Unknown
unknowns are entities outside present scientific awareness; known unknowns are entities already
recognized but still imperfectly captured; known knowns are entities for which some currently
available hypothesis already reaches negligible perfect knowledge.

\subsection*{Ideal Known Knowns}

It is sometimes useful to isolate the stronger notion corresponding to exact, rather than merely
negligible, perfect knowledge.

\begin{definition}
We define the set of \emph{ideal known knowns}\index{Ideal known known} by
\[
E_{IKK}
=
\left\{
e \in E \,\middle|\,
\exists (r,d)\in \hat{H}_e
\text{ such that }
\mu(r)=0
\text{ and }
\nu_r(d)=0
\right\}.
\]
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:ideal_known_known_subset}
We have
\[
E_{IKK} \subseteq E_{KK}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If $e \in E_{IKK}$, then there exists $(r,d)\in\hat{H}_e$ such that
\[
\mu(r)=0
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\nu_r(d)=0.
\]
Since $0=O(1)$, it follows that the same hypothesis also satisfies
\[
\mu(r)=O(1)
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\nu_r(d)=O(1).
\]
Therefore $e \in E_{KK}$.
\end{proof}

This proposition shows that ideal known knowns form a distinguished subclass of the known
knowns. In most practical contexts, however, the negligible notion is the more robust one, because
Kolmogorov-based quantities are defined only up to additive constants.

\subsection*{Knowledge Frontier}

The most relevant boundary for ongoing research is not the boundary between the known and the
unknown in the mere sense of hypothesis existence, but the boundary between what is already
captured up to negligible perfect knowledge and what is not. This motivates the following notion.

\begin{definition}
The \emph{knowledge frontier}\index{Knowledge frontier} is the set
\[
\mathcal{F} = E_{KU}.
\]
\end{definition}

Thus, the knowledge frontier consists of those entities that have already entered scientific inquiry,
but for which no currently known hypothesis attains negligible perfect knowledge. These entities
form the natural region in which further improvement of our hypotheses is still epistemically
meaningful.

This definition is simpler and more operational than the earlier anti-Pareto construction. It
captures the intuitive idea that the frontier is made of entities that are already visible to science,
but not yet adequately understood.

\subsection*{New Research Entities}

A \emph{new research entity}\index{New research entity} is an entity that belongs to the unknown
unknown region and becomes the subject of scientific inquiry once a first hypothesis about it is
formulated.

\begin{definition}
Let $t$ denote time. We say that an entity $e \in E$ is a \emph{new research entity at time $t+1$} if
\[
e \in E^{(t)}_{UU}
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
e \in E^{(t+1)}_{K}.
\]
\end{definition}

In other words, a new research entity is an entity that was previously an unknown unknown and
has just crossed into the known region by acquiring its first known hypothesis.

The next proposition formalizes the obvious but important relation between new research entities
and unknown unknowns.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:new_research_entity_from_uu}
If $e$ is a new research entity at time $t+1$, then
\[
e \in E^{(t)}_{UU}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
This is immediate from the definition of new research entity at time $t+1$.
\end{proof}

\subsection*{Temporal Monotonicity}

If the set of known hypotheses grows monotonically over time, then the region of unknown
unknowns can only shrink.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:uu_monotone}
Assume that for every entity $e \in E$ and every $t \in \mathbb{N}$,
\[
\hat{H}^{(t)}_e \subseteq \hat{H}^{(t+1)}_e.
\]
Then
\[
E^{(t+1)}_{UU} \subseteq E^{(t)}_{UU}
\qquad\text{for all } t\in\mathbb{N}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let $e \in E^{(t+1)}_{UU}$. Then
\[
\hat{H}^{(t+1)}_e = \varnothing.
\]
Since $\hat{H}^{(t)}_e \subseteq \hat{H}^{(t+1)}_e$, it follows that
\[
\hat{H}^{(t)}_e = \varnothing.
\]
Therefore $e \in E^{(t)}_{UU}$, and hence
\[
E^{(t+1)}_{UU} \subseteq E^{(t)}_{UU}.
\]
\end{proof}

Under the same monotonicity assumption, the set of known knowns can only grow.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:kk_monotone}
Assume that for every entity $e \in E$ and every $t \in \mathbb{N}$,
\[
\hat{H}^{(t)}_e \subseteq \hat{H}^{(t+1)}_e.
\]
Then
\[
E^{(t)}_{KK} \subseteq E^{(t+1)}_{KK}
\qquad\text{for all } t\in\mathbb{N}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let $e \in E^{(t)}_{KK}$. Then there exists $(r,d)\in \hat{H}^{(t)}_e$ such that
\[
\mu(r)=O(1)
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\nu_r(d)=O(1).
\]
Since $\hat{H}^{(t)}_e \subseteq \hat{H}^{(t+1)}_e$, the same hypothesis also belongs to
$\hat{H}^{(t+1)}_e$. Hence $e \in E^{(t+1)}_{KK}$.
\end{proof}

These two monotonicity properties express the most basic dynamics of scientific discovery:
entities move out of the unknown unknown region when a first hypothesis is formulated, and they
move out of the knowledge frontier when a sufficiently good hypothesis is found.

\subsection*{Concluding Remark}

The unknown unknown region plays a foundational role in the theory of nescience. By definition,
it contains those entities for which no hypothesis has yet been proposed. Scientific innovation
begins precisely when an entity leaves this region and becomes the object of representation and
description. In this sense, the reduction of the unknown unknown region is one of the clearest
formal signatures of epistemic expansion.

\section{Science vs. Pseudoscience}

In the theory of nescience, scientific progress is measured by the systematic reduction of ignorance. As we propose and validate hypotheses about previously unstudied entities, those entities transition from the unknown unknown region to either the known known or known unknown regions. From this perspective, the set of unknown entities should decrease over time.

\begin{proposition}
Let $\mathcal{E}^{(t)}_{UU}$ denote the set of unknown unknown entities at time $t$ and $\mathcal{E}^{(t+1)}_{UU}$ at a posterior time. Then, $\mathcal{E}^{(t+1)}_{UU} \subseteq \mathcal{E}^{(t)}_{UU} \quad \text{for all } t \in \mathbb{N}$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
At each time step $t$, let the process of scientific discovery introduce new hypotheses about entities in $\mathcal{E}$, thereby moving those entities from the unknown unknow region into the known known region (either known unknowns or known known).
\end{proof}

In addition to the reduction in the cardinality of the unknown unknown region, we can also observe a progressive reduction in the nescience of individual entities as more refined hypotheses are introduced. Recall that the nescience of an entity is defined based on its current best hypothesis.

\begin{proposition}
Let $e \in \mathcal{E}$ be an entity such that $\hat{\mathcal{H}}^{(t)}_e$ and $\hat{\mathcal{H}}^{(t+1)}_e$ are the collections of known hypotheses about $e$ at times $t$ and $t+1$, respectively. Then, if a new hypothesis $h' \in \hat{\mathcal{H}}^{(t+1)}_e \setminus \hat{\mathcal{H}}^{(t)}_e$ is such that $\nu(h') < \nu(h)$ for all $h \in \hat{\mathcal{H}}^{(t)}_e$, the nescience of $e$ satisfies:

$$
\nu^{(t+1)}(e) < \nu^{(t)}(e),
$$

where $\nu^{(t)}(e)$ denotes the nescience of the current best hypothesis at time $t$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By definition, we have that $\nu^{(t)}(e) = \min_{h \in \hat{\mathcal{H}}^{(t)}_e} \nu(h)$ and $\nu^{(t+1)}(e) = \min_{h \in \hat{\mathcal{H}}^{(t+1)}_e} \nu(h)$. If a new hypothesis $h' \in \hat{\mathcal{H}}^{(t+1)}_e$ is added such that $\nu(h') < \nu^{(t)}(e)$, then $\nu^{(t+1)}(e) = \nu(h') < \nu^{(t)}(e)$.
\end{proof}

This result reflects the iterative nature of scientific inquiry: as we formulate new hypotheses or improve existing ones, the combined errors associated with our representations and descriptions tend to diminish. Over time, and under continued research, the nescience of most entities should asymptotically approach its minimum, potentially reaching the Pareto frontier, or in rare cases, even ideal perfect knowledge.

This characteristic trend of decreasing nescience offers a powerful criterion for distinguishing scientific disciplines from pseudoscientific ones. In genuine scientific fields, the average nescience of topics demonstrably declines over time as knowledge improves. In contrast, pseudoscientific domains typically exhibit stagnation in nescience: either the number of proposed hypotheses remains static or their quality fails to improve, leading to no measurable reduction in ignorance. Thus, the presence or absence of a decreasing trend in nescience provides a formal, quantitative indicator for demarcating science from pseudoscience.

\section{Nescience of Areas}
\label{sec:nescience_areas}

As discussed in Section \ref{sec:areas}, entities can be grouped into research areas, defined as subsets $\mathcal{A} \subset \mathcal{E}$ of the overall entity space. The concept of a research area is meaningful when the entities in $\mathcal{A}$ share a common epistemic domain or possess a unifying property, such as belonging to the same scientific field or addressing a related phenomenon. To quantify how much we do not know about a research area $\mathcal{A}$, we must work with the subset of entities within $\mathcal{A}$ for which at least one hypothesis is currently known $\hat{\mathcal{A}} \subseteq \mathcal{A}$. A representation of the area $\mathcal{A}$ is then a symbolic encoding $R_{\hat{\mathcal{A}}}$ of the known entities in $\hat{\mathcal{A}}$, suitable for further descriptive analysis. Given this representation, we define a description of the area $\mathcal{A}$ as a string $d_{\hat{\mathcal{A}}}$ that models or summarizes the structure or content of $R_{\hat{\mathcal{A}}}$.

The concept of nescience can be extended from individual entities to entire research areas, allowing us to quantitatively assess how well a given area is understood. This extension relies on generalizing the core components of nescience to sets of entities. We use the notions of combined miscoding of the known subset (see Section \ref{sec:miscoding_areas}), combined inaccuracy (see Section \ref{sec:inaccuracy_areas}), and combined surfeit (see Section \ref{sec:surfeit_areas}) to define a composite measure of nescience at the area level.

In practice, due to the difficulty of precisely identifying the entire known subset $\hat{\mathcal{A}} \subseteq \mathcal{A}$, it is often convenient to use approximations. One practical and interpretable approximation is the notion of average nescience, which provides a summary of the epistemic quality of the known entities within the area.

\begin{definition}
Let $\mathcal{A} \subset \mathcal{E}$ be an area, and $\hat{\mathcal{A}}$ be the subset of known entities. The \emph{average nescience of the area} is defined as:
\[
\bar{\nu}(\mathcal{A}) = \frac{1}{|\hat{\mathcal{A}}|} \sum_{e \in \hat{\mathcal{A}}} \nu(e),
\]
where $\nu(e)$ denotes the nescience of the current best hypothesis about entity $e$ given a particular decision maker.
\end{definition}

For instance, when dealing with research topics, a research area may correspond to a broad disciplinary domain, such as biology, mathematics, or physics, each comprising a collection of entities that fall under that domain. In this context, the area of biology would include all research topics formally classified within the biological sciences. This classification allows us to compute and compare the nescience of distinct knowledge domains by aggregating the nescience of the individual topics they encompass (see Section \ref{sec:measuring_research_areas}). Such comparisons provide insight into which areas of human knowledge remain most poorly understood and where future research efforts might be most impactful.
