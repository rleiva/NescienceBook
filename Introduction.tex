\chapter{Introduction}
\label{chap:Introduction}

\begin{quote}

\emph{If presented with a choice between indifferent alternatives,\\
then one ought to select the simplest one.}\\
Occam's razor principle 
\end{flushright}

\bigskip

We find ourselves in an age where knowledge has become one of our most valued resources, driving both economic progress and societal well-being. Science continually provides us with deeper insights and practical solutions, shaping our daily lives through innovations ranging from advanced medical therapies to transformative digital technologies. However, the path of scientific exploration is neither straightforward nor free from significant barriers. Despite remarkable progress, our current scientific methodologies remain bounded by inherent limitations that restrict our ability to fully understand and address complex issues.

These limitations manifest in various ways, including fragmented research efforts, rigid disciplinary boundaries, and an often narrow approach to funding allocation, which tends to overlook bold, high-risk projects with transformative potential. Moreover, the conventional metrics of scientific success frequently incentivize incremental progress rather than genuinely groundbreaking discoveries, limiting the pace and scope of innovation.

The \emph{theory of nescience} emerges from the critical recognition of these challenges and and the need to overcome them. It advocates a renewed approach to exploring the unknown, driven by curiosity and the willingness to question accepted knowledge rigorously. By systematically addressing the limitations inherent in contemporary scientific practice, our objective is to provide robust frameworks that significantly enhance our capability to solve real-world problems and catalyze meaningful advancements across diverse fields.

\section{Scientific Knowledge}

The pursuit of knowledge begins with identifying the \emph{entities}\index{Entity} we seek to understand, entities that are often extraordinarily diverse. Mathematicians focus on abstract concepts, biologists on living organisms, and engineers on machines. Our quest for understanding is fundamentally driven by our need to predict outcomes, solve problems, and navigate the complexities of the world around us. For instance, we know that applying sufficient heat to wood will ignite a fire, or that a fever may indicate a viral infection. Practical problem-solving, therefore, hinges crucially on our ability to recognize patterns and create simplified models of these entities. These models empower us to interact beneficially with the world.

Yet, while ideally we strive to build precise \emph{descriptions}\index{Description} capable of fully reconstructing the entities we study, in reality, such perfection often eludes us, particularly with abstract entities. Consequently, we rely on \emph{representations}\index{Representation}, texts or datasets capturing essential details of these entities. Physicists might represent an entity through experimental results; computer scientists through measured data; sociologists through observed facts. Figure \ref{fig:representationProblem} illustrates this critical interplay: although we aim to directly describe entities, practically, our descriptions are based on constructed representations.

Understanding the effectiveness of our scientific knowledge requires examining two fundamental sources of error. First, the representation of an entity may fail to encode the entity faithfully, either because relevant information is missing or because irrelevant information has been included. We call this error \emph{miscoding}\index{Miscoding}. Second, even when a suitable representation has been chosen, the description or model used to explain it may fail to capture its structure correctly or economically. We call this error \emph{mismodel}\index{Mismodel}. 

Collectively, miscoding and mismodel hinder our quest for clear understanding and reliable prediction. By combining these two fundamental sources of error into a single measure, known as \emph{nescience}\index{Nescience}, we quantify our lack of knowledge about an entity. This provides a systematic framework for identifying whether our ignorance arises primarily from inadequate representations, inadequate descriptions, or both, and for guiding the progressive reduction of that ignorance.

\section{Entities}

The theory of nescience begins with the identification of the objects about which knowledge is sought. Scientific inquiry is directed toward a wide variety of such objects: planets, particles, languages, ecosystems, social institutions, mathematical structures, technological systems, and many others. In this book, we refer to these objects of investigation as \emph{entities}\index{Entity}. An entity may be concrete, such as a chemical compound, a biological cell, or a physical process, or abstract, such as a mathematical function, an algorithm, or a cultural practice.

The range of entities that may become objects of scientific investigation is broad and historically variable. Advances in instrumentation, changes in practical concerns, and developments in conceptual frameworks can all modify the entities that are regarded as scientifically relevant. What unifies these cases is not a common ontological nature, but their role in scientific practice: they are objects that researchers attempt to represent, describe, model, explain, or predict. In this sense, the theory assumes that at least part of the unknown is, in principle, knowable by scientific means (see Section \ref{sec:what-is-an-entity}). The central problem is then to characterize the conditions under which such understanding is possible, and to identify the ways in which it may fail.

A first conceptual difficulty concerns the status of the set of entities under consideration, denoted by $\mathcal{E}$. This set should not be understood as a universal set containing all possible objects, but as a domain-relative collection fixed by the scope of a particular investigation. The theory of nescience therefore avoids the assumption that there exists a single unrestricted set of all entities. Such an assumption would conflict with standard set-theoretic considerations, including Cantor's theorem\index{Cantor's theorem} and related paradoxes such as Russell's paradox\index{Russell's paradox}, both of which are discussed in Section \ref{sec:descriptions_entities}.

Accordingly, each application of the theory begins by fixing a non-empty set $\mathcal{E}$ of research entities. The nature of this set depends on the domain under study. In mathematics, it may consist of classes of abstract structures such as groups, functions, or graphs. In biology, it may contain organisms, cells, genes, or ecosystems. In the social sciences, it may include social practices, institutions, or economic systems. In computer science, it may contain algorithms, data structures, programs, or computational processes.

Once such a domain-relative set of entities has been fixed, the theory studies how its elements may be represented, how those representations may be described, and how failures in representation and description give rise to nescience. The purpose is not to provide a universal ontology of all possible objects, but to establish a general framework that can be applied to well-delimited domains of inquiry.

In many scientific contexts, entities cannot be investigated directly. This is especially clear for abstract entities, but it also occurs in empirical domains, where the object of study is accessed only through observations, measurements, diagrams, datasets, or other symbolic records. For this reason, scientific inquiry typically proceeds by means of \emph{representations}\index{Representation}: finite strings or symbolic structures that stand in for the entities under investigation and make them available for analysis, comparison, and modeling.

In the theory of nescience, representations are treated as finite binary strings. This restriction is not intended to exclude diagrams, texts, equations, datasets, or experimental records, but to emphasize that such objects must ultimately be encoded in a form that can be stored, communicated, and processed. Different scientific contexts may therefore use different representation styles. A biological organism may be represented by a genome, a set of anatomical measurements, or a collection of images; a physical system may be represented by experimental data; and an abstract object may be represented by a formal expression, an algorithm, or a proof. A single entity may thus admit several representations, each emphasizing different aspects of the entity and serving different investigative purposes.

A natural first idea would be to introduce an encoding function from entities to representations. Such a function would assign to each entity $e \in \mathcal{E}$ a string $r$ representing it. However, this approach is too strong for the purposes of the theory. In general, the set of entities $\mathcal{E}$ is not effectively enumerable, and we do not usually possess a direct procedure for transforming an entity into a faithful representation. Instead, the theory adopts the opposite perspective: it begins with finite strings and asks whether a given string contains enough information to reconstruct an entity.

This motivates the introduction of a representation oracle. Relative to a fixed set of entities $\mathcal{E}$, the oracle determines whether a string $r \in \mathcal{B}^{\ast}$ is a decodable representation of some entity in $\mathcal{E}$. When this is the case, the oracle is assumed to have an internal reconstruction function that associates the string with the entity it represents. The oracle is not assumed to be computable or available in practice. Its role is formal: it provides an ideal reference point for distinguishing strings that genuinely represent entities from strings that do not.

The set of representations associated with $\mathcal{E}$ is therefore not directly accessible in scientific practice. In real research, we work instead with effective representations, denoted by $\hat{\mathcal{R}}_{\mathcal{E}}$: strings that are currently taken, within a given scientific context, to represent entities of interest. These effective representations may be correct, incomplete, misleading, or assigned to the wrong entity. They constitute the practical material from which scientific models are built, but they do not guarantee faithful reconstruction of the entities they are intended to represent.

It is important to distinguish this practical notion from stronger theoretical notions. Some representations may be decodable by the oracle; some may be minimal, in the sense that no shorter representation reconstructs the same entity; and some may be admissible, in the sense that they contain no semantically irrelevant independent information. Scientific practice rarely begins with such ideal representations. Instead, it proceeds by refining effective representations: adding missing information, removing irrelevant information, correcting misleading encodings, and sometimes discovering that a representation does not refer to the intended entity at all.

\begin{example}
\label{ex:animals_DNA}
If the entities under study are animals, one possible representation style is to encode them by means of their DNA\index{DNA}. Such a representation may be highly informative for many biological purposes, since it captures inherited structure and supports the study of species, traits, and evolutionary relations. However, DNA does not encode all aspects of an individual animal. It does not include its life history, acquired injuries, learned behavior, or environmental interactions. Thus, DNA may be an adequate representation for some research questions, such as the study of species-level traits, but insufficient for others, such as the study of a particular individual.
\end{example}

Because representations are finite strings, not every entity can necessarily be represented. This limitation is especially clear when the set of entities is uncountable. For instance, if $\mathcal{E}$ is the set of real numbers, then only countably many of them can be represented by finite binary strings. Most real numbers therefore cannot be reconstructed from any finite representation. This illustrates a basic limitation of representation-based knowledge: the space of possible entities may exceed the space of available finite encodings.

A second difficulty arises because the effective representations used in practice may fail to correspond to the entities they are intended to represent. A string may appear to be a representation of one entity while in fact tracking another, or it may contain only fragmentary information about several entities at once. Such cases are central to the theory of nescience, because they show that ignorance may arise not only from poor descriptions or models, but also from failures at the level of representation itself. These failures will be studied in Chapter \ref{chap:Miscoding}.

Once entities have been represented by finite strings, the next problem is to describe those representations in a form suitable for explanation, prediction, and further reasoning. In the theory of nescience, descriptions are not applied directly to entities, but to their representations. A description is therefore a formal object that attempts to reconstruct, summarize, or model a representation. This distinction is important: scientific models do not usually give direct access to the entities themselves, but operate through the symbolic, empirical, or computational representations available at a given stage of inquiry.

Descriptions change over time as scientific understanding develops. A representation that initially requires a long and irregular description may later be explained by a shorter and more structured one. Scientific progress can therefore be understood, in part, as the search for descriptions that capture the regularities present in representations while avoiding unnecessary assumptions and irrelevant detail.

\begin{example}
Consider the historical development of descriptions of macroscopic physical motion. Aristotelian physics, Cartesian mechanics, Newtonian mechanics, and relativistic mechanics may be understood as successive attempts to describe representations of physical phenomena with increasing generality and precision. Each framework provides a different way of reconstructing and organizing the empirical representations available at a given historical stage. The point is not that later descriptions simply replace earlier ones in all contexts, but that they may reduce mismodel by accounting for a wider range of representations with greater accuracy or economy.
\end{example}

The notion of description must be restricted in order to avoid the ambiguities associated with unrestricted definability. Classical paradoxes such as the Berry paradox\index{Berry paradox} illustrate the difficulties that arise when descriptions are treated as arbitrary linguistic expressions. To avoid such difficulties, the theory of nescience requires descriptions to be computationally meaningful. A valid description is a finite string that encodes an effective procedure capable of producing, or reconstructing, a representation. Here, ``effective'' means that the reconstruction can be carried out by a computer without requiring further human interpretation.

This computational restriction connects scientific description with the theory of computation. In the present framework, a description is represented by a pair $\langle TM,a\rangle$, where $TM$ is a Turing machine\index{Turing machine} and $a$ is an input string. The Turing machine captures the regularities, rules, or model structure used to generate the representation, while the input string contains the remaining information required for reconstruction. This decomposition reflects familiar distinctions in scientific practice, such as a model together with its initial conditions, a theory together with auxiliary assumptions, or a general algorithm together with a particular instance.

The interpretation of the pair $\langle TM,a\rangle$ depends on the domain of application. In physics, the Turing machine may encode a system of equations or a simulation procedure, while the input may encode initial conditions or parameter values. In machine learning, the Turing machine may encode a trained model or prediction rule, while the input may encode data required to reconstruct the observed outputs. In mathematics, it may encode a formal procedure together with the assumptions or objects to which the procedure is applied. In all cases, the description is evaluated by its capacity to reconstruct the relevant representation.

Figure \ref{fig:entities_topics_models} summarizes the relation between entities, representations, and descriptions. Entities are accessed through representations, and representations are modeled by descriptions. The set of all possible descriptions is denoted by $\mathcal{D}$. However, not every finite string is a valid description: to qualify as a description, a string must encode a computational procedure of the appropriate kind. Moreover, not every valid description is an adequate description of a given representation. A representation may admit many descriptions, differing in length, accuracy, generality, and explanatory value.

A central theoretical ideal is the search for minimal descriptions: descriptions that reconstruct a given representation with no unnecessary information. This ideal is closely related to Kolmogorov complexity, since the shortest program that outputs a string gives a formal measure of the information required to describe it. However, as discussed in Appendix \ref{chap:Algorithmic_Information}, Kolmogorov complexity is not computable in general. Consequently, there is no general algorithm that, given a representation, returns its shortest description.

This incomputability has direct consequences for scientific modeling. In practice, researchers cannot usually know whether a proposed model is minimal, nor whether it is the best possible description of the representation under study. Scientific inquiry must therefore proceed by means of effective descriptions: descriptions actually available within a given scientific context, without any guarantee that they are minimal or fully adequate. These effective descriptions are refined through comparison with representations, correction of failures, simplification of unnecessary structure, and replacement by more successful alternatives.

The theory of nescience uses this framework to identify the sources of failure that arise in scientific knowledge. Errors may occur at the level of representation, when a string fails to encode the intended entity adequately; this is the problem of miscoding. Errors may also occur at the level of description, when a model fails to reconstruct a representation correctly or economically; this is the problem of mismodel. The following sections introduce these components and explain how they are combined into the measure of nescience.

The preceding sections distinguished between entities and their representations. In practice, however, the representations available to researchers are rarely guaranteed to be correct, complete, or free from irrelevant information. Scientific inquiry typically begins with effective representations: finite strings that are currently taken to represent an entity, even though they may fail to coincide with any admissible representation of that entity. The purpose of miscoding is to measure this failure at the representational level.

Let $e \in \mathcal{E}$ be an entity and let $r'$ be an effective representation that is intended to represent $e$. Ideally, $r'$ would belong to the set of admissible representations of $e$, denoted $\bar{\mathcal{R}}_e$. In that case, the representation would contain sufficient information about the entity and would not contain independent information irrelevant to it. In general, however, $r'$ may deviate from $\bar{\mathcal{R}}_e$ in two different ways: it may omit information that is required to represent the entity adequately, or it may include information that does not belong to the entity at all. Miscoding is introduced to quantify these two forms of representational failure.

The first component of miscoding is representational deficiency. A representation is deficient when it lacks information required to obtain an admissible representation of the entity. Formally, this deficiency can be understood in terms of the shortest program needed to transform the effective representation $r'$ into an admissible representation $r \in \bar{\mathcal{R}}_e$. If this transformation requires a long program, then substantial information is missing from $r'$. In that case, the representation is poor not because it is too long, but because it fails to contain the information needed to reconstruct the entity adequately.

The second component is representational surplus. A representation may contain information that is not required to represent the entity and is not recoverable from the rest of the representation. Such information is not merely redundant; it may be semantically extraneous. For example, an empirical dataset may contain variables that are unrelated to the phenomenon under study, or a historical description may include assumptions that later turn out to belong to a different explanatory framework. This surplus can mislead the construction of descriptions, because any model built from the representation may be forced to account for structure that is not genuinely part of the entity.

Miscoding combines these two components. Deficiency measures how much information must be added or corrected in order to obtain an admissible representation. Surplus measures how much independent information must be removed because it does not contribute to representing the entity. A representation is therefore well coded only when it is sufficiently informative and sufficiently free from semantically irrelevant content. In this sense, miscoding is not simply a measure of error in the ordinary empirical sense, but a measure of the distance between an effective representation and the class of admissible representations of the intended entity.

A complication arises because admissible representations are generally not unique. The same entity may admit several different representations, each emphasizing different aspects of the entity or serving different scientific purposes. Therefore, miscoding cannot be defined by comparing an effective representation with one arbitrarily chosen correct representation. Instead, it must be defined relative to the closest admissible representation of the entity.

\begin{example}
\label{ex:leibniz-wallis}
Consider the entity $e$ corresponding to the mathematical constant $\pi$, the ratio between the circumference and diameter of a circle. One representation of this entity is the Wallis product\index{Wallis product},
\[
2\left(\frac{2}{1}\cdot\frac{2}{3}\cdot\frac{4}{3}\cdot\frac{4}{5}\cdot\frac{6}{5}\cdots\right),
\]
while another is the Leibniz series\index{Leibniz series},
\[
4\left(1-\frac{1}{3}+\frac{1}{5}-\frac{1}{7}+\cdots\right).
\]
These two strings have very different syntactic forms, but both represent the same mathematical entity. It would therefore be misleading to regard one as highly miscoded merely because it differs from the other. What matters is not similarity to a fixed representation, but distance from the set of admissible representations of the entity.
\end{example}

This point is important because the set $\bar{\mathcal{R}}_e$ of admissible representations of an entity is generally inaccessible in practice. Researchers usually work with effective representations, while the admissible representations serve as an ideal theoretical reference. The representation oracle introduced earlier provides the formal device needed to make this reference precise. It determines, at the theoretical level, which strings genuinely reconstruct entities and which strings fail to do so. Miscoding is therefore an ideal quantity: it is defined by comparison with admissible representations, but it cannot usually be computed exactly.

There is a further difficulty. Since effective representations are assigned within scientific practice, they may be associated with the wrong entity. A string may be treated as a representation of one entity while in fact tracking another, or it may fail to correspond to any genuine entity at all. Such cases are not peripheral; they occur whenever scientific practice introduces a candidate entity that is later rejected, reinterpreted, or replaced.

\begin{example}
\label{ex:polywater}
In 1961, the Soviet physicist Nikolai Fedyakin reported experimental results that appeared to indicate the existence of a new form of water, later called polywater\index{Polywater}. This alleged substance was described as having unusual physical properties, including a higher boiling point, a lower freezing point, and increased viscosity. Subsequent investigations showed that these effects were caused by contamination. The effective representations associated with polywater therefore did not correspond to a distinct form of water, but to experimental artifacts produced by impurities.
\end{example}

Miscoding captures this kind of representational failure. It concerns the relation between an entity and the strings used to represent it, prior to the construction of a model or description. A low-miscoding representation provides a suitable basis for description; a high-miscoding representation may lead even a well-constructed model toward a misleading conclusion. For this reason, reducing miscoding is a fundamental part of reducing nescience: before asking whether a description models a representation adequately, we must ask whether the representation itself adequately encodes the entity under investigation.

\section{Mismodel}
\label{sec:ch1_mismodel}

The preceding section considered errors that arise at the level of representation. Even if an entity $e$ is represented by a sufficiently adequate string $r$, a second source of nescience remains: the representation may not be described correctly. In the theory of nescience, this source of error is called \emph{mismodel}\index{Mismodel}. Whereas miscoding concerns the relation between an entity and its representation, mismodel concerns the relation between a representation and its description.

A description is intended to reconstruct, explain, or model a representation. Ideally, for a representation $r$, we would have a description $d$ capable of reconstructing $r$ completely and without unnecessary information. In practice, however, the descriptions available to us are effective descriptions: finite strings or computational procedures that are currently used to model a representation, without any guarantee that they are minimal or fully adequate. Mismodel measures the extent to which such a description fails to coincide with an ideal description of the representation.

This failure has two components. The first is \emph{descriptive deficiency}: the description does not contain enough information to reconstruct the representation. In such a case, the output of the description differs from the intended representation, and additional information is required to correct it. The second is \emph{descriptive surplus}: the description contains more information than is needed to reconstruct the representation. In that case, the description may be accurate in the sense that it produces the right representation, but it remains unnecessarily complex. Mismodel combines these two aspects: a good description should be both adequate and economical.

Figure \ref{fig:mismodel} illustrates this situation. The ideal description $d^\ast$ reconstructs the intended representation $r$, whereas the effective description $d'$ may reconstruct a different string $r'$, or may reconstruct $r$ only by using unnecessary descriptive structure. The mismatch between $d'$ and $d^\ast$ is not merely a matter of prediction error. It concerns the degree to which the available description fails to capture the structure of the representation in an adequate and minimal way.

The theoretical definition of mismodel is based on the distance between an effective description and the set of minimal descriptions of the representation. If the effective description lacks information required to reconstruct the representation, then one must add corrective information; this corresponds to descriptive deficiency. If the effective description contains independent information not required by any minimal description, then one must remove unnecessary structure; this corresponds to descriptive surplus. A description has low mismodel only when it is close to a minimal description in both directions: it omits little and adds little.

This formulation generalizes two more familiar notions. The first is \emph{inaccuracy}\index{Inaccuracy}. Inaccuracy approximates the deficiency of a description by measuring how far the output of the description is from the intended representation. In empirical contexts, this may resemble the ordinary notion of model error: a model predicts values that differ from the observed data, and additional information is required to account for the discrepancy. The second is \emph{surfeit}\index{Surfeit}. Surfeit approximates the surplus of a description by measuring the extent to which the description is longer or more complex than necessary. A highly accurate model may still have high surfeit if it achieves its accuracy by introducing excessive parameters, ad hoc assumptions, or irrelevant structure.

\begin{example}
\label{ex:introduction:mismodel:newton}
Consider a dataset representing measurements of gravitational acceleration. A model may predict a constant acceleration of approximately $980.000,\mathrm{cm/s^2}$, while the observed measurements vary around this value. The discrepancy between the model output and the measured representation provides a practical approximation of descriptive deficiency: additional information is required to reconstruct the observed data exactly. If the complete dataset requires $1,620$ bits to encode, while the residual information needed after using the model requires $453$ bits, then the corresponding inaccuracy can be estimated as
\[
\frac{453}{1620}=0.27.
\]
This value does not compute mismodel exactly, but it approximates one of its components: the information still needed to reconstruct the representation given the model.
\end{example}

Surfeit captures the complementary case. Suppose two descriptions reconstruct the same representation, but one does so by using a compact law while the other uses a long list of special cases, auxiliary clauses, or unnecessary parameters. The second description may be accurate, but it is not minimal. Its defect lies not in failing to produce the representation, but in containing avoidable descriptive complexity. From the standpoint of nescience, such surplus matters because an unnecessarily long description indicates that the regularities of the representation have not been fully captured.

The distinction between deficiency and surplus is important because the two components may move in opposite directions. A more complex model may reduce inaccuracy by fitting the representation more closely, while increasing surfeit by introducing unnecessary structure. Conversely, a simpler model may reduce surfeit but increase inaccuracy by failing to reconstruct important details. The problem of modeling is therefore not merely the minimization of prediction error, but the search for descriptions that balance adequacy and economy.

In principle, the best description of a representation would be a minimal description: a shortest effective procedure capable of reconstructing it. This ideal connects mismodel with Kolmogorov complexity, since the shortest program that outputs a string provides a formal measure of its descriptive content. However, such minimal descriptions are generally unknown. Moreover, Kolmogorov complexity is not computable in general. Consequently, mismodel is an ideal theoretical quantity rather than a directly computable metric.

For this reason, practical applications rely on approximations. Inaccuracy estimates the information still needed to correct the output of a description, while surfeit estimates the unnecessary complexity present in the description itself. Together, they provide computable or semi-computable proxies for the two components of mismodel. These proxies are imperfect, but they allow the theory to be applied in domains such as machine learning, empirical science, and the analysis of scientific descriptions.

Mismodel therefore captures the second fundamental source of nescience. Even when the representation of an entity is adequate, our description of that representation may remain deficient, excessive, or both. Reducing mismodel means improving descriptions so that they reconstruct representations more faithfully and more economically. In this sense, scientific progress involves not only finding better representations of entities, but also finding better descriptions of the representations already available.

\section{Nescience}
\label{sec:ch1_nescience}

\emph{Nescience}\index{Nescience} is an old-fashioned English word meaning lack of knowledge or awareness. In this book, the term is used in a specific technical sense. Whereas ignorance often refers to the absence of knowledge that is already available and could in principle be acquired, nescience refers to the absence of knowledge that has not yet been obtained, formulated, or incorporated into scientific understanding. The purpose of the theory of nescience is to provide a formal framework for measuring this lack of knowledge.

The preceding sections introduced two fundamental sources of nescience. The first is \emph{miscoding}\index{Miscoding}, which concerns the relation between an entity and its representation. A representation may fail because it omits information needed to encode the entity adequately, or because it contains information that is not genuinely associated with the entity. The second is \emph{mismodel}\index{Mismodel}, which concerns the relation between a representation and its description. A description may fail because it does not reconstruct the representation adequately, or because it contains unnecessary descriptive structure. Thus, miscoding measures representational failure, while mismodel measures descriptive failure.

Given an entity $e$, scientific inquiry may be understood as the search for a pair $(r,d)$, where $r$ is a representation of the entity and $d$ is a description of that representation. The quality of such a pair depends on both components. A good representation must encode the entity with low miscoding, and a good description must model the representation with low mismodel. Nescience is introduced as a measure that combines these two quantities into a single assessment of how poorly the pair $(r,d)$ captures the entity under investigation.

The formal notion of mismodel is generally difficult to evaluate directly, because it refers to minimal descriptions, which are typically unknown and are related to the non-computability of Kolmogorov complexity. For this reason, practical applications often rely on two complementary approximations. \emph{Inaccuracy}\index{Inaccuracy} approximates descriptive deficiency: the extent to which the output of a description fails to reconstruct the intended representation. \emph{Surfeit}\index{Surfeit} approximates descriptive surplus: the extent to which a description contains unnecessary structure beyond what is required to reconstruct the representation. Together, inaccuracy and surfeit provide practical ways to estimate mismodel when minimal descriptions are not accessible.

The two components of nescience are often in tension. Reducing miscoding may require changing the representation of the entity, for example by introducing new variables, collecting additional data, or adopting a different symbolic encoding. Such a change may improve the representation, but it may also make the existing description inadequate, thereby increasing mismodel. Conversely, improving a description may reduce mismodel relative to the current representation, while leaving the representation itself poorly aligned with the entity. In scientific practice, progress often requires adjusting both the representation and the description, rather than optimizing one while keeping the other fixed.

For this reason, nescience is not merely a measure of model error. Traditional approaches often assume that a representation has already been fixed and then focus on selecting a model that fits it as well as possible. This is a useful strategy in many contexts, but it neglects the possibility that the representation itself may be defective. The theory of nescience makes this additional source of error explicit. It treats scientific understanding as depending jointly on how entities are represented and how those representations are described.

The search for knowledge can therefore be formulated as an optimization problem over pairs $(r,d)$. For each candidate pair, miscoding evaluates the adequacy of the representation, while mismodel evaluates the adequacy and economy of the description. A pair is preferable to another when it reduces one of these quantities without increasing the other. In general, however, there may be trade-offs between them. This leads naturally to a Pareto view of scientific progress: a pair $(r,d)$ is Pareto optimal\index{Pareto optimality} if no alternative pair improves either miscoding or mismodel without worsening the other.

The Pareto perspective is useful because there may be no single best pair independently of context. Different scientific objectives may justify different balances between representational fidelity and descriptive economy. For example, an exploratory study may tolerate a more complex representation if it captures aspects of the entity that were previously ignored. A predictive application may prefer a description that performs well on available data, even if the representation remains provisional. A theoretical investigation may prioritize a more economical description, even at the cost of leaving some empirical detail unexplained. The theory of nescience does not eliminate these choices, but it makes their structure explicit.

In practical applications, it may still be necessary to select a single pair $(r,d)$ from a set of candidates. This requires a utility function\index{Utility function} or aggregation rule that ranks candidate pairs according to the aims of the investigation. The specific form of this function is not fixed by the theory itself. In machine learning, for instance, one may combine practical estimates of miscoding, inaccuracy, and surfeit to compare models and representations. In other scientific contexts, different weighting schemes may be appropriate, depending on whether the priority is prediction, explanation, simplicity, robustness, or experimental feasibility.

The central point is that nescience provides a unified framework for analyzing the incompleteness of scientific knowledge. It identifies whether our lack of understanding arises primarily because we have not represented the entity adequately, because we have not described the representation adequately, or because both failures occur simultaneously. By distinguishing miscoding from mismodel, the theory offers a structured way to evaluate scientific progress and to guide the refinement of both representations and descriptions.

\section{Perfect Knowledge}

In the theory of nescience, scientific progress is understood as the progressive reduction of the gap between an entity, its representation, and its description. This reduction can proceed in two complementary ways. First, researchers may improve the representation of the entity, for example by collecting better data, refining experimental procedures, introducing more adequate variables, or adopting a more suitable symbolic encoding. Second, they may improve the description of that representation, for example by formulating a better model, eliminating unnecessary assumptions, or finding a more concise explanatory structure. In this sense, scientific inquiry proceeds through the iterative proposal and evaluation of pairs $(r,d)$, where $r$ is a representation of the entity and $d$ is a description of that representation.

We say that \emph{perfect knowledge}\index{Perfect knowledge} of an entity has been achieved when there exists a pair $(r,d)$ whose nescience is equal to zero. Since nescience is determined by miscoding and mismodel, this condition requires both quantities to vanish. Zero miscoding means that the representation $r$ adequately encodes the intended entity: it neither omits information required for representing the entity nor contains independent information irrelevant to it. Zero mismodel means that the description $d$ adequately and economically reconstructs the representation: it neither fails to account for the representation nor contains unnecessary descriptive structure.

Perfect knowledge is therefore not merely a state in which a model fits the available data. A model may fit a representation well while the representation itself remains defective. Conversely, a representation may be adequate while the available description remains deficient or unnecessarily complex. Perfect knowledge requires both levels to be resolved simultaneously: the entity must be properly represented, and the representation must be properly described.

This definition also shows why perfect knowledge is an ideal limiting notion rather than an ordinary empirical condition. In practice, miscoding and mismodel cannot usually be computed exactly. Miscoding depends on the relation between effective representations and admissible representations of the entity, which are generally inaccessible. Mismodel depends on the relation between effective descriptions and minimal descriptions of the representation, which are also generally unknown. Moreover, the identification of minimal descriptions is connected with Kolmogorov complexity, which is not computable in general. For this reason, claims of perfect knowledge cannot normally be established by direct calculation.

The link with minimal descriptions explains the connection between perfect knowledge and randomness. If a description is minimal, then it cannot be compressed further without losing the ability to reconstruct the relevant representation. In the language of algorithmic information theory, such a description is incompressible relative to the representation it describes. Thus, at the ideal limit, perfect knowledge corresponds to a situation in which all removable regularity has already been captured by the description, and no further compression or simplification is possible.

This does not mean that every random or incompressible string constitutes perfect knowledge. A random string may be meaningless, may fail to represent the intended entity, or may describe the wrong representation. Randomness is relevant only after the representational and descriptive relations have been fixed. The description must be minimal with respect to an adequate representation of the intended entity. Otherwise, incompressibility alone does not imply understanding.

It is also important to note that perfect knowledge need not be unique. A single entity may admit several admissible representations, and each such representation may admit one or more minimal descriptions. Different scientific contexts may therefore realize perfect knowledge through different pairs $(r,d)$. For example, one representation may be more convenient for prediction, another for explanation, and another for computation. If each representation adequately encodes the entity and each corresponding description has zero mismodel, then these pairs may constitute distinct but equally valid realizations of perfect knowledge.

In practical scientific work, the objective is not usually to prove that perfect knowledge has been reached, but to determine whether successive changes reduce nescience. Since exact values are generally unavailable, researchers must rely on approximations, proxies, and domain-specific criteria. A new representation or description may temporarily increase one component while decreasing another, and it may not be immediately clear whether the overall change constitutes progress. For this reason, the reduction of nescience should be understood as a long-term tendency rather than as a monotonic sequence of guaranteed improvements.

Perfect knowledge may also be unattainable in some domains. There may be structural limitations in the available representations, intrinsic complexity in the entity, or trade-offs between representational adequacy and descriptive economy. In such cases, inquiry may converge not to zero nescience, but to a frontier of best available pairs $(r,d)$, where any further improvement in one component requires deterioration in another. When this occurs, progress may require a change of perspective: a new representation, a new descriptive language, or a reformulation of the entity under investigation.

Thus, perfect knowledge functions as a theoretical reference point. It defines the state toward which the reduction of nescience is directed, even when that state cannot be reached or recognized in practice. By specifying the simultaneous disappearance of miscoding and mismodel, the concept clarifies what it would mean to understand an entity completely within the framework of the theory.

\section{Unknown Unknowns}
\label{sec:intro_unknown_unknown}

Scientific inquiry is not limited to solving problems that have already been formulated. It also involves the discovery of entities, questions, and relations that are not yet part of the current body of scientific knowledge. These cases belong to the domain of the \emph{unknown unknowns}\index{Unknown unknowns}: not merely unsolved problems, but possible objects of inquiry whose existence, relevance, or significance has not yet been recognized.

Within the theory of nescience, the unknown unknowns can be approached through the distinction between entities that are effectively known and entities that are not yet effectively known. An entity is effectively known when scientific practice has assigned to it at least one effective representation. Conversely, an entity may be knowable in principle while remaining effectively unknown because no representation has yet been constructed, adopted, or recognized for it. The problem of discovering unknown unknowns is therefore the problem of identifying candidate entities for which no effective representation is currently available, or whose available representations have not yet been interpreted as belonging to a distinct entity.

A naive approach would be to generate arbitrary binary strings and ask whether any of them constitutes, or approximates, a representation of a previously unknown entity. This strategy expresses the idea of discovery by blind search. However, the space of finite strings is far too large for such an approach to be useful in practice, and most generated strings would have no scientific meaning. The theory therefore seeks more structured procedures for exploring the boundary between what is already effectively known and what may still lie outside current scientific awareness.

To make this problem tractable, it is useful to distinguish between different regions of knowledge. Some entities are effectively known and relatively well understood: they have established representations and descriptions with comparatively low nescience. Other entities are effectively known but still poorly understood: they have available representations, but their associated nescience remains high. These constitute known unknowns. Beyond them lie possible entities for which no effective representation is yet available, or for which existing strings have not yet been recognized as representations of a distinct object of inquiry. These constitute the unknown unknowns.

The boundary between the effectively known and the effectively unknown may be called the \emph{knowledge frontier}\index{Knowledge frontier}. This frontier is not a sharp metaphysical boundary, but a practical and historical one. It depends on the representations, descriptions, classifications, and research practices available at a given time. As scientific work progresses, the frontier may shift: new representations are constructed, old representations are reinterpreted, and entities that were previously invisible to scientific practice become objects of inquiry.

Identifying this frontier in practice is difficult. Scientific knowledge is distributed across publications, datasets, taxonomies, theories, and disciplinary traditions. Moreover, different fields may use different terminologies for related entities, or may classify the same entity under different conceptual schemes. For this reason, the discovery of unknown unknowns cannot be reduced to a simple enumeration of already known research topics. It requires the analysis of existing representations, the relations among entities, and the degree of nescience associated with different regions of inquiry.

One strategy developed in this book is based on combining existing representations. If two effective representations correspond to entities already studied in different contexts, their joint representation may define a new candidate entity or reveal a previously unnoticed relation. This procedure does not guarantee discovery: many combinations will be trivial, meaningless, or already known. Nevertheless, it provides a systematic way to explore the space of possible research directions without relying on blind search over arbitrary strings.

The motivation for this strategy is that scientific novelty often arises from recombination. New entities and questions can emerge when representations from different domains are placed in relation to one another. In the language of the theory, joint representations may allow us to construct candidate entities located near or beyond the current knowledge frontier. The significance of such candidates must then be evaluated by further investigation: one must determine whether the joint representation corresponds to a coherent entity, whether it admits useful descriptions, and whether studying it is likely to reduce nescience.

\begin{example}
One may combine a representation from theoretical computer science with a representation from phenomenology. For instance, joining the notion of a minimum-complexity computer program with the notion of self-awareness suggests the candidate research entity ``minimum-complexity self-aware computer.'' This expression does not by itself establish a well-defined scientific object, but it identifies a possible direction of inquiry: whether there exists a meaningful lower bound on the complexity required for a computational system to exhibit self-awareness.
\end{example}

The theory suggests two practical criteria for guiding the search beyond this frontier. First, candidate entities are more likely to be fruitful when they are generated from entities that still have high nescience. Poorly understood entities often lie near the boundary of current knowledge, and their combination may reveal gaps or relations that have not yet been investigated. Second, candidates are more likely to be novel when they arise from distant research areas. Interdisciplinary combinations reduce the probability that the resulting entity has already been studied, especially when the relevant fields have developed independently.

These criteria do not provide a mechanical solution to discovery. A generated candidate may be ill-defined, scientifically uninteresting, or impossible to represent adequately. The role of the theory is more modest: it provides a systematic way to propose and prioritize candidate entities for further investigation. Once a candidate has been generated, it must still be assessed by asking whether it can be represented, whether it admits meaningful descriptions, whether it connects to existing entities, and whether its study is likely to reduce nescience.

Unknown unknowns are therefore not treated as mysterious objects beyond all analysis. They are approached as potential research entities that have not yet entered the set of effectively known entities. By using nescience, representation, and joint construction as guiding concepts, the theory provides a disciplined method for exploring regions of inquiry that would otherwise remain dependent on chance, intuition, or accidental disciplinary contact.

\section*{References}

The theory of nescience builds upon several well-established foundations in information theory, computability, algorithmic complexity, and the philosophy of science. The following references provide the theoretical background and conceptual tools necessary to understand and situate the ideas introduced in this chapter.
 
Sipser's book \cite{sipser2012introduction} is a widely respected introduction to formal languages, automata, and computability theory. It lays the groundwork for understanding which descriptions are computationally feasible, an essential aspect of the theory of nescience, as it assumes that knowledge must be computable to be meaningful.

Li and Vitányi's book \cite{li2013introduction} is a comprehensive volume that presents the theory of Kolmogorov complexity, which formalizes the idea of description length using the shortest program capable of generating a given object. The concepts developed by Li and Vitányi are central to the theory of nescience, especially in defining metrics such as inaccuracy, miscoding, and surfeit based on algorithmic information theory.

Cover and Thomas's book \cite{cover2012elements} is a foundational text in information theory, providing the mathematical framework for understanding concepts such as entropy, mutual information, and data compression. It serves as a rigorous yet accessible introduction to how information can be quantified, transmitted, and encoded, ideas that relate to the notions of description length in the theory of nescience.

Chalmers's book \cite{chalmers2013thing} offers a critical and historical introduction to the philosophy of science. It examines the assumptions, limits, and methodologies of scientific practice. This perspective is invaluable for situating the theory of nescience within the broader discourse about how scientific knowledge is constructed, evaluated, and refined over time.
