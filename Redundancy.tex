\chapter{Surfeit}
\label{chap:Redundancy}

\begin{quote}
\begin{flushright}
\emph{Everything should be made as simple as possible,\\
but not simpler.}\\
Albert Einstein 
\end{flushright}
\end{quote}
\bigskip


In Chapter \ref{chap:Mismodel} we introduced the concept of \emph{mismodel} as the discrepancy between a description currently available in scientific practice and the ideal class of minimal descriptions of a representation. If \(r\in\mathcal B^\ast\) is a representation and \(d\in\mathcal D\) is a valid description, then the mismodel of \(d\) with respect to \(r\) is determined by two components: the information still missing from \(d\), and the information contained in \(d\) that is not needed in any minimal description of \(r\).

The theoretical definition of mismodel is useful because it identifies the two fundamental ways in which a description can fail. A description may be \emph{deficient}: it may not contain enough information to reconstruct the representation. Or it may be \emph{excessive}: it may contain unnecessary descriptive content. However, mismodel is not computable in general. The main reason is that minimal descriptions are not known in scientific practice. Moreover, even if a candidate minimal description were available, verifying its minimality would require solving problems related to Kolmogorov complexity, which is non-computable in general.

For this reason, mismodel cannot usually be computed directly. Instead, it must be approximated through operational quantities. There are two natural ways to do this. The first one, studied in Chapter \ref{chap:Error}, is to evaluate how far the output produced by the current description is from the representation \(r\). This gives rise to the notion of \emph{inaccuracy}. Inaccuracy is therefore a practical approximation to the deficiency component of mismodel: it measures the extent to which the current model fails to reproduce the representation.

The second approach, studied in the present chapter, is to evaluate how far the current description is from being minimal as a description. This gives rise to the notion of \emph{surfeit}. Surfeit is intended as a practical approximation to the surplus component of mismodel. Rather than comparing the description with an unknown minimal description of \(r\), it examines the internal compressibility of the description itself. A description with a large amount of removable structure, repeated content, avoidable parameters, or unnecessary algorithmic machinery is unlikely to be close to minimal. Conversely, a description that is hard to compress is a better candidate for being near minimal, although not necessarily an accurate one.

This distinction is essential. Inaccuracy evaluates the relation between the description and the representation. Surfeit evaluates the description itself. A model may be concise but inaccurate; it may reproduce the representation well but be unnecessarily complicated; or it may be both accurate and concise. The theory of nescience requires these dimensions to be analyzed jointly.

The central idea of this chapter is therefore modest but practical: since the surplus of a description cannot normally be computed, we approximate it by measuring how much the description can still be compressed. This approximation is not perfect. It detects syntactic and algorithmic redundancy, but it may fail to detect incompressible irrelevant information. Nevertheless, it captures an important and widely used principle of scientific practice: a better understanding of a phenomenon should allow us to replace unnecessarily complex models by simpler, more economical ones without increasing their inaccuracy.

In this chapter we introduce surfeit formally, study its main properties, explain its relation to the theoretical surplus of descriptions, and discuss how it can be approximated in practice. We also examine how surfeit may be decreased, how it interacts with inaccuracy, and how the concept can be extended to research areas.

\section{Surfeit}
\label{sec:Definition_surfeit}

Let \(d\in\mathcal D\) be a valid description. Recall that a description is a finite string, usually interpreted as a pair \(\langle TM,a\rangle\), where \(TM\) is a Turing machine and \(a\) is an input such that \(TM(a)\) halts. The length \(l(d)\) measures the size of the description, while the Kolmogorov complexity \(K(d)\) measures the length of the shortest program capable of producing \(d\).

If \(K(d)\) is much smaller than \(l(d)\), then \(d\) contains compressible structure. In practical terms, this means that some part of the description is not being expressed economically. This compressible excess may correspond to repeated assumptions, redundant parameters, unnecessarily verbose formalism, duplicated rules, or avoidable implementation details. We call this excess the \emph{surfeit} of the description.

\begin{definition}[Absolute surfeit]
Let \(d\in\mathcal D\) be a valid description. We define the \emph{absolute surfeit}\index{Absolute surfeit} of \(d\), denoted by \(\Sigma(d)\), as
\[
\Sigma(d)=l(d)-K(d).
\]
\end{definition}

Absolute surfeit measures, in bits, how much longer the current description is than its shortest possible description. However, absolute values are difficult to compare across descriptions of very different sizes. For this reason, we introduce a relative version.

\begin{definition}[Surfeit]
Let \(d\in\mathcal D\) be a valid description. We define the \emph{surfeit}\index{Surfeit} of \(d\), denoted by \(\sigma(d)\), as
\[
\sigma(d)=1-\frac{K(d)}{l(d)}.
\]
\end{definition}

The quantity \(\sigma(d)\) is a normalized measure of the internal compressibility of the description. If \(\sigma(d)\) is close to \(0\), then \(d\) is close to incompressible. If \(\sigma(d)\) is close to \(1\), then most of the symbols of \(d\) are algorithmically redundant.

The definition deliberately does not include the representation \(r\). This is the crucial difference between surfeit and the theoretical surplus of a description. The surplus of \(d\) with respect to \(r\) compares \(d\) with unknown minimal descriptions of \(r\). Surfeit instead asks a weaker but more practical question: how far is \(d\), as a string, from being minimally expressed?

\begin{example}
Consider a model for classifying emails as spam or not spam. Suppose the model contains many duplicated rules, repeated feature transformations, unused parameters, and several branches of code that never affect the final prediction. Such a model may still achieve good classification accuracy, but its description is unnecessarily long. A compressed or simplified version of the same model could produce the same predictions with fewer symbols. In this case, the model has high surfeit.

By contrast, a shorter decision tree that captures the relevant distinctions with few rules has lower surfeit. This does not imply that the decision tree is more accurate. It only means that, as a description, it contains less compressible excess.
\end{example}

The surfeit of a description is always a number between \(0\) and \(1\), up to the usual additive constants involved in Kolmogorov complexity.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:range_surfeit}
For every non-empty description \(d\in\mathcal D\),
\[
0\leq \sigma(d)\leq 1.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since \(K(d)\leq l(d)+O(1)\), and since \(K(d)\geq 0\), we have, ignoring the usual additive constant,
\[
0\leq \frac{K(d)}{l(d)}\leq 1.
\]
Therefore,
\[
0\leq 1-\frac{K(d)}{l(d)}\leq 1.
\]
Hence \(0\leq \sigma(d)\leq 1\).
\end{proof}

The lower endpoint corresponds to descriptions that cannot be compressed further.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:zero_surfeit}
Let \(d\in\mathcal D\) be a valid description. Then
\[
\sigma(d)=0
\]
if and only if
\[
K(d)=l(d).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By definition,
\[
\sigma(d)=1-\frac{K(d)}{l(d)}.
\]
Thus \(\sigma(d)=0\) if and only if
\[
1-\frac{K(d)}{l(d)}=0,
\]
which is equivalent to
\[
K(d)=l(d).
\]
\end{proof}

A description with zero surfeit is incompressible. This does not mean that it is correct, useful, or accurate. It only means that the description itself contains no algorithmically detectable redundancy. A random string has zero surfeit, but it need not describe anything meaningful. For this reason, surfeit must always be interpreted together with inaccuracy.

\begin{example}
Suppose that \(d_1\) is a very short program that always outputs the same constant value, regardless of the input. The description may have very low surfeit because it is concise. However, if the representation \(r\) contains complex experimental data, the output of \(d_1\) may be far from \(r\), and therefore its inaccuracy may be high.

Conversely, suppose that \(d_2\) is a large program that stores the entire dataset \(r\) explicitly and prints it verbatim. Then its inaccuracy may be zero, but its surfeit may be high if the dataset has a simpler generative structure that the program fails to capture.
\end{example}

This example shows why surfeit cannot replace inaccuracy. Surfeit measures whether the description is economical. Inaccuracy measures whether the description reconstructs the representation. A good scientific model should be evaluated with respect to both dimensions.

\section{Surfeit as an Approximation to Description Surplus}
\label{sec:surfeit_as_approximation}

The theoretical surplus of a description was introduced in Chapter \ref{chap:Mismodel}. If \(r\in\mathcal B^\ast\) is a representation and \(\mathcal D_r^\ast\) denotes the set of minimal descriptions of \(r\), the surplus of \(d\) with respect to \(r\) measures the amount of information contained in \(d\) that is not needed once a minimal description of \(r\) is available. In its algorithmic form, it can be expressed as
\[
s_r(d)=\min_{d^\star\in\mathcal D_r^\ast} K(d\mid d^\star).
\]

This quantity is conceptually precise, but it is not operational in general. The set \(\mathcal D_r^\ast\) is usually unknown, because minimal descriptions of \(r\) are not available in scientific practice. Moreover, determining whether a description is minimal would require solving a Kolmogorov-complexity problem. Hence, \(s_r(d)\) is normally non-computable.

Surfeit provides a weaker but computable-oriented proxy:
\[
s_r(d)
\quad\text{is approximated by}\quad
\sigma(d).
\]
The approximation is based on the following intuition. If a description contains avoidable descriptive material, this excess will often manifest itself as internal compressibility. In such cases, measuring how much \(d\) can be compressed gives information about how much unnecessary content the description contains.

However, the approximation is not exact. The theoretical surplus \(s_r(d)\) is semantic and representation-relative: it depends on what is unnecessary for describing \(r\). Surfeit \(\sigma(d)\) is syntactic and description-internal: it depends only on the compressibility of \(d\). Therefore, surfeit can detect redundant structure, but it cannot detect all forms of irrelevant information.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:surfeit_weak_proxy}
Let \(r\in\mathcal B^\ast\) be a representation and \(d\in\mathcal D\) a description. The value \(\sigma(d)\) depends only on \(d\), while \(s_r(d)\) depends on both \(d\) and \(r\). Hence, in general, surfeit cannot be an exact measure of description surplus.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By definition,
\[
\sigma(d)=1-\frac{K(d)}{l(d)},
\]
which depends only on the string \(d\). On the other hand,
\[
s_r(d)=\min_{d^\star\in\mathcal D_r^\ast}K(d\mid d^\star),
\]
which depends on the set \(\mathcal D_r^\ast\), and therefore on the representation \(r\). Thus two different representations \(r_1\) and \(r_2\) may yield different values \(s_{r_1}(d)\) and \(s_{r_2}(d)\), while \(\sigma(d)\) remains unchanged. Consequently, surfeit cannot coincide with description surplus in general.
\end{proof}

The previous proposition clarifies the role of surfeit. It is not a replacement for the theoretical concept of surplus. It is a practical approximation that becomes informative under additional assumptions about the kind of unnecessary content present in the description.

The approximation is most reliable when the superfluous part of the description is itself compressible or predictable from the rest of the description.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:compressible_appendix_increases_surfeit}
Let \(d,u\in\mathcal B^\ast\) be strings, and suppose that \(u\) is computable from \(d\) up to an additive constant, that is,
\[
K(u\mid d)=O(1).
\]
Then
\[
K(du)\leq K(d)+O(1).
\]
Consequently,
\[
\sigma(du)\geq 1-\frac{K(d)+O(1)}{l(d)+l(u)}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If \(K(u\mid d)=O(1)\), then there exists a constant-size program that reconstructs \(u\) from \(d\). Therefore, to produce \(du\), it is enough to produce \(d\) and then apply this constant-size program to obtain \(u\). Hence
\[
K(du)\leq K(d)+O(1).
\]
Since \(l(du)=l(d)+l(u)\), we have
\[
\sigma(du)
=
1-\frac{K(du)}{l(du)}
\geq
1-\frac{K(d)+O(1)}{l(d)+l(u)}.
\]
\end{proof}

This proposition formalizes a common situation: if a description contains a large block of information that can be generated from the rest of the description, then the description becomes more compressible and its surfeit increases. Repeated rules, duplicated assumptions, copied derivations, and predictable code fragments are typical examples.

However, there are also cases where surplus is not detected by surfeit.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:incompressible_noise_limitation}
Let \(d,u\in\mathcal B^\ast\) be strings such that \(u\) is incompressible and algorithmically independent of \(d\), that is,
\[
K(u)\geq l(u)-O(1)
\]
and
\[
K(u\mid d)\geq l(u)-O(1).
\]
Then
\[
K(du)\geq K(d)+l(u)-O(1).
\]
In particular, appending \(u\) to \(d\) need not increase the surfeit of the resulting description.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since \(u\) is algorithmically independent of \(d\), the information required to reconstruct \(u\) is not contained in \(d\). Therefore, any program that produces \(du\) must contain enough information to produce \(d\) and, in addition, enough information to produce \(u\) given \(d\). Hence
\[
K(du)\geq K(d)+K(u\mid d)-O(1).
\]
Using \(K(u\mid d)\geq l(u)-O(1)\), we obtain
\[
K(du)\geq K(d)+l(u)-O(1).
\]
Thus the appended string \(u\) may increase both the length and the Kolmogorov complexity of the description by approximately the same amount. In such a case, the ratio \(K(du)/l(du)\) need not decrease, and the surfeit need not increase.
\end{proof}

This limitation is important. A description may contain irrelevant information that is itself incompressible. Such information contributes to the theoretical surplus \(s_r(d)\), because it is not needed to describe \(r\), but it may not contribute to \(\sigma(d)\), because it is not compressible. Surfeit is therefore a detector of compressible excess, not a universal detector of semantic irrelevance.

We can summarize the quality of the approximation as follows:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Surfeit is a good approximation to description surplus when unnecessary parts of the model are redundant, repeated, predictable, or compressible.
    \item Surfeit is a weak approximation when unnecessary parts are incompressible but still irrelevant to the representation.
    \item Surfeit must be interpreted together with inaccuracy, because a concise description may fail to reconstruct the representation.
    \item Surfeit is useful in practice because it can be estimated with compression methods, whereas the theoretical surplus of descriptions cannot be computed in general.
\end{itemize}

\section{Practical Surfeit}
\label{sec:practical_surfeit}

The definition of surfeit still contains Kolmogorov complexity \(K(d)\), which is non-computable. Therefore, even the description-internal version of surfeit cannot be computed exactly. In practice, we approximate \(K(d)\) by the length of a compressed version of \(d\), using a fixed compression method.

Let \(C\) be a compressor, and let \(C(d)\) denote the length of the compressed version of \(d\). We can then define a practical estimator of surfeit.

\begin{definition}[Practical surfeit]
Let \(C\) be a fixed compressor and let \(d\in\mathcal D\) be a valid description. We define the \emph{practical surfeit}\index{Practical surfeit} of \(d\) with respect to \(C\), denoted by \(\hat{\sigma}_C(d)\), as
\[
\hat{\sigma}_C(d)
=
1-\frac{C(d)}{l(d)}.
\]
When necessary, the value is truncated to the interval \([0,1]\).
\end{definition}

The compressor \(C\) acts as a computable approximation to Kolmogorov complexity. Different compressors may yield different values, because each compressor detects different kinds of regularity. For this reason, practical surfeit should not be interpreted as an absolute quantity independent of the chosen compression method. It is a model-selection heuristic relative to a fixed coding procedure.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:practical_surfeit_range}
Let \(C\) be a compressor satisfying \(0\leq C(d)\leq l(d)\) for every \(d\in\mathcal D\). Then
\[
0\leq \hat{\sigma}_C(d)\leq 1.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since \(0\leq C(d)\leq l(d)\), we have
\[
0\leq \frac{C(d)}{l(d)}\leq 1.
\]
Therefore,
\[
0\leq 1-\frac{C(d)}{l(d)}\leq 1.
\]
Hence \(0\leq \hat{\sigma}_C(d)\leq 1\).
\end{proof}

In applied contexts, \(d\) may be a computer program, a symbolic formula, a decision tree, a set of rules, a neural-network architecture together with its parameters, or a textual description of a scientific theory. The choice of compressor must be compatible with the representation of \(d\). For example, a compressor designed for natural language may be appropriate for scientific texts, while a structural encoding may be more appropriate for decision trees or algebraic expressions.

\begin{example}
In machine learning, a decision tree can be encoded as a finite string describing its internal nodes, thresholds, features, and leaf predictions. A tree with repeated subtrees, unnecessary branches, or unused tests will often be compressible. Its practical surfeit \(\hat{\sigma}_C(d)\) will therefore be high. Pruning the tree may reduce its length while preserving most of its predictive accuracy, thereby decreasing surfeit without significantly increasing inaccuracy.
\end{example}

Practical surfeit is therefore closely related to familiar principles such as pruning, model compression, regularization, and minimum-description-length methods. The difference is conceptual: in the theory of nescience, surfeit is not merely a preference for simplicity. It is an approximation to the surplus component of mismodel.

\section{Conditional Surfeit}
\label{sec:conditional_surfeit}

We are interested in studying how the surfeit of a description for a representation is affected when some background knowledge is assumed. In particular, we examine the case where a description is constructed under the assumption that some prior information is already known. The surfeit of such a description is referred to as the \emph{conditional surfeit}.

\begin{definition}[Conditional surfeit]
Let \(r\in\mathcal B^\ast\) be a representation, \(s\in\mathcal B^\ast\) a string, and \(d_{r\mid s}\in\mathcal D\) a description of \(r\) given \(s\). We define the \emph{conditional surfeit}\index{Conditional surfeit} of the conditional description \(d_{r\mid s}\), denoted by \(\sigma(d_{r\mid s})\), as
\[
\sigma(d_{r\mid s})
=
1-\frac{K(r\mid s)}{l(d_{r\mid s})}.
\]
\end{definition}

This definition is primarily motivated by practical considerations. When we assume perfect knowledge of \(s\), we can isolate and study the informational content of \(r\) that is not already covered by \(s\), that is, the new knowledge introduced by \(r\) relative to \(s\). Conditional surfeit thus allows us to quantify the conciseness of a description in light of what is already known.

Conditional surfeit, being a relative measure, is a number between \(0\) and \(1\), provided that \(d_{r\mid s}\) is a valid conditional description whose length is at least \(K(r\mid s)\).

\begin{proposition}
Let \(r\in\mathcal B^\ast\) be a representation, \(s\in\mathcal B^\ast\) be a string, and \(d_{r\mid s}\in\mathcal D\) be a description of \(r\) given \(s\). If
\[
l(d_{r\mid s})\geq K(r\mid s)>0,
\]
then
\[
0\leq \sigma(d_{r\mid s})\leq 1.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since \(l(d_{r\mid s})\geq K(r\mid s)>0\), we have
\[
0<\frac{K(r\mid s)}{l(d_{r\mid s})}\leq 1.
\]
Therefore,
\[
0\leq 1-\frac{K(r\mid s)}{l(d_{r\mid s})}<1.
\]
Hence \(0\leq \sigma(d_{r\mid s})\leq 1\).
\end{proof}

Intuition tells us that the surfeit of a description should decrease when relevant background knowledge is assumed, provided that such background knowledge is itself represented without surfeit. However, a complete formal treatment requires a precise definition of conditional descriptions and of the operations by which background knowledge is made available to a model. For this reason, we leave the deeper study of conditional surfeit for future refinement of the theory.

In the same way that surfeit provides a weaker, description-internal version of description surplus, we can introduce conditional redundancy as a weaker version of conditional surfeit.

\begin{definition}[Conditional redundancy]
Let \(s\in\mathcal B^\ast\) be a string, and let \(d_{r\mid s}\in\mathcal D\) be a conditional description given \(s\). We define the \emph{conditional redundancy}\index{Conditional redundancy} of \(d_{r\mid s}\), denoted by \(\rho(d_{r\mid s})\), as
\[
\rho(d_{r\mid s})
=
1-\frac{K(d_{r\mid s})}{l(d_{r\mid s})}.
\]
\end{definition}

Conditional redundancy is a relative measure, and so it is a number between \(0\) and \(1\).

\begin{proposition}
We have that
\[
0\leq \rho(d_{r\mid s})\leq 1
\]
for every non-empty conditional description \(d_{r\mid s}\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Since \(K(d_{r\mid s})\leq l(d_{r\mid s})\), we have
\[
\frac{K(d_{r\mid s})}{l(d_{r\mid s})}\leq 1.
\]
Thus,
\[
1-\frac{K(d_{r\mid s})}{l(d_{r\mid s})}\geq 0.
\]
Also, since \(K(d_{r\mid s})\geq 0\), we have
\[
1-\frac{K(d_{r\mid s})}{l(d_{r\mid s})}\leq 1.
\]
Therefore,
\[
0\leq \rho(d_{r\mid s})\leq 1.
\]
\end{proof}

Finally, we can extend the concepts of conditional surfeit and conditional redundancy to a finite number of background topics.

\begin{definition}
Let \(t,s_1,s_2,\ldots,s_n\) be a finite collection of topics, and let
\[
d_{t\mid s_1^\star,s_2^\star,\ldots,s_n^\star}
\]
be a conditional description of \(t\) given perfect descriptions of \(s_1,s_2,\ldots,s_n\). We define the \emph{conditional surfeit} of this description as
\[
\sigma(d_{t\mid s_1^\star,s_2^\star,\ldots,s_n^\star})
=
1-\frac{
K(t\mid s_1^\star,s_2^\star,\ldots,s_n^\star)
}{
l(d_{t\mid s_1^\star,s_2^\star,\ldots,s_n^\star})
}.
\]
The \emph{conditional redundancy} of the same description is defined as
\[
\rho(d_{t\mid s_1^\star,s_2^\star,\ldots,s_n^\star})
=
1-\frac{
K(d_{t\mid s_1^\star,s_2^\star,\ldots,s_n^\star})
}{
l(d_{t\mid s_1^\star,s_2^\star,\ldots,s_n^\star})
}.
\]
\end{definition}

It is easy to show that the basic range properties of conditional surfeit and conditional redundancy apply to the case of multiple background topics as well, whenever the corresponding lengths are positive and the denominator is at least as large as the relevant conditional complexity.

\section{Decreasing Surfeit}
\label{sec:decreasing_surfeit}

Our objective is to reduce the surfeit of a current description \(d_1\). This may be done by simplifying the description, removing unused assumptions, pruning unnecessary parameters, replacing a verbose formulation with a shorter equivalent one, or discovering a more compact modeling principle. The result is a new description \(d_2\).

The decrease of surfeit should not be interpreted in isolation. A shorter or less redundant description may become more inaccurate. Conversely, a more complex description may reduce inaccuracy. For this reason, surfeit reduction is only one part of the broader problem of reducing mismodel and, ultimately, nescience.

\begin{definition}[Variation of surfeit]
Let \(d_1,d_2\in\mathcal D\) be two descriptions. We define the \emph{variation of surfeit}\index{Variation of surfeit} from \(d_1\) to \(d_2\), denoted by \(\Delta_\sigma(d_1,d_2)\), as
\[
\Delta_\sigma(d_1,d_2)=\sigma(d_1)-\sigma(d_2).
\]
\end{definition}

A positive value of \(\Delta_\sigma(d_1,d_2)\) indicates that \(d_2\) has lower surfeit than \(d_1\). A negative value indicates that surfeit has increased.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:variation_surfeit_range}
For every pair of descriptions \(d_1,d_2\in\mathcal D\),
\[
-1\leq \Delta_\sigma(d_1,d_2)\leq 1.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By Proposition \ref{prop:range_surfeit}, both \(\sigma(d_1)\) and \(\sigma(d_2)\) belong to \([0,1]\). Therefore their difference belongs to \([-1,1]\).
\end{proof}

We can also introduce a relative measure of improvement.

\begin{definition}[Relative variation of surfeit]
Let \(d_1,d_2\in\mathcal D\) be two descriptions such that \(\sigma(d_1)\neq 0\). We define the \emph{relative variation of surfeit}\index{Relative variation of surfeit} from \(d_1\) to \(d_2\), denoted by \(\Delta^r_\sigma(d_1,d_2)\), as
\[
\Delta^r_\sigma(d_1,d_2)
=
\frac{\sigma(d_1)-\sigma(d_2)}{\sigma(d_1)}.
\]
\end{definition}

A value of \(0\) indicates that there is no change in surfeit. A value of \(1\) indicates complete elimination of surfeit. Negative values indicate that surfeit has increased. As usual with relative quantities, this measure becomes unstable when \(\sigma(d_1)\) is close to zero.

The following proposition gives a sufficient condition for decreasing surfeit through simplification.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:surfeit_decrease_sufficient}
Let \(d_1,d_2\in\mathcal D\) be descriptions such that
\[
l(d_2)<l(d_1)
\]
and
\[
K(d_2)=K(d_1)+O(1).
\]
Then, for sufficiently large descriptions, \(d_2\) has lower surfeit than \(d_1\), that is,
\[
\sigma(d_2)<\sigma(d_1).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By definition,
\[
\sigma(d_i)=1-\frac{K(d_i)}{l(d_i)}
\]
for \(i=1,2\). If \(K(d_2)=K(d_1)+O(1)\) and \(l(d_2)<l(d_1)\), then, ignoring the additive constant for sufficiently large descriptions,
\[
\frac{K(d_2)}{l(d_2)}
>
\frac{K(d_1)}{l(d_1)}.
\]
Therefore,
\[
1-\frac{K(d_2)}{l(d_2)}
<
1-\frac{K(d_1)}{l(d_1)}.
\]
Hence \(\sigma(d_2)<\sigma(d_1)\).
\end{proof}

This proposition captures the ideal form of simplification: the description becomes shorter without losing algorithmic content. In scientific terms, this corresponds to removing unnecessary expressive material while preserving the same explanatory or predictive structure.

However, simplification can also remove useful information. If the shortened description no longer reconstructs the representation accurately, inaccuracy will increase. Therefore, the main practical problem is not merely to decrease surfeit, but to decrease surfeit without increasing inaccuracy too much.

\section{Surfeit-Inaccuracy Rate of Change}
\label{Surfeit_inaccuracy_rate_of_Change}

In the previous section, we studied changes in surfeit. We now examine the interaction between surfeit and inaccuracy. This interaction is central because the two quantities approximate the two components of mismodel. Inaccuracy approximates the deficiency of the description with respect to the representation, while surfeit approximates its surplus.

A model that is too simple may have low surfeit but high inaccuracy. A model that is too complex may have low inaccuracy but high surfeit. Scientific modeling often consists in navigating this trade-off.

\begin{definition}[Surfeit-inaccuracy rate of change]
Let \(r\in\mathcal B^\ast\) be a representation, and let \(d_1,d_2\in\mathcal D\) be two descriptions such that
\[
\iota(d_2,r)-\iota(d_1,r)\neq 0.
\]
We define the \emph{surfeit-inaccuracy rate of change}\index{Surfeit-inaccuracy rate of change}, denoted by \(\Delta_{\sigma\iota}(d_1,d_2,r)\), as
\[
\Delta_{\sigma\iota}(d_1,d_2,r)
=
\frac{\sigma(d_2)-\sigma(d_1)}
{\iota(d_2,r)-\iota(d_1,r)}.
\]
\end{definition}

This quantity measures how surfeit changes per unit change in inaccuracy when moving from \(d_1\) to \(d_2\). The interpretation depends on the signs of the numerator and denominator.

\begin{itemize}
    \item If both surfeit and inaccuracy decrease, then the move from \(d_1\) to \(d_2\) is unambiguously favorable.
    \item If both surfeit and inaccuracy increase, then the move is unambiguously unfavorable.
    \item If surfeit decreases while inaccuracy increases, then the move is a simplification that sacrifices some accuracy.
    \item If inaccuracy decreases while surfeit increases, then the move is an enrichment that sacrifices simplicity.
\end{itemize}

\begin{example}
Let \(r\in\mathcal B^\ast\) be a representation. Suppose that for \(d_1\) we have
\[
\sigma(d_1)=0.40,
\qquad
\iota(d_1,r)=0.15,
\]
and for \(d_2\) we have
\[
\sigma(d_2)=0.20,
\qquad
\iota(d_2,r)=0.25.
\]
Then
\[
\Delta_{\sigma\iota}(d_1,d_2,r)
=
\frac{0.20-0.40}{0.25-0.15}
=
\frac{-0.20}{0.10}
=
-2.
\]
The transition from \(d_1\) to \(d_2\) reduces surfeit by \(0.20\), but increases inaccuracy by \(0.10\). The value \(-2\) indicates that two units of surfeit are removed for each unit of inaccuracy introduced.
\end{example}

The rate of change should be interpreted with caution when the denominator is close to zero. Small changes in inaccuracy may produce very large values of \(\Delta_{\sigma\iota}\), even when the actual change in surfeit is modest.

\begin{example}
Let
\[
\sigma(d_1)=0.35,
\qquad
\iota(d_1,r)=0.20,
\]
and
\[
\sigma(d_2)=0.30,
\qquad
\iota(d_2,r)=0.2001.
\]
Then
\[
\Delta_{\sigma\iota}(d_1,d_2,r)
=
\frac{0.30-0.35}{0.2001-0.20}
=
\frac{-0.05}{0.0001}
=
-500.
\]
The numerical value is large, but the actual changes are small: surfeit decreases by \(0.05\), and inaccuracy increases by only \(0.0001\). This illustrates why absolute variations should always be considered together with rates of change.
\end{example}

As we optimize both surfeit and inaccuracy, we encounter descriptions where improving one quantity requires worsening the other. These descriptions form a Pareto frontier.

\begin{definition}[Pareto optimality for inaccuracy and surfeit]
Let \(r\in\mathcal B^\ast\) be a representation, and let \(d_1\in\mathcal D\) be a description. We say that \(d_1\) is \emph{Pareto optimal}\index{Pareto optimal description} with respect to inaccuracy and surfeit if there is no description \(d_2\in\mathcal D\) such that
\[
\iota(d_2,r)\leq \iota(d_1,r)
\]
and
\[
\sigma(d_2)\leq \sigma(d_1),
\]
with at least one inequality strict.
\end{definition}

Pareto optimal descriptions represent the best available trade-offs between reconstructing the representation accurately and avoiding unnecessary descriptive complexity. In practical scientific work, model selection should normally be restricted to this frontier. A description outside the frontier is dominated: another description is at least as good in both inaccuracy and surfeit and strictly better in one of them.

\section{Surfeit of Areas}
\label{sec:surfeit_areas}

The concept of surfeit can be extended from individual descriptions to descriptions of research areas. Let \(A\subseteq\mathcal E\) be a research area, and let \(\hat A=\{e_1,e_2,\ldots,e_n\}\) be the subset of entities in \(A\) currently under consideration. A description of the area should encode the models, relations, assumptions, and organizing principles used to describe the entities in \(\hat A\).

\begin{definition}[Description of an area]
Let \(A\subseteq\mathcal E\) be a research area with known subset
\[
\hat A=\{e_1,e_2,\ldots,e_n\}.
\]
A \emph{description of the area} \(\hat A\) is a valid description \(d_{\hat A}\in\mathcal D\) intended to describe the entities in \(\hat A\) and their relevant relations.
\end{definition}

We define the surfeit of an area description exactly as in the case of individual descriptions.

\begin{definition}[Surfeit of an area description]
Let \(d_{\hat A}\in\mathcal D\) be a description of a known research area \(\hat A\). We define the \emph{surfeit of the area description}\index{Surfeit of an area} as
\[
\sigma(d_{\hat A})
=
1-\frac{K(d_{\hat A})}{l(d_{\hat A})}.
\]
\end{definition}

This quantity measures the internal compressibility of the description of the area. A high value suggests that the area is being described with avoidable redundancy: repeated concepts, duplicated subtheories, overlapping taxonomies, or unnecessarily fragmented models. A low value suggests that the available description is compact relative to its own information content.

In practice, \(K(d_{\hat A})\) must be approximated by compression.

\begin{definition}[Practical surfeit of an area]
Let \(C\) be a fixed compressor and let \(d_{\hat A}\in\mathcal D\) be a description of a known research area. We define the \emph{practical surfeit of the area} as
\[
\hat\sigma_C(d_{\hat A})
=
1-\frac{C(d_{\hat A})}{l(d_{\hat A})}.
\]
\end{definition}

The surfeit of an area is especially useful when studying scientific maturity. A mature area should not merely accumulate more descriptions; it should organize them more efficiently. If a field contains many overlapping definitions, duplicated models, inconsistent taxonomies, or repeated explanatory structures, then its area description will tend to be compressible. Reducing this compressibility by unifying concepts, eliminating duplication, and discovering more general principles corresponds to decreasing surfeit at the area level.

\begin{example}
Consider an early stage of a scientific field in which several subcommunities introduce different names, notations, and models for closely related phenomena. A description of the whole area may contain many repeated structures because the same principles are rediscovered independently in different subfields. Later, a unifying theory may show that these models are special cases of a single framework. The new area description is shorter and less redundant. In the terminology of this chapter, the surfeit of the area has decreased.
\end{example}

Area-level surfeit should also be interpreted together with area-level inaccuracy. A unified theory that compresses many models into one may be elegant but inaccurate if it fails to reproduce the empirical or formal content of the subareas it replaces. As before, conciseness is valuable only when it does not destroy descriptive adequacy.

Surfeit is the final metric we introduce to quantitatively assess our understanding of a research entity. It measures the presence of superfluous symbols in the description used to model that entity. Intuitively, our lack of knowledge is often reflected in the length (i.e., number of symbols) of our current description. Lengthy descriptions tend to include erroneous or redundant elements. As our understanding of the subject improves, we should be able to identify and remove these unnecessary symbols, resulting in a more concise and accurate description.

We define the surfeit of a description for an entity as the difference in length between the given description and the optimal (i.e., shortest) one for that entity. Within the framework of the theory of nescience, we assume that the pinnacle of knowledge about an entity, its perfect description, is represented by the shortest description capable of fully reconstructing the entity's representation. This notion of perfection relies on both the validity of the representation and the accuracy of the description.

The length of the most concise description of an entity is determined by the Kolmogorov complexity of a representation of that entity. In practical scenarios, given that our knowledge of entities is typically incomplete, the most concise possible description remains unknown. Moreover, as previously discussed, Kolmogorov complexity is not computable in general. Consequently, surfeit is a metric that must be approximated in practice.

If we could construct a perfect description of an entity, it would necessarily be a random string; otherwise, it would contain redundant elements that could be eliminated. Within the framework of the theory of nescience, attaining perfect knowledge corresponds to reaching a state of randomness. This inherent randomness defines a boundary on the depth of understanding achievable for a given research topic. However, rather than constituting a limitation, recognizing and understanding this boundary opens new opportunities in both science and technology. For example, by assessing how far our current description deviates from a random string, we can estimate our proximity to realizing a perfect description.

In this chapter, we formally introduce the concept of surfeit and examine its properties, including conditional surfeit. We will also present the notion of redundancy as a practical approximation of surfeit. Strategies for reducing both surfeit and redundancy will be discussed, as well as the relationship between reductions in surfeit and changes in inaccuracy or miscoding. Finally, we extend the concept of surfeit to support the analysis of entire research areas.

\section{Surfeit}
\label{sec:Definition_surfeit}

Given the length of a description of a representation for an entity and the length of its shortest possible description, we can introduce a relative measure to quantify the unnecessary effort involved in explaining the entity using that particular description. We call this quantity \emph{surfeit}. Surfeit is a key component of our definition of nescience, as it reflects the degree to which our current understanding of the research entity includes superfluous or redundant information.

\begin{definition}[Surfeit]
Given a representation $r \in \mathcal{B}^\ast$, and a description $d \in \mathcal{D}$ for $r$, we define the \emph{surfeit of the description $d$ for the representation $r$}\index{Surfeit}, denoted by $\sigma(d, r)$, as
\[
\sigma (d, r) = \frac{ | l(d) - K(r) |}{l(d)}
\]
\end{definition}

For most descriptions, the length of the description $l(d)$ for $r$ will exceed the length of its shortest possible description $K(r)$. Intuitively, the less we know about an entity, the longer our description tends to be. As our understanding of the entity improves, we should be able to remove all redundant elements from the description. There may also be cases where the description is shorter than the optimal one. In such instances, the description oversimplifies the problem, which can be equally problematic. This justifies the use of the absolute value $| l(d) - K(r) |$ rather than simply $l(d) - K(r)$. Naturally, the current description might also be inaccurate, or the representation may be invalid. These concerns are addressed separately by the metrics of inaccuracy and miscoding.

In our definition of surfeit, we chose a relative measure rather than an absolute one (i.e., $| l(d) - K(r) |$) because we aim to compare the surfeit not only among different models of the same entity but also across models of different entities. We prefer to use $K(r)$ instead of the equivalent $l \left( r^\star \right)$ to maintain consistency with the definition of inaccuracy provided in Section \ref{sec:inaccuracy:inaccuracy}.

\begin{example}
In a practical machine learning scenario, consider the task of classifying images of cats and dogs, where the representation consists of a set of training images. An initial complex model, burdened with excessive parameters and irrelevant features, can be seen as a lengthy "description" of the problem. The optimal model, by contrast, achieves a balance between accuracy and simplicity. Surfeit quantifies the excess complexity of the initial model relative to this optimal one. It measures the "extra" components that are not essential for accurate classification. A high surfeit indicates that the model is overly complicated, highlighting the need for simplification to improve both efficiency and generalization, a fundamental principle in machine learning for building models that perform well on unseen data.
\end{example}

The surfeit of a description is a number between $0$ and $1$.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:range_surfeit}
Let $r \in \mathcal{B}^\ast$ be a representation, and $d \in \mathcal{D}^\star_r$ one of its valid descriptions, then we have that $0 \leq \sigma(d, r) \leq 1$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
The numerator $|l(d) - K(r)|$ is non-negative for all $d$ and $r$, and the denominator $l(d) > 0$ (as descriptions must be non-empty). Hence, the entire expression is non-negative $\sigma(d, r) \geq 0$.

To show that $\sigma(d, r) \leq 1$, observe that:
\[
|l(d) - K(r)| = l(d) - K(r) \leq l(d).
\]
since $d$ is a valid description of $r$, and so $l(d) \geq K(r)$. Thus:
\[
\sigma(d, r) = \frac{|l(d) - K(r)|}{l(d)} \leq \frac{l(d)}{l(d)} = 1.
\]
\end{proof}

The surfeit is zero when the length of the description $l(d)$ equals the Kolmogorov complexity $K(r)$ of the representation $r$ of the entity, indicating that the description has achieved theoretical conciseness.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:zero_surfeit}
Let $r \in \mathcal{B}^\ast$ be a representation, and $d \in \mathcal{D}^\star_r$ a valid description for $r$, then we have that  $\sigma(d, r) = 0$ if and only if $l(d) = l(d^\star)$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Assume $l(d) = l(d^\star) = K(r)$. Then:
\[
\sigma(d, r) = \frac{ |l(d) - K(r)| }{ l(d) } = \frac{ |K(r) - K(r)| }{ K(r) } = \frac{0}{K(r)} = 0.
\]
Assume $\sigma(d, r) = 0$. Then:
\[
\frac{ |l(d) - K(r)| }{ l(d) } = 0.
\]
This implies that the numerator must be zero, i.e.:
\[
|l(d) - K(r)| = 0 \Rightarrow l(d) = K(r) = l(d^\star).
\]
\end{proof}

It is important to distinguish between conciseness and correctness. A surfeit of zero indicates that the description is as brief as theoretically possible, but not necessarily accurate. The accuracy of a description is evaluated using the inaccuracy metric. Thus, while a zero surfeit reflects the optimal compactness of a description, its correctness and reliability must be assessed separately through inaccuracy. Together, surfeit and inaccuracy provide a more complete assessment of the description's efficiency and validity.

The following example underscores the need to balance minimal surfeit with low inaccuracy in order to develop models that are both efficient and reliable.

\begin{example}
In a machine learning scenario, consider a model designed to classify emails as spam or not. Suppose this model achieves a surfeit of zero, indicating optimal conciseness with no superfluous elements in its description. However, despite this streamlined complexity, the model exhibits high inaccuracy, frequently misclassifying emails. This illustrates the distinction between surfeit and inaccuracy: while the model is theoretically as concise as possible, reflected in its zero surfeit, its practical utility is compromised by incorrect classifications, as captured by the inaccuracy metric. 
\end{example}

It is important to note that, in theory, more than one description may yield a surfeit of zero for the same representation. This occurs when two distinct, yet incompressible, descriptions have exactly the same length. In such cases, both descriptions are equally concise and minimal, meaning that neither contains redundant information that could be further compressed. Since surfeit measures the relative difference between the actual length of a description and the shortest possible one, any description whose length equals the Kolmogorov complexity $K(r)$ of the representation will result in $\sigma(d, r) = 0$. Therefore, while the perfect description is often referred to as unique, from a theoretical standpoint, multiple descriptions of equal minimal length and incompressibility may coexist.

\section{Redundancy}
\label{sec:Definition_redundancy}

Our definition of surfeit compares the length of a description with the Kolmogorov complexity of the representation, not with the Kolmogorov complexity of the description itself (i.e., $K(d)$). In other words, surfeit is not a measure of the redundancy within a description. It is possible to construct an incompressible description (i.e., one without any internal redundancy) that is not the shortest possible description of the representation it refers to (see Example \ref{ex:description_neural}). Such a description would not be redundant in the traditional sense, yet it would still exhibit surfeit according to the theory of nescience.

Moreover, it might happen that the description $d$ under consideration does not describe the representation $r$; in other words, $d \notin \mathcal{D}_r$. For practical applications, it is useful to introduce an alternative, and arguably weaker, notion of redundancy that applies solely to the description itself, independently of any particular representation.

\begin{definition}[Redundancy]
Given a description $d \in \mathcal{D}$, we define the \emph{redundancy}\index{Redundacy} of the description $d$, denoted by $\rho(d)$, as
\[
\rho(d) = 1 - \frac{K(d)}{l(d)}
\]
\end{definition}

The redundancy of a description $d$ is a quantity related to the description itself, and it does not depend on the representation $r$ being described. The following example shows that a description can have low redundancy (being incompressible) and still have high surfeit if it is longer than necessary to describe the underlying task.

\begin{example}
Consider the task of computing the first $n$ digits of $\pi$. Description $d_1$ is a program that implements an algorithm to generate and print these $n$ digits. Description $d_2$ is a self-extracting compressed program that simply stores the first $n$ digits of $\pi$ as a hard-coded string, followed by a basic routine to print them. 

Description $d_2$ does not exploit the mathematical structure of $\pi$, it merely reproduces the result. Therefore, relative to the representation of the task (computing $\pi$), the surfeit \( \sigma(d_2, r) \) is high because \( l(d_2) \gg K(r) \). 

In contrast, description $d_1$ is significantly shorter since it captures the generative process behind $\pi$. While $d_1$ may still contain some redundancy \( \rho(d_1) > 0 \), for instance due to suboptimal coding practices, its surfeit remains low since it is near the minimal length required to describe the task effectively.
\end{example}

The redundancy of a description always falls within the range $[0, 1]$.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:range_surfeit}
We have that $0 \leq \rho(d) \leq 1$ for all $d \in \mathcal{D}$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Since Kolmogorov complexity is always less than or equal to the length of the string, we have $0 \leq K(d) \leq l(d)$, 
which implies that $0 \leq \frac{K(d)}{l(d)} \leq 1$. Subtracting from 1 reverses the inequalities.
\end{proof}

Finally, next proposition formalizes our intuition that the surfeit of a description is greater or equal than its redundancy.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:surfeit_comparison}
Let $r \in \mathcal{B}^\ast$ be a representation , and $d \in \mathcal{D}^\star_r$ one of its valid descriptions, then we have that $\rho(d) \leq \sigma(d, r)$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Proving that $\rho(d) \leq \sigma(d, r)$ is equivalent to prove that $K(d) \geq K(r)$ for all $d$. Lets assume that there exist a $d$ such that $K(d) < K(r)$, that would mean there exists a Turing machine $\langle TM, a \rangle$ such that $TM(a)=r$ but $l(<TM, a>) < K(r)$. That is a contradiction with the fact that $K(r)$ is the length of the shortest possible Turing machine that prints $r$.
\end{proof}

It would be very nice if Proposition \ref{prop:surfeit_comparison} applies to all possible description. Unfortunately, the proposition is true only when we deal with valid descriptions (from $\mathcal{D}^\star_r$).

\section{Conditional Surfeit}

We are interested in studying how the surfeit of a description for a representation is affected when some background knowledge is assumed. In particular, we examine the case where a description is constructed under the assumption that some prior information is already known. The surfeit of such a description is referred to as the \emph{conditional surfeit}.

\begin{definition}
Let $r \in \mathcal{B}^\ast$ be a representation, $s \in \mathcal{B}^\ast$ a string, and $d \in \mathcal{D}$ a description of $r$ given $s$. We define the \emph{conditional surfeit}\index{Conditional surfeit} of the conditional description $d_{r \mid s}$, denoted by $\sigma(d_{r \mid s})$, as: 
\[
\sigma(d_{r \mid s}) = 1 - \frac{K\left( r \mid s \right)}{l \left( d_{r \mid s} \right)}
\]
\end{definition}

This definition is primarily motivated by practical considerations. When we assume perfect knowledge of $s$, we can isolate and study the informational content of $r$ that is not already covered by $s$, that is, the new knowledge introduced by $r$ relative to $s$. Conditional surfeit thus allows us to quantify the conciseness of a description in light of what is already known.

Conditional surfeit, being a relative measure, is a number between $0$ and $1$.

\begin{proposition}
Let $r \in \mathcal{B}^\ast$ be a representation, $s \in \mathcal{B}^\ast$ be a string, and $d \in \mathcal{D}$ be a description of $r$ given $s$. We have that $0 \leq \sigma(d_{r \mid s}) \leq 1$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Given that $l \left( d_{r \mid s} \right) > 0$ and that $K\left( r \mid s \right) > 0$, since they are the lengths of non-empty strings, and that $ll \left( d_{r \mid s} \right) \geq K\left( r \mid s \right)$.
\end{proof}

Intuition tell us that the surfeit of a description could only decrease if we assume the background knowledge given by the description of another topic. This is because we require that this background knowledge must be a perfect description (it presents no surfeit). However, as it was the case of joint surfeit, we have to wait until Chapter \ref{chap:Nescience} to formalize this intuition.

In the same way we introduced the concept of redundancy of a description as a weaker version of the concept of surfeit, we can also introduce the concept of conditional redundancy as a weaker version of the concept of conditional surfeit.

\begin{definition}
Let $s \in \mathcal{B}^\ast$ be a string, and $d \in \mathcal{D}$ be a conditional description given $s$. We define the \emph{conditional surfeit} of the conditional description $d_{t \mid s^\star}$, denoted by $\sigma(d_{t \mid s^\star})$, as: 
\[
\rho(d_{r \mid s}) = 1 - \frac{K \left( d_{t \mid s^\star} \right)}{l \left( d_{t \mid s^\star} \right)}
\]
\end{definition}

Conditional surfeit is a relative measure, and so, a number between $0$ and $1$.

\begin{proposition}
We have that $0 \leq \rho(d_{t \mid s^\star}) \leq 1$ for all $t,s$ and all $d_{t,s}$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Given that $K(d_{t \mid s^\star}) \leq l(d_{t \mid s^\star})$ we have that $\frac{K(d_{t \mid s^\star})}{l \left( d_{t \mid s^\star} \right)} \leq 1$ and so, $1 - \frac{K(d_{t \mid s^\star})}{l \left( d_{t \mid s^\star} \right)} \geq 0$. Also, since $\frac{K(d_{t \mid s^\star})}{l \left( d_{t \mid s^\star} \right)} > 0$ (both quantities are positive integers), we have that $1 - \frac{K(d_{t \mid s^\star})}{l \left( d_{t \mid s^\star} \right)} \leq 1$.
\end{proof}

Finally, we can extend our concepts of conditional surfeit and conditional redundancy to multiple, but fine, number of topics.

\begin{definition}
Let $t, s_1, s_2, \ldots, s_n \in \mathcal{T}$ be a finite collection of topics, and let $d_{t \mid s_1^\star, s_2^\star, \ldots,s_n^\star}$ any conditional description of $t$ given $s_1, s_2, \ldots, s_n$. We define the \emph{conditional surfeit} of the description $d_{t \mid s_1^\star, s_2^\star, \ldots,s_n^\star}$, denoted by $\sigma(d_{t \mid s_1^\star, s_2^\star, \ldots,s_n^\star})$, as: 
\[
\sigma(d_{t \mid s_1^\star, s_2^\star, \ldots,s_n^\star}) = 1 - \frac{K\left( t \mid s_1^\star, s_2^\star, \ldots,s_n^\star \right)}{l \left( d_{t \mid s_1^\star, s_2^\star, \ldots,s_n^\star} \right)}
\]
And the \emph{conditional redundancy} of the description $d_{t_1, t_2, \ldots, t_n}$, denoted by $\rho(d_{t_1, t_2, \ldots, t_n})$, as:
\[
\rho(d_{t_1, t_2, \ldots, t_n}) = 1 - \frac{K(d_{t_1, t_2, \ldots, t_n})}{l \left( d_{t_1, t_2, \ldots, t_n} \right)}
\]
\end{definition}

It is easy to show that the properties of conditional surfeit and conditional redundancy apply to the case of multiple topics as well.

\section{Decreasing Surfeit}

Our objective is to reduce the surfeit of our current best description $d_1$, thereby enhancing our understanding of the original entity. This improvement may involve either refining $d_1$ to eliminate redundant symbols or developing an entirely new description based on a different modeling approach. In either case, the result is a new description $d_2$. In this section, we analyze how the introduction of a new description $d_2$ affects the surfeit relative to that of the original description $d_1$.

\begin{definition}
Let $r \in \mathcal{B}^\ast$ be a representation, and let $d_1, d_2 \in \mathcal{D}$ be two descriptions. We define the \emph{variation of surfeit}\index{Variation of surfeit} between the descriptions $d_1$ and $d_2$, with respect to $r$, denoted by $\Delta^{a}_{\sigma} ( d_1, d_2, r )$, as:
\[
\Delta^{a}_{\sigma} ( d_1, d_2, r ) = \sigma(d_1, r) - \sigma(d_2, r)
\]
\end{definition}

Since surfeit is bounded between 0 and 1, the maximum possible variation is $\pm 1$. A positive value of $\Delta_{\sigma}$ indicates that $d_2$ is preferable to $d_1$ in terms of surfeit. Conversely, a negative value suggests that $d_1$ is more concise than $d_2$.

It is important to emphasize that a new description may also introduce a substantial increase in inaccuracy, potentially offsetting the improvement in surfeit. For a detailed discussion of inaccuracy, refer to Chapter \ref{chap:Error}, and for an explanation of how inaccuracy and surfeit combine into the unified metric of nescience, see Chapter \ref{chap:Nescience}.

We can also introduce a relative measure of the variation in surfeit when transitioning from description $d_1$ to $d_2$.

\begin{definition}
Let $r \in \mathcal{B}^\ast$ be a representation, and $d_1, d_2 \in \mathcal{D}$ be two descriptions. We define the \emph{relative variation of surfeit}\index{Relative variation of surfeit} between descriptions $d_1$ and $d_2$, denoted by $\Delta^{r}_{\sigma}(d_1, d_2, r)$, as:
\[
\Delta^{r}_{\sigma}(d_1, d_2, r) = \frac{\sigma(d_1, r) - \sigma(d_2, r)}{\sigma(d_1, r)}
\]
provided that $\sigma(d_1, r) \neq 0$.
\end{definition}

A value of $0$ indicates that there is no change in surfeit between the two descriptions. A value of $1$ corresponds to a complete elimination of surfeit, meaning that the new description $d_2$ has zero surfeit, while the original description $d_1$ had a nonzero surfeit. Negative values indicate that surfeit has increased, suggesting that the new description $d_2$ either includes more irrelevant or redundant symbols than $d_1$, or is too short to adequately represent the entity, thus omitting important information. It is important to note that the relative variation can become arbitrarily negative. This occurs when the surfeit of the original description $\sigma(d_1, r)$ is close to zero, while the surfeit of the new description $\sigma(d_2, r)$ is significantly larger. In such cases, the denominator becomes very small, causing the relative variation to diverge toward $-\infty$.

As surfeit approaches zero, relative variations become increasingly unstable. A small absolute change can result in a large relative variation when the initial surfeit is very low. For example, if $\sigma(d_1, r) = 0.1$ and surfeit decreases by 0.05, the relative improvement is 50\%. However, if $\sigma(d_1, r) = 0.9$ and the same absolute reduction occurs, the relative improvement is only about 5.6\%. Therefore, both absolute and relative variations are important for assessing the significance and scale of changes in surfeit.

\section{Surfeit-inaccuracy rate of Change}
\label{Surfeit_inaccuracy_rate_of_Change}

In the preceding section, we examined how the surfeit of a description can be reduced by modifying that description, either by adding missing symbols, or by removing redundant, or irrelevant symbols. In this section, we turn our attention to a more general approach for reducing the surfeit associated with an entity, by allowing a increase in the inaccuracy of the description. Rather than reducing the surfeit in isolation, it may be more effective to modify both, surfeit and inaccuracy, simultaneously.

The balance between the amount of inaccuracy we are willing to accept in order to achieve a reduction in surfeit is referred to as the \emph{surfeit-inaccuracy trade-off}. For a broader discussion of trade-offs in multi-objective optimization, refer to Section \ref{sec:trade_offs}.

\begin{definition}
Let $d_1, d_2 \in \mathcal{D}$ be two descriptions, and $r \in \mathcal{B}^\star$ a representation. We define the \emph{rate of change between the surfeit and inaccuracy} of the descriptions $d_1, d_2$ given the representation $r$, denoted by $\Delta_{\sigma \iota} (d_1, d_2, r)$ as:
\[
\Delta_{\sigma \iota} ( \mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2 ) = \frac{\sigma(d_2, r) - \iota(d_1, r)}{\iota(d_2, r) - \iota(d_1, r)}
\] 
provided that $\iota(d_2, r) - \iota(d_1, r) \neq 0$.
\end{definition}

The ratio $\Delta_{\sigma \iota}$ represents the rate of change between surfeit and inaccuracy when transitioning from the first description to the second. A positive value of $\Delta_{\sigma \iota}$ implies that both quantities, surfeit and inaccuracy, either decrease (which is desirable) or increase (which is undesirable). The interpretation becomes more nuanced when $\Delta_{\sigma \iota}$ is negative, indicating that one of the quantities decreases while the other increases. In such cases, two scenarios must be considered:

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
\item Surfeit decreases and inaccuracy increases, we aim for $\Delta_{\sigma \iota}(d_1, d_2, r) < M$, where $M < -1$, thereby ensuring that the reduction in surfeit compensates for the increase in inaccuracy.
\item Surfeit increases and inaccuracy decreases, we aim for $\Delta_{\sigma \iota}(d_1, d_2, r) > M$, where $-1 < M < 0$, thereby ensuring that the reduction in inaccuracy justifies the increase in surfeit.
\end{enumerate}

In both cases, caution is warranted when the change in inaccuracy is small, as it can disproportionately affect the ratio and potentially lead to misleading conclusions.

\begin{example}
Let $d_1, d_2 \in \mathcal{D}$ be two descriptions, and $r \in \mathcal{B}^\star$ a representation. For $d_1$, the surfeit $\sigma(d_1, r_1)$ is 0.40, and the inaccuracy $\iota(d_1, r)$ is 0.15. For $d_2$, the surfeit $\sigma(d_2, r)$ is 0.20 (a decrease from 0.40), and the inaccuracy $\iota(d_2, r)$ is 0.25 (an increase from 0.15). Using the definition of the rate of change, we compute:
\[
\Delta_{\sigma \iota} (d_1, d_2, r) = \frac{0.40 - 0.20}{0.15 - 0.25} = \frac{0.20}{-0.10} = -2
\]
In this case, transitioning from description $d_1$ to $d_2$ results in a decrease of surfeit by 0.20 units and an increase in inaccuracy by 0.10 units. The rate of change is $-2$.
\end{example}

Having a very small change in inaccuracy can significantly amplify the value of the rate of change, potentially giving the misleading impression that surfeit and inaccuracy are varying at an extreme rate, even when the actual changes are minor. This phenomenon is illustrated in the following example.

\begin{example}
Let $d_1 \in \mathcal{D}$ be a description and $r \in \mathcal{B}^\star$ a representation with an surfeit $\sigma(d_1, r)$ of 0.35 and a inaccuracy $\iota(d_1, r)$ of 0.20. Let $d_1 \in \mathcal{D}$ be a second description with an surfeit $\sigma(d_2, r)$ of 0.30 (a slight decrease from 0.35) and a inaccuracy $\iota(d_2, r)$ of 0.2001 (a very small increase from 0.20). Applying the definition of the rate of change:
\[
\Delta_{\sigma \iota} (d_1, d_2, r) = \frac{0.35 - 0.30}{0.20 - 0.2001} = \frac{0.05}{-0.0001} = -500
\]
The rate of change is $-500$, which may misleadingly suggest a dramatic shift. In reality, the surfeit only decreased by 0.05 units, and the inaccuracy increased by a negligible 0.0001 units. The extremely small denominator inflates the result, making the change appear far more significant than it actually is.
\end{example}

As we attempt to optimize both surfeit and inaccuracy, we inevitably encounter descriptions where improving one objective necessitates compromising the other. The set of such "best trade-off" configurations constitutes the Pareto frontier (see Section \ref{sec:multiobjective_optimization}). Points on the Pareto frontier are said to be Pareto optimal because any attempt to improve one objective leads to a deterioration in the other.

\begin{definition}
Let $d_1 \in \mathcal{D}$ be a description and $r \in \mathcal{B}^\star$ a representation. The description $d_1$ is said to be a Pareto point\index{Pareto point} with respect to surfeit $\sigma$ and inaccuracy $\iota$ if there does not exist another description $d_2$ such that:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item $\sigma(d_2, r) \leq \sigma(d_1, r)$ and $\iota(d_2, r) \leq \iota(d_1, r)$, and
    \item $\sigma(d_2, r) < \sigma(d_1, r)$ or $\iota(d_2, r) < \iota(d_2, r)$.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

In simpler terms, a description is Pareto optimal if: (i) no other description is better in both surfeit and inaccuracy, and (ii) any improvement in one metric necessarily results in a worsening of the other.

The rate of change $\Delta_{\sigma \iota}$ between any two Pareto optimal points provides insight into how the trade-off between surfeit and inaccurac evolves along the Pareto frontier. If decision-makers are more sensitive to changes in surfeit than to inaccuracy, they may prefer configurations with a less negative $\Delta_{\sigma \iota}$. Conversely, if inaccuracy is of greater concern, they may accept solutions where $\Delta_{\sigma \iota}$ indicates a larger increase in surfeit in exchange for a smaller gain in inaccuracy.

\section{Surfeit of Areas}
\label{sec:surfeit_areas}

The concept of surfeit can be extended to research areas, to quantitative measure the amount of extra effort we are using to describe the topics of the area.

\begin{definition}
Let $A \subset \mathcal{T}$ be an area with known subset $\hat{A} = \{t_1, t_2, \ldots, t_n\}$, and let $d_{\hat{A}}$ be a description. We define the \emph{surfeit of the description} $d_{\hat{A}}$ as:
\[
\sigma \left( d_{\hat{A}} \right) = 1  - \frac{K( \langle t_1, t_2, \ldots, t_n \rangle )}{l \left( d_{\hat{A}} \right)}
\]
\end{definition}

As it was the case of the concept of redundancy, in general we do not know the complexity of the area $K(\hat{A})$, and so, in practice, it must be approximated by the complexity of the descriptions themselves $K(\hat{d}_{\hat{A}})$. However, in the particular case of areas, we could have also problems with the quantity $\hat{d}_{\hat{A}}$, since it requires to study the conditional descriptions of the topics included in the area.

\begin{definition}
Let $A \subset \mathcal{T}$ be an area with known subset $\hat{A} = \{t_1, t_2, \ldots, t_n\}$, and let $d_{\hat{A}}$ be a description. We define the \emph{weak redundancy of the description} $d_{\hat{A}}$ as:
\[
\rho(d_{\hat{A}}) =  1  - \frac{K \left( d_{\hat{A}} \right)}{l \left( d_{\hat{A}} \right)}
\]
\end{definition}
